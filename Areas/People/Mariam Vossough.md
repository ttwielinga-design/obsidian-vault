## Claude Skills Documentation

## **Two-Stage Pre-Publication QA System (For When You Think You're Done But Probably Aren't)**

**The Problem I'm Solving:**

I publish a weekly Substack teaching professional women how to use AI without losing their voice, feeling like they're cheating ([The Women’s AI Voice](https://mariamvossough.substack.com/?utm_campaign=profile_chips)). The creative work happens before these skills touch anything \- I ideate through voice notes, collaborate with AI using my "Pocket Personalities" and do extensive human editing.

By the time I think I'm ready to publish, I've been staring at the same draft for hours (sometimes days). That's when I'm blind to the obvious: structural gaps, voice drift, mobile formatting disasters or weak headlines that I've become attached to because I wrote them three drafts ago and they're now part of the family.

I needed a systematic final check before hitting publish \- one that wouldn't just catch typos but would catch the strategic mistakes I can't see anymore.

**What the Skills Do:**

I built two Claude skills that work as a sequential QA system:

**Skill 1: Creative QA Check** \- Strategic quality assessment

This one searches my project knowledge for current brand positioning, then audits the draft against specific categories:

*Voice & Brand Authenticity:* Does this sound like me? Are there any British colloquialisms that might confuse people?

*Structure & Content Flow:* Does the opening hook grab attention? Do transitions feel natural? Is there an emotional arc? Does it deliver on the title promise?

*Scanability:* Can readers skim and get main points? Do headers create clear visual hierarchy? Where are pull quote opportunities?

*Strategic Elements:* Primary and secondary keywords identified? Headline optimised? Subheadline clear with keywords? Are section headers compelling? Internal links driving traffic to relevant previous posts? 

*Substack-Specific:* First 140 characters compelling for email preview? Pull quotes identified (1-2 sentences worth highlighting)? 

*Accessibility & Clarity:* Reading level appropriate? Any unexplained jargon? Sentence length varied (especially important for ADHD readers)?

It returns prioritised recommendations: Critical → Important → Nice to have. Not "here's how to write this better" but "here's what you missed that will hurt performance."

**Skill 2: Publication Proofreader** \- Final technical polish

After I fix the strategic issues, this one does the unglamorous work:

*Language & Grammar:* Typos, grammar errors, proper capitalisation in headers, Oxford comma removal (if any have snuck in), consistent punctuation style.

*Formatting Consistency:* All curly quotes (not straight quotes), bullet vs numbered list consistency, bold/italic usage (not overused or underused), 

*Mobile Optimisation:* 1-3-1 and 1-2-1 paragraph formatting applied, no paragraphs longer than 3-4 lines on mobile view, lists breaking up dense text.

*Visual Elements:* Alt text for all images (descriptive, accessible, includes context), image placement supports content flow, caption recommendations.

*Links & Technical:* All links functional, link text descriptive (not just "here" or "click here"), internal links strategically placed, placeholder text removed.

*Basic SEO Check:* Title optimised, subtitle works as meta description (compelling \+ clear \+ keywords), first 140 characters compelling (email preview text).

*Headline/Subtitle Alternatives:* 3-5 alternative headline options with different angles/keywords, 2-3 alternative subtitle options.

*Final Polish:* Subscribe button placement confirmed, word count and estimated reading time, final scanability check, any remaining formatting issues.

It gives me a copy-paste-ready checklist so I can fix everything in 10 minutes instead of 45 minutes of second-guessing myself.

**My Workflow Context:**

* Finish what I think is the final draft  
* Run Creative QA Check skill → discover my opening is weak, I've used "moreover" four times and my pull quotes are buried in paragraph seven  
* Fix strategic issues, run Publication Proofreader skill → get two typos I missed, five headline alternatives and formatting fixes with before/after examples  
* Publish with confidence

**Why This Works:**

The separation between strategic and technical is crucial. If I tried to check everything at once, I'd either miss the big stuff or waste time on typos before fixing structural problems.

Running them sequentially means I fix the strategy first (does this actually work?), then polish the execution (is this clean and correct?). The creative-qa-check skill has caught weak hooks, poor section headings and structural gaps that could've harmed engagement. The proofreader has caught mobile formatting disasters and given me better headlines than my attachment-bias brain could see.

**What Didn't Work:**

Early versions tried to do both at once \- total mess. I'd get "fix this typo" next to "restructure your entire opening" and couldn't prioritise. Splitting them into two distinct skills with different purposes was the breakthrough.

Also, I opted for ego-free feedback. I need the Creative QA to be blunt ("This opening won't hook anyone") not diplomatic. If I wanted encouragement, I'd ask my mum.

**Why Someone Else Would Use This:**

Anyone publishing long-form content who's ever hit publish, then immediately spotted the typo, the weak headline or the paragraph that doesn't actually make sense. The "I've been staring at this too long to see it clearly anymore" problem.

It's especially useful if you have specific brand voice requirements or formatting standards (like Substack's mobile-first reading experience). The skills enforce consistency when your brain is too tired to remember all the rules.
