Fisher, J. G., Peffer, S. A., & Sprinkle, G. B. (2003). Budget-Based Contracts, Budget Levels, and Group Performance. Journal of Management Accounting Research, 15(1), 87-94.

The research aims to investigate the impact of different group incentive contracts and varying budget levels on group performance. Three key hypotheses were proposed to frame the study:

H1: The group budget-linear contract will result in the highest level of group performance, followed by the group budget-fixed contract and the group piece-rate contract.
Logical Reasoning: This hypothesis posits that the group budget-linear contract, designed to pay zero for performance below the budget, a fixed bonus upon achieving the budget, and additional compensation for production above the budget, will lead to the highest group performance. This contract incentivizes optimal effort with a balance between fixed and variable compensation. The group budget-fixed contract, offering no compensation for performance above the budget, is expected to have a lower impact on motivation compared to the budget-linear contract. The group piece-rate contract, encouraging free-riding behavior due to its payment structure, is expected to yield the lowest performance among the three contracts.

Evaluation: The findings support this hypothesis, indicating that the group budget-linear contract indeed led to significantly higher group performance compared to both the group budget-fixed and group piece-rate contracts. This aligns with the hypothesis, confirming that the payment structure of the budget-linear contract effectively motivated higher group productivity.

H2: Group performance will increase with increases in the budget level.
Logical Reasoning: This hypothesis suggests that as the budget level becomes more challenging but still achievable, group performance will rise. The reasoning behind this hypothesis aligns with prior research on goal-setting theory, indicating that moderately difficult goals tend to boost performance as they positively influence motivation. It anticipates that moderate difficulty levels in budget targets would enhance effort and coordination among group members.

Evaluation: The study's findings support this hypothesis to a certain extent. The 75% budget level led to significantly higher group performance compared to both lower (50%) and higher (100%) budget levels. This aligns with the hypothesis, indicating that a moderately challenging budget level fosters better group performance.

The implications of the study highlight the significance of effective incentive structures and appropriate budget level settings in driving group productivity. Budget-based contracts, with their varied reward structures, seem to encourage cooperation among group members, unlike piece-rate contracts that may lead to free-riding behavior.

Limitations such as the absence of interaction between group members and the need for further research exploring factors like group size, communication, and alternative mechanisms influencing group performance in diverse settings.


Summary of Michael C. Jensen's Paper on Compensation Systems:

Overview:
Title: Professional Forum: Remuneration: Where we’ve been, how we got to here, what are the problems, and how to fix them
Author: Michael C. Jensen
Publication Year: 2003
Focus: Critique of traditional compensation systems and proposals for reform.

Key Points:
Issues with Traditional Compensation Systems:
Short-term Orientation: Emphasizes short-term gains over long-term performance.
Gaming Behaviors: Managers and employees manipulate metrics to meet targets.
Impact on Integrity: Erodes organizational integrity and ethical conduct.
The Problems of Using Targets and Bonuses:
Shortcomings: Targets based on current performance lead to future problems.
Gaming and Unethical Conduct: Encourages short-term thinking and unethical behaviors.
Advocacy for Linear Pay-for-Performance Systems:
Elimination of Targets: Suggests removing targets and budgets from compensation systems.
Long-term Projections: Encourages setting bonus lines based on longer-term growth and profitability.
Challenges in Transitioning to Linear Systems:
Resistance to Change: Managers resist shifting from ingrained target-based systems.
Psychological Factors: Fear of turnover, reluctance to abandon traditional practices.
Leadership and Implementation:
CEO's Role: Emphasizes the CEO's crucial role in leading the transition and communication.
Education and Communication: Highlights the need for extensive education on the new system.

Shift to Linear Pay-for-Performance Systems:
Leadership and communication

Impact of traditional systems

Calls for a fundamental shift away from traditional compensation systems   linear pay-for-performance models to combat gaming behaviors and promote long-term organizational success.
Emphasizing the need for strong leadership, extensive education, and a gradual cultural shift within organizations to successfully implement these changes.
