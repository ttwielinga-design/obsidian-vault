Abstract
This study develops a model of vertical information sharing between superior and subordinate in the budgeting proc-
ess. Upward information sharing involves the revelation of private information by subordinate to superior. Its pro-
posed antecedents are budget participation and organizational commitment while job performance is a proposed
outcome. Downward information exchanges from superior to subordinate are also examined by including role ambi-
guity in the model. Survey results suggest that vertical information sharing is an important intervening variable in
understanding the performance eﬀects of budget participation and organizational commitment.

One of the most important beneﬁts of the budg-
eting process is the sharing of information between
organizational members (e.g., Hopwood, 1976).
Information exchanges between subordinate and
superior during budget discussions are especially
important with many possible beneﬁts both for
the individual and organization (Shields & Shields,
1998). Vertical information sharing involves both
upward communication of information from sub-
ordinate to superior and downward communica-
tion from superior to subordinate. Regarding
upward communication, a common assumption
in the accounting literature, especially in agency
studies, is that subordinates often know more
about their operational areas than do their superi-
ors, i.e., subordinates have so-called private infor-
mation (e.g., Chow, Cooper, & Waller, 1988;
Christensen, 1982; Merchant, 1981; Nouri & Par-
ker, 1998; Waller, 1988; Young, 1985). Budget
participation may enable subordinates to commu-
nicate their private information to their superiors
resulting in better budgets and decision-making
(e.g., Magner, Welker, & Campbell, 1996; Nouri
& Parker, 1998; Shields & Shields, 1998). While
many studies assume that participation increases
the revelation of information, they do not directly
assess it. Miller and Monge (1986) point this out in
their
meta-analysis
of
participatory
decision-
making in the management, applied psychology,
and organizational behavior literatures. Cognitive

While budget participation may facilitate infor-
mation sharing, another important consideration
is the motivation of subordinates to reveal infor-
mation (Hopwood, 1976). Motivation to share
information may depend, in part, upon a previ-
ously overlooked factor, the organizational com-
mitment of the subordinate. Those who are
highly committed to the organization want the
organization to succeed; consequently, they are
more likely to reveal their private information
which can be used to enhance the performance of
both the subordinate and the organization.
In the proposed theoretical framework, budget
participation and organization commitment in-
crease upward communication of information
which, in turn, increases the job performance of
the subordinate. Information sharing may increase
individual performance in a number of ways
including: (1) enabling the superior to help develop
better strategies for the subordinate (Murray,
1990); (2) ensuring that the subordinate receives
adequate budgetary support (Nouri & Parker,
1998).

upward communication, and to create a more
comprehensive
model
of
vertical
information
sharing, downward communication is also exam-

budgeting process, subordinates gain additional
information from superiors and others that in-

1996). As Chenhall and Brownell (1988) argue,
discussions with superiors during the budgeting
process help clarify the goals and methods of
the subordinate. They report evidence that budget
participation reduces the role ambiguity of the
subordinate, which, in turn, leads to higher job
performance. Prior studies also report evidence
that role ambiguity aﬀects organizational commit-
ment (e.g., Welsch & LaVan, 1981); consequently,
role ambiguity may be an intervening variable in
the relation between budget participation and
organizational commitment. Prior research sug-

organizational
commitment, job performance) theorized by the
current study to be connected to upward informa-
tion sharing. To properly specify the relations be-
tween these variables, role ambiguity is included
in the theoretical model.
To assess the theoretical framework, a question-
naire was administered to 70 managers and super-

support the hypothesized relations. Regarding
upward
communication
during
the

with individual job performance. Regarding down-

budget participation inﬂuences role ambiguity

commitment.
Several indirect eﬀects involving upward infor-
mation sharing also are statistically signiﬁcant.
Both
budget
participation
and
organizational
commitment have an indirect eﬀect on job per-
formance through information sharing. Also, role
ambiguity has an indirect eﬀect on information
sharing through organizational commitment.

Theoretical development and hypotheses
To recap, vertical information sharing during
the budget process involves the communication
of information between superior and subordinate.
Prior studies in the accounting literature have ex-
plored two dimensions of this communication:
(1) ﬂow of information from the subordinate to
the superior (upward information sharing); (2)
ﬂow of information from the superior to the sub-
ordinate (downward information sharing). Each
dimension is discussed separately. The proposed
theoretical model with the related hypotheses is
presented in Fig. 1.
Upward information sharing
Many accounting studies in this area are based
upon an agency framework that assumes two pri-
mary actors, the principal and the agent. The
principal hires the agent to perform a task on
the behalf of the principal. In an organizational
context, the principal is often portrayed as an
executive who delegates responsibility for certain
tasks to a subordinate who functions as an agent.
As Baiman (1990) notes, agency models assume
that the agent has ‘‘private’’ information about
the agents area of responsibility. The informa-
tion is private in the sense that the principal
can not acquire the information or the principal
can acquire it only by incurring signiﬁcant mon-
itoring costs. The diﬀerence between agent and
principal in information levels is termed informa-
tion asymmetry. Using an agency framework,
several papers propose that budget participation
allows subordinates to reveal their private infor-
mation, which, in turn, leads to better perform-
ance by the individual and economic gains for
the organization (e.g., Baiman & Evans, 1983;
Christensen, 1982; Penno, 1984; Nouri & Parker,
1998).
According to Shields and Young (1993), one of
the primary reasons that companies use participa-
tive budgeting is to facilitate the communication of
private information from subordinates to superi-
ors (also see Shields & Shields, 1998). Shields

According to Simons (1995), the budgeting
(proﬁt planning) system can be used to gather pri-
vate information from subordinates about the
strategic uncertainties confronting the organiza-
tion. Strategic uncertainties are ‘‘the uncertainties
and contingencies that could threaten or invali-

1995, p. 94).’’ They include things such as com-
petitor actions, changes in consumer preferences,
and technological changes. Middle and lower level
managers represent an important source of infor-
mation about uncertainties and methods for deal-
ing with them. Disclosure of such information
may lead to revisions of organizational strategy
in a process that Simons (1995) calls the ‘‘emer-
gent’’ approach to strategy formation because
strategy emerges from lower levels of the organi-
zation. Top management can tap critical informa-
tion
held
by
lower
management
through
interactive control systems that promote active
and open dialogue between organizational levels.
As Simons (1995) notes, budgeting systems may
be used on an interactive basis to encourage dis-
cussions between superiors and subordinates that
result
in
information
sharing.
While
Simons
(1995) focuses on organizational systems in his
writings, his ideas also may be applicable at the
individual level. Within an organization, those
subordinates who have interactive dialogue with
their superiors in the budgeting process may
reveal
critical
information
about
strategic
uncertainties.
Regarding the nature of information sharing
by the subordinate during the budgeting process,
empirical evidence is sparse in the accounting lit-
erature. One of the few empirical studies in this
area is Nouri and Parker (1998) which argues that
subordinates
often
have
private
information
about the budgetary support required to perform
their tasks. Through budgetary participation, the
subordinate may ‘‘incorporate this information
into the budget to ensure that they have adequate
resources to successfully perform their duties’’ (p.
469). In their sample, budget participation is
linked to budgetary support. (Magner et al.,
1996) propose that budget participation allows
subordinates the opportunity to reveal private
information which leads to higher quality budg-
ets, i.e., budgets that more accurately represent
future conditions in the subordinates work envi-
ronment. In the sample of Magner et al. (1996),
budget participation is highly correlated with per-
ceived budget quality.
While many accounting studies assume that
budget participation leads to upward information
sharing (e.g., Brownell & Dunk, 1991; Magner
et al., 1996; Mia, 1989; Nouri & Parker, 1998),
none, to the knowledge of the authors, directly
examine the relation between participation and
information sharing. A similar situation exists in
the management and applied psychology litera-
tures
involving
participatory
decision-making
(PDM) Cognitive studies in this area argue that
PDM ‘‘enhances the ﬂow and use of important
information in organizations’’ (Miller & Monge,
1986, p. 770). These studies assume that subordi-
nates have more information about their work
than do their superiors and, that through PDM,
subordinates reveal such information. As Miller
and Monge (1986) note in their literature review,
such studies assume information sharing but do
not directly assess it.
The current study proposes and tests a direct
relation between budget participation and up-
ward information sharing. As deﬁned by Brown-
ell (1982b), budget participation is the process in
which individuals ‘‘are involved in, and have
inﬂuence on,’’ the setting of budgets that di-
rectly aﬀect the individual (p. 124). High partic-
ipation
includes
frequent
and
wide
ranging
discussions between superiors and subordinates
about budgeting issues (Milani, 1975). The fre-
quency and scope of such discussions provide
subordinates with many opportunities to share
their
insights
(i.e.,
their
private
information)
with superiors; consequently, the likelihood in-
creases that the subordinate will communicate
private information. This suggests the following
hypothesis:

H1: Budget participation and upward information
sharing have a positive relation.
While involvement in the budget process may
facilitate the communication of private informa-
tion, such involvement does not ensure it. A subor-
dinate involved in the budgeting process may
withhold, or even distort, private information if
this personally beneﬁts the subordinate. As Hop-
wood (1976) notes, ‘‘For although [budget] partic-
ipation may result in an exchange of information,
its accuracy is known to be dependent upon the
motives of lower management (p. 86).’’ Organiza-
tional commitment may motivate managers to
fully reveal their information; consequently, com-
mitment is included in the proposed theoretical
model.
Meyer and Allen (1997) describe organizational
commitment ‘‘as a psychological state linking the
employee to their organizations’’ (p. 23). As noted
by Mathieu and Zajac (1990), the most common
conceptualization of organizational commitment
is aﬀective (attitudinal) commitment which is used
in the current study. Aﬀective commitment is char-
acterized by: (1) strong belief in the goals and val-
ues of the organization; (2) willingness to exert
considerable eﬀort to help the organization to
reach its goals; and (3) strong desire to maintain
organizational membership (Angle & Perry, 1981;
Mowday, Porter, & Steers, 1982; Porter, Steers,
Mowday, & Boulian, 1974).
For individuals who are committed to their
organization, the desire to achieve organizational
goals extends beyond personal interests in acquir-
ing tangible organizational rewards such as in-
come
or
promotion
(Lincoln
&
Kalleberg,
1990). Committed individuals exhibit a ‘‘willing-
ness to aid the organization even if direct reward
is not contingent upon that aid’’ (Moorman,
Niehoﬀ, & Organ, 1993, p. 211). Committed
employees identify with organizational success.
Based upon analytic models, Alles and Datar
(2002) argue that identiﬁcation with organiza-
tional success may encourage the employee to
share information even at the expense of sacriﬁc-
ing slack. According to the proposed theoretical
framework in the current study, individuals with
high commitment are more likely to reveal pri-
vate information during the budgeting process.
Such information could beneﬁt the organization
and, for committed individuals, organizational
success is important. This suggests the following
hypothesis:
H2:
Organizational
commitment
and
upward
information sharing have a positive relation.
Besides examining
potential
antecedents of
information sharing, this study also investigates a
possible outcome, individual job performance.
Accounting researchers have theorized that infor-
mation sharing inﬂuences job performance in a
number of ways. Magner et al. (1996) propose that
the revelation of private information by subordi-
nates results in more realistic plans and more accu-
rate budgets that, in turn, may result in higher
performance. Murray (1990) argues that the dis-
closure of private information may enable the
superior to help develop better strategies for the
subordinate. Sharing information also may ensure
that the subordinate receives adequate budgetary
support (Nouri & Parker, 1998). Based upon
these
arguments,
the
following
hypothesis
is
proposed:
H3: Upward information sharing has a positive
relation with job performance.
To recap, the current study proposes links be-
tween information sharing and several variables:
budget participation, organizational commitment,
and job performance. To properly specify this
model of information sharing, potential links be-

Downward information sharing
Hopwood (1976) is among the ﬁrst accounting
researchers to propose that the budget process
can function as a communication mechanism
whereby subordinates obtain more information
about their jobs. Chenhall and Brownell (1988)
are among the ﬁrst researchers to empirically
examine this possibility. They argue that, through
budget participation, subordinates gain informa-
tion that helps clarify their organizational roles
including their duties, responsibilities, and ex-
pected performance. Empirical evidence in Chen-
hall and Brownell (1988) suggests that budget
participation is linked to role ambiguity. Also, in
the psychology and organizational behavior litera-
tures, several studies report evidence of a relation
between participatory decision-making and role
ambiguity (see meta-analysis by Jackson & Schu-
ler, 1985). Prior research suggests the following
hypothesis:
H6: Budget participation and role ambiguity have
a negative relation.
Regarding the consequences of role ambiguity,
two possible outcomes are relevant to the pro-
posed theoretical model: lower organizational
commitment and lower job performance (see Fig.
1). Regarding organizational commitment, several
studies propose that role ambiguity lowers com-
mitment (e.g., DeCotiis & Summers, 1987; Morris
& Koch, 1979; Welsch & LaVan, 1981). As Welsch
and LaVan (1981) argue, if a job is ‘‘unclear as to
its expectations, . . . organizational commitment
will necessarily suﬀer since the employee is not
given the opportunity to integrate himself or her-
self into the position or organization’’ (p. 1081–
1082). In their meta-analysis of organizational
commitment, Mathieu and Zajac (1990) conclude
that prior studies demonstrate a signiﬁcant nega-
tive
association
between
role
ambiguity
and
organizational commitment. This suggests the
following:
H7: Role ambiguity and organizational commit-
ment have a negative relation.
Role ambiguity may also lead to lower job per-
formance. High role ambiguity indicates that an
employee is uncertain about his/her role within
the organization. This includes uncertainty about
organizational expectations and the means to ful-
ﬁll them. Based upon expectancy theory, Beehr,
Walsh, and Taber (1976) argue that role ambiguity
decreases performance by reducing the expectation
that strong eﬀort leads to performance. Also,
according to Chenhall and Brownell (1988), indi-
viduals who do not fully understand their duties
and responsibilities, i.e., those with high role ambi-
guity, ‘‘will hesitate to make decisions and will rely
on learning by trial and error’’ (p. 226). In Chen-
hall and Brownell (1988) and several prior studies
cited by them, empirical evidence suggests that role
ambiguity has a signiﬁcant negative association

Methodology
Data were collected using a survey question-
naire. The ﬁrst part of the methodology section de-
scribes the distribution of the questionnaire and
the respondents. The second part describes the sur-
vey measures while the third part explains the con-
trols for response biases. Lastly, the method for
assessing the relations between variables, path
analysis, is brieﬂy discussed.
Questionnaire distribution and respondents
To secure companies for survey distribution,
the business school of one of the authors was con-
tacted. Administrators identiﬁed ﬁrms with strong
aﬃliations with the business school which might be
willing to participate in the survey. From the list
supplied by the business school, banks and insur-
ance companies were excluded resulting in a ﬁnal
list of 19 ﬁrms. Executives at these companies were
initially contacted by phone followed by a mailing
of a packet that further explained the study and in-
cluded a sample questionnaire. Of the 19 contacted
companies, 13 agreed to participate. The 13 com-
panies included one trucking ﬁrm and 12 manufac-
turing
ﬁrms
that
produced
ﬂooring,
food,
computers, and industrial products. Regarding
the number of employees, each company had at
least 100 employees while the median was 1174
employees. For the six companies which did not
want to participate, the rational provided by exec-
utives fell into two categories: the questionnaire
was too intrusive; or company personnel were
too busy. For the participating ﬁrms, company
executives were asked to distribute the question-
naires to managers and supervisors who had bud-
get
responsibilities
within
their
respective
organizations. Executives identiﬁed 114 such indi-
viduals. Of these, 74 individuals responded. Four
failed to complete the questionnaire and therefore
were not included in the statistical analysis; conse-
quently, the eﬀective response rate was 61% (70/
114). In the introductory letter to participants,
anonymity was stressed. Respondents mailed com-
pleted questionnaires directly to the researchers
using postage-paid envelopes provided to them.
Of the respondents who completed the survey,
the mean age was 43 years while the mean length
of employment with their company was 11 years.
The respondents represented many functional
areas including accounting, ﬁnance, human re-
sources, marketing, operations, and production.
The average number of employees in the organiza-
tional hierarchy who were under the respondent
was 53. Of the 70 respondents, 17 classiﬁed them-
selves as lower level managers or ﬁrst level supervi-
sors. The remaining 53 respondents classiﬁed
themselves as mid-level or top level managers.
(There were no statistically signiﬁcant diﬀerences
between the two groups for any of the variables
used in the study.)
Measures
The variables measured in the questionnaire in-
clude budget participation, information sharing,
organizational commitment, role ambiguity, and
job performance. The questions used to measure
the variables appear in Appendix A.
To measure budget participation, the six item
scale of Milani (1975) was used. The instrument
attempts to assess the degree of involvement and
inﬂuence that an individual has in the budgeting
process. A sample question in the scale is: ‘‘The
portion of the budget I am involved in setting.’’
The response scale is a seven point Likert-type
scale ranging from one (very little) to seven (very
much). Prior accounting researchers (e.g., Brown-
ell, 1982a; Mia, 1988) report satisfactory reliabil-
ity and validity for the scale. In the current
study, factor analysis of the scale revealed only
one component with an eigenvalue greater than
1, which explained 58.3% of the total variation.
The Cronbach alpha in the current study is .85.
The score for the variable was the aggregate of
the responses to the six questions. This same pro-
cedure for calculating scores was used for all
variables.

Information sharing by the subordinate was
measured using a two item scale developed for this
study. The instrument attempts to determine the
degree to which subordinates communicate infor-
mation about local conditions to their superiors.
One item in the scale is: ‘‘Through the budgeting
process, I share my insights with my superior
about the situation in my area of responsibility.’’
The other item in the scale is: ‘‘In the budgeting
process, I communicate information to my superi-
ors about opportunities and problems facing the
organization.’’ The response scale is a seven-point
Likert-type scale ranging from one (strongly disa-
gree) to seven (strongly agree). The Cronbach al-
pha is .75. Factor analysis revealed that the two
questions loaded on one component which ex-
plained 79.7% of total variation. 1
Organizational
commitment
was
measured
using the nine-item scale from Mowday, Steers,
and Porter (1979). Sample items in the scale in-
clude: ‘‘I really care about the fate of this organi-
zation’’ and ‘‘I found that my values and the
organizations values are very similar.’’ The re-
sponse scale is a seven point Likert-type response
scale ranging from one (strongly disagree) to seven
(strongly agree). The scale of Mowday et al. (1979)
is similar to the scale developed by Allen and
Meyer (1990) to measure aﬀective organizational
commitment. As argued in Dunham, Grube, and
Castaneda (1994), the two scales ‘‘provide basi-
cally the same information’’ (p. 377). Satisfactory
reliability and validity for the scale have been re-
ported by prior researchers (e.g., Blau, 1987; Price
& Muller, 1981). In the current study, the Cron-
bach alpha is .90. The organizational commitment
scale loaded on two factors, which explained
70.9% of the total variation. Dunham et al.
(1994) ran conﬁrmatory factor analysis using sev-
eral samples and found this scale to load on two
or three factors.
To measure role ambiguity, this study used a six
item scale developed by Rizzo, House, and Lirtz-
man (1970). This scale has been used extensively
in prior research including Chenhall and Brownell
(1988). In their meta-analyses of role ambiguity,
both Fisher and Gitelson (1983) and Jackson and
Schuler (1985) conclude that the scale is a satisfac-
tory measure of role ambiguity. Sample items from
the scale (reverse wording) include: ‘‘Explanation
to me is clear of what has to be done’’ and ‘‘I know
exactly what is expected of me.’’ The response for-
mat is a seven point Likert-type scale ranging from
one (very false) to seven (very true). In the current
study, the Cronbach alpha is .82. The six items
loaded on one factor which explained 52.7% of
the total variation.
To measure job performance, this study used
the self-assessment scale of Mahoney, Jerde, and
Carroll (1963, 1965). This performance measure
has appeared in several prior accounting studies
(e.g., Brownell, 1982a; Brownell & Hirst, 1986;
Brownell & McInnes, 1986; Kren, 1992). The
nine-item instrument includes one measure of
overall job performance and eight measures of
eight diﬀerent performance dimensions (one meas-
ure
per
dimension).
Regarding
performance
dimensions, respondents were asked to rate them-
selves in a number of areas such as: ‘‘directing,
leading, and developing your subordinates’’ and
‘‘determining goals, policies, and courses of ac-
tion.’’ The response scale for each of the nine items
ranges from one (well below average) to seven
(well above average). The total score across the
nine items is used in the statistical analysis.

performance ratings of individuals supplied by
their superiors. He reports that the Mahoney
scale ‘‘possessed less leniency, restriction of range,

Assessing potential response biases
The current study attempts to assess two poten-
tial response biases: (1) order response bias; (2) so-
cial desirability responding (SDR). Regarding
order response bias, as Alreck and Settle (1985)
note, the order of questions in a questionnaire
may aﬀect responses. To assess this bias, two ver-
sions of the survey were distributed that diﬀered
only by question order. To determine if responses
diﬀered by version, one way analysis of variance
was performed on each variable. Results indicate
no signiﬁcant diﬀerences between versions for
any variable.
Regarding
social
desirability
responding
(SDR), this is the proclivity of individuals ‘‘to pre-
sent themselves favorably with respect to current
social norms and standards’’ (Zerbe & Paulhus,
1987, p. 250). As noted by several researchers,
SDR represents a potential response bias in survey
research if respondents answer questions so that
their responses appear socially acceptable (Arnold,
Feldman, & Purbhoo, 1985; Ganster, Hennessey,
& Luthans, 1983; Smith, 1967). If SDR occurs, it
may produce spurious relations between variables
in a theoretical model (e.g., Ganster et al., 1983).
To assess SDR, the individual propensity for the
bias was measured using a scale adopted from
Crowne and Marlowe (1964) (see Appendix A).
Correlations between the SDR scale and variables
in the theoretical model were calculated as recom-
mended by Smith (1967). The SDR measure had
insigniﬁcant correlations with budget participation
and job performance. In contrast, the SDR scale
had signiﬁcant correlations (p < .05) with organi-
zational commitment, role ambiguity, and infor-
mation sharing.
To examine the inﬂuence of SDR, the measures
for organizational commitment, role ambiguity
and information sharing were adjusted for SDR
bias using the approach of Anderson, Warner,
and Spencer (1984, p. 576). 2 Statistical analysis
of the sample data using the adjusted measures
yielded the same ﬁndings as using the original
measures. This suggests that SDR bias did not
inﬂuence
the
proposed
relations
between
the
variables in the theoretical model. Results for the
original measures are reported in the current
study.

the subordinate and the organization (e.g., Mag-

further argue that budget participation may enable
subordinates to communicate such information
upward to their superiors (e.g., Baiman & Evans,
1983; Christensen, 1982; Magner et al., 1996;
Nouri & Parker, 1998; Penno, 1984; Shields &
Shields, 1998; Simons, 1995). While these research-
ers propose a relation between budget participa-
tion
and
information
sharing,
they
do
not
directly test the proposition. The current study
empirically examines the relation and ﬁnds evi-
dence suggesting a direct link.
Measuring
information
sharing
during
the

test of the relation between budget participation
and information sharing, but also the examination

zational commitment is an important antecedent
of information sharing. Those subordinates who
are highly committed are more likely to reveal

Regarding the relation between budget partici-

of the current study suggest that an indirect link
exists. A few studies (e.g., Nouri & Parker, 1998;
Quirin et al., 2000) report evidence that budget

organizational

ment; however, results in this study suggest that
the relation between budget participation and
organizational commitment is fully mediated by
a previously unexamined intervening variable, role
ambiguity.
Results also suggest links between organiza-
tional commitment, information sharing, and job
performance. As theorized, the direct path be-
tween information sharing and individual per-
formance is statistically signiﬁcant. Concerning
the relation between organizational commitment
and job performance, numerous studies in applied
psychology, organizational behavior, and manage-
ment propose that organizational commitment
leads to higher individual performance (see reviews
by: Meyer & Allen, 1997; Randall, 1990). Several
prior accounting studies also theorize a link be-
tween
commitment
and
performance
(Ferris,
1981; Ferris & Larcker, 1983; Nouri & Parker,
1998; Quirin et al., 2000). In the current study, re-
sults suggest that organizational commitment is
not directly linked to performance; instead, organ-
izational commitment has a signiﬁcant indirect ef-
fect on performance through information sharing.
In other words, information sharing fully mediates
the relation between commitment and perform-
ance. To the knowledge of the authors, no prior
studies have examined this possibility. In the ap-
plied psychology, organizational behavior, and
management literatures, prior studies assume that
commitment leads to higher work eﬀort or motiva-
tion, which, in turn leads to higher performance
(see Meyer & Allen, 1997, Chapter 3). The results
of the current study may interest researchers in
these related disciplines.
The current study includes role ambiguity in the
modeling of information sharing. Results reveal
that budget participation is inversely linked to role
ambiguity, which, in turn, is negatively linked to
performance. This is consistent with ﬁndings re-
ported by Chenhall and Brownell (1988). Extend-
ing their model, the current study also proposes
that role ambiguity adversely aﬀects organizational
commitment, which, in turn, inﬂuences informa-
tion sharing. Statistical results support this. The re-
sults involving role ambiguity (lower commitment,
less information sharing, lower performance) are
congruent with the ideas of Simons (1995). In his
framework, role ambiguity could be considered
an organizational ‘‘block’’ that frustrates opportu-
nities for individual achievement and contribution
to the organization (Chapter 2). Based in part upon
the work of Lawrence and Dyer (1983), Simons
(1995) argues that organizational members must
clearly understand organizational expectations if
they are to share information that leads to organi-
zational learning and innovation.
There are a number of research issues related to
information sharing in the budgeting process that
could be investigated in the future. For example,
information sharing may positively aﬀect not only
subordinate performance but also organizational
performance. Disclosure of private information
by subordinates may facilitate coordination be-
tween organizational subunits (Kanodia, 1993)
and lead to better resource allocation among sub-
units (Shields & Young, 1993). As noted previ-
ously, Simons (1995) argues that subordinates
have private information about strategic uncer-
tainties and methods for dealing with them.
Revealing this information could lead to improve-
ments of existing strategy or completely new strat-
egies that beneﬁt the organization.
Another issue for future research is the linkage
between budget participation, organizational jus-
tice (fairness), and information sharing in the
budgeting process. Several accounting researchers
(e.g., Libby, 1999; Lindquist, 1995) argue that
the form and extent of budget participation inﬂu-
ences the individuals perceptions of organization
fairness. Perceptions of organizational fairness, in
turn, may inﬂuence the willingness of the individ-
ual to share private information in the budgeting
process.

the usual limitations of survey research. The
