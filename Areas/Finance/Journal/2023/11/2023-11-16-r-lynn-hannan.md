Flattening the organization: the effect of organizational
reporting structure on budgeting effectiveness
R. Lynn Hannan • Frederick W. Rankin •
Kristy L. Towry
Published online: 1 May 2010
 Springer Science+Business Media, LLC 2010
Abstract
This study investigates whether increasing a superior’s span of control
improves the effectiveness of the budgeting process. We characterize the superior’s
utility function as consisting of utilities for norm enforcement and wealth, leading
the superior to reject proﬁtable projects believed to contain excessive slack. We
develop theory to predict that superiors become more willing to reject projects as
their span of control increases. Further, subordinates anticipate superiors’ behavior
and reduce slack as span of control increases. Experimental results are consistent
with these predictions. As span of control increases, superiors show a greater
willingness to reject projects that they believe contain excessive slack, and subor-
dinates submit budgets with less slack. The net result is that superiors earn more
proﬁt per subordinate under an expanded span of control. Our study suggests that
increasing span of control can improve the effectiveness of the budgeting process,
an important component of most ﬁrms’ control environments.

1 Introduction
In most organizations, budgeting plays a crucial role in planning and control,
resource allocation, and coordination. Accordingly, budgeting issues and problems
are extensively researched in managerial accounting. Much of this research focuses
on participatory budgeting, a tool that a superior can use to elicit information from
better-informed subordinates. Prior studies have examined a number of issues,
including the factors that inﬂuence the effectiveness of participatory budgeting and
the behavioral consequences of participation. While most prior research focuses on
the participatory dyad (that is, the superior and the subordinate), our study addresses
the inﬂuence of organizational structure on the effectiveness of participatory
budgeting. Speciﬁcally, we examine the effect of increasing a superior’s span of
control (that is, the number of subordinates reporting to the superior, and by logical
extension, the span of operations controlled by the superior) on the level of slack
subordinates report in the budgeting process.
We provide theory and experimental evidence to suggest that increasing the span
of control can reduce the level of slack in subordinates’ budgets because of the
superior’s preference to enforce social norms.1 Thus we demonstrate a beneﬁt to
increasing the span of control that has not been identiﬁed previously. Much of the
prior organizational design research assumes that increasing the span of control
weakens the control environment (Simon 1957; Williamson 1967; Leavitt 2005).
Even the popular press describes ‘‘one inescapable fact about ﬂatter organizations:
The more you ﬂatten, the less you control’’ (Henricks 2005, p. 70). The view that
increasing the span of control diminishes control effectiveness is based primarily on
the notion that the superior cannot effectively monitor many subordinates
concurrently. Although reduced monitoring effectiveness can be a cost of increasing
the span of control, we investigate a potential beneﬁt, which occurs in a
participatory budgeting context. We demonstrate that increasing the span of control
can improve the effectiveness of the participatory budgeting process, thereby
strengthening this important aspect of the control environment.
This research is important to scholars interested in internal control, budgeting,
and organizational design issues, as well as to the practitioners who determine
organizational structures and establish managerial control systems. Empirical and
anecdotal evidence suggests that ﬁrms are ﬂattening their organizational structures
(Henricks 2005; Rajan and Wulf 2006). Because budgeting is a central component
of most ﬁrms’ control systems, it is important to understand the effect of changes in
organizational structures on budgeting effectiveness. Speciﬁcally, if ﬂattening the
organization improves the effectiveness of a formal control mechanism such as
budgeting, this may change the optimal design of the organization and the
management control system. Therefore, such an understanding can lead to improved
control and ultimately better ﬁrm performance. Our research also provides useful
information for the organizational

We develop our hypotheses in a simple capital budgeting setting, in which

subordinates involved in capital budgeting. We manipulate the span of control by
varying the number of subordinates responsible to a superior.3 The experimental

superiors and subordinates. That is, under an expanded span of control, superiors
show a greater willingness to reject projects that they believe include excessive
slack, and subordinates incorporate less slack in their budgets. The overall result of
these two effects is that, as the span of control is increased, superiors earn higher

provides corroborating evidence to support our theory.
The experimental method is particularly useful for our investigation because it
allows us to systematically manipulate the variables of interest while holding all
other factors constant. That is, by holding constant such factors as information
asymmetry and monitoring effectiveness, we can make strong inferences regarding
the causal mechanism for the reduced slack. Further, through the systematic
manipulation of span of control, we can address the endogeneity of organizational
design. Determinants of organizational structures include factors such as human vs.
capital intensity (Rajan and Zingales 2001), technology (Rajan and Wulf 2006), and
the level of diversiﬁcation (Harris and Raviv 2002). The very existence of these
factors makes it difﬁcult to use archival data to ascertain how the resulting
organizational structures affect budgeting effectiveness.

2 Hypotheses development
2.1 Prior literature: span of control
We are aware of no prior studies that speciﬁcally examine the effect of increasing
the span of control on the effectiveness of the budgeting process. However, a
signiﬁcant body of research has examined organizational design issues, including
the determination of the optimal span of control (for example, Calvo and Wellisz
1978; Keren and Levhari 1979). These studies generally assume that increasing the
span of control will lead to a loss of control (for example, Leavitt 2005). For
example, Williamson (1967) argues that delegation leads to control loss because
subordinates cannot fully satisfy their superiors’ expectations. Williamson goes on
to recognize that, at any given layer in the organization, as the span of control
increases the cumulative control loss is also likely to increase. Similarly, Simon
(1957) recognizes that ﬁrms face a dilemma in determining the optimal span of
control due to the tradeoff between the excessive red tape inherent in a restricted
span and the limited ability to supervise too many subordinates in an expanded span.
Other research also assumes that increasing the span of control will reduce the
effectiveness of controls. This assumption is usually based on the supervisor’s
limited ability to monitor the work of many subordinates simultaneously. For
example, in examining the optimal span of control and wage differentials
throughout the hierarchy, Qian (1994) models the probability of a supervisor
checking a subordinate’s work as a decreasing function of the span of control.
Colombo and Delmastro (1999) make the same argument, concluding that
increasing the span of control will result in more shirking by subordinates. Singh
(1985) argues that converting workers to supervisors (and thus decreasing the
average span of control) will increase efﬁciency through better monitoring. In
summary, the organizational design literature has generally assumed a negative
relationship between span of control and control effectiveness.

While not explicitly examining the effects of span of control on control
effectiveness, research in accounting also provides results that are potentially
relevant to the topic. Speciﬁcally, the principal-agent literature examines several
features of the multi-agent setting, which, if incorporated into the employment
contract, can serve as control mechanisms. It is possible that these contractual
mechanisms, such as peer monitoring, relative performance evaluation, and
competition for scarce resources, become more valuable as the superior’s span of
control increases (for example, Frederickson 1992; Balakrishnan 1995; Arya et al.
1996). We extend this research to behavioral mechanisms, providing theory and
experimental evidence to suggest that increasing the span of control can reduce the
level of slack in subordinates’ budgets through the effects of social norm
enforcement.4
2.2 Setting
Our setting is a modiﬁcation of the capital budgeting setting used in a number of
recent experimental studies (for example, Evans et al. 2001; Hannan et al. 2006;
Rankin et al. 2008). In our setting, the superior has a portfolio of projects from
which to choose and sufﬁcient means to fund all projects in the portfolio. That is,
capital is not rationed, and so there is no competition for scarce resources. Each
project is proposed by a different subordinate, and the actual cost associated with
each of the projects is stochastic. For simplicity, each project generates the same
revenue. The actual surplus of each project is greater than or equal to zero for all
possible realizations of the cost, and so if the superior is a proﬁt maximizer, the
superior should prefer funding the project to rejecting it.5 Revenue and the
probability distribution over costs are common knowledge; however, only the
subordinate ever knows the actual cost. Because of the subordinate’s private
information, the superior uses participatory budgeting to elicit a ‘‘cost report’’ (that
is, budget) from the subordinate.
Antle and Eppen (1985) assume that the superior’s utility function includes
preferences for wealth only and show analytically that the superior’s proﬁt is
maximized when he or she sets an ex ante hurdle cost. If the subordinate’s cost
report is less than or equal to the hurdle cost, then the project is funded, and the
subordinate earns rents equal to the difference between the hurdle cost and the
actual cost. However, if the subordinate’s cost report is greater than the hurdle cost,
then the project is rejected. Rankin et al. (2003) provide experimental corroboration
of Antle and Eppen’s result, demonstrating that allowing superiors to commit ex

superiors and subordinates. Below, we review the prior literature leading to the
conclusion that the superior’s utility function includes preferences for enforcing
social norms.
2.3 Prior literature: utility for enforcing social norms
Following recent trends in behavioral economics to incorporate social preferences
into formal models (for example, Rabin 1993; Fehr and Schmidt 1999; Bolton and
Ockenfels 2000; Mittendorf 2006, 2008), we characterize the superior’s utility
function as consisting of two components, a utility for wealth and a utility for
enforcing social norms. Numerous experimental studies support the notion that
individuals will incur a monetary cost to enforce social norms (See Camerer 2003;
Gintis et al. 2003 for reviews). In fact, experimental studies (Kahneman et al. 1986;

Speciﬁcally, they ﬁnd evidence that punishing behavior leads to activation of the

We argue that the superior’s utility for enforcing a social norm is both separable
by and speciﬁc to the subordinate. In other words, if a subordinate is perceived to
have violated a social norm by incorporating excess slack into the budget, the
superior will receive utility from rejecting that subordinate’s project but not from
rejecting any other subordinate’s project. The concept of separability is based on the
notion that societies place great importance on individual responsibility for adhering
to social norms. In fact, socialization, through which an individual becomes a
member of a group, is deﬁned as the process through which the individual adopts
the group’s norms (Schaefer 2004). That is, an individual’s ability and willingness
to adhere to a group’s norms forms the basis of eligibility for membership in the
group. If individuals are held responsible for adhering to social norms, it is
reasonable to expect that the utility for enforcing these norms would be speciﬁc to
the individual as well.
The notion of separability is consistent with the current state of the literature.
Speciﬁcally, Fehr and Schmidt (1999) and Falk and Fischbacher (2006) model
utility for equity and reciprocity, respectively, as separable by individual, and
experimental evidence is generally consistent with the utility functions proposed in
these papers (for example Falk et al. 2005; Falk and Fischbacher 2005). Although
separability has not been explicitly tested, Falk and Fischbacher (2005) review the
relevant literature and conclude that, in building a model of reciprocity (and
retribution) involving more than two players, the best approach assumes
separability.

The characterization of the two sources of utility forms the basis of our

speciﬁc to, the subordinate. That is, the wealth received from accepting one

that a social norm has been violated. The notion of ‘‘strength of perception’’

with the degree of certainty that slack is excessive.10,11 As discussed earlier, and in
contrast to the superior’s utility for wealth, we characterize the superior’s utility for
enforcing a social norm as both separable by and speciﬁc to the subordinate.

2.6 Hypothesis: subordinate behavior
Our second hypothesis relates to the reporting behavior of subordinates. While prior
literature implies that social preferences such as honesty or fairness may inﬂuence
the reporting behavior of subordinates, individual preferences such as these would
not likely interact with span of control.12 Therefore, for purposes of our hypothesis
development, we make the simplifying assumption that subordinates are wealth
maximizers. Under this assumption, we predict that under an expanded span of
control, subordinates will submit budgets with less slack. This prediction ﬂows
directly from Hypothesis 1. That is, if superiors are more willing to reject projects
under an expanded span of control, then wealth-maximizing subordinates will
incorporate less slack in their budgets under an expanded span of control. Of course,
this logic assumes that subordinates have some degree of insight into superiors’
project acceptance decisions. Economists describe such insight as strategic
anticipation, that is, the ability to anticipate another’s action and choose the best
response given this expectation. Experimental results from ultimatum games (Roth
et al. 1991; Winter and Zamir 2005) and budgeting settings (Rankin et al. 2003)
provide evidence that individuals are adept at anticipating the behavior of others.
Accordingly, we expect that subordinates will strategically anticipate the behavior
of superiors and report lower project costs under an expanded span of control. This
leads to our second hypothesis.

Participants interact over a computer network in order to maintain anonymity. At

dyads, and the matching protocol ensures that each subordinate and superior are
paired only once during the experiment. Participants are aware that there is only one
subordinate reporting to each superior and that they are re-matched each period.
Each period consists of the same basic procedures: (1) each subordinate learns the
actual cost of his or her project, (2) each subordinate proposes a project by
submitting a cost report to his or her respective superior, (3) the superior determines
whether to accept and fund the project, and (4) the subordinate is informed of the
superior’s decision. The subordinate receives no information regarding other
subordinates’ actual costs, project proposals, or acceptance decisions.
The subordinate’s payoff function is
Reported cost – actual cost, if the project is accepted
0, if the project is rejected
To prevent the possibility of negative earnings, the computer program requires that
reported cost be greater than or equal to actual cost.
The superior’s payoff function is
Project revenue – reported cost, if the project is accepted
0, if the project is rejected
Project revenue and the distribution of project costs are public information. If a
project is implemented, it generates revenues of $30. Project costs are uniformly
distributed between $1 and $30 in whole dollar increments, that is, {$1, $2, $3,…,
$30}. The actual project cost is determined randomly each period and is private
information, known only to the individual subordinate. Project costs are indepen-
dently drawn for each subordinate, and participants are informed as such.
The process by which the superior decides whether to accept the project differs
slightly between periods 1–4 and periods 5–8. In the ﬁrst four periods, the superior
decides whether to accept the project after observing the reported cost. In the last
four periods, we employ the ‘‘strategy method,’’ by requiring the superior to set a
threshold cost before observing the reported cost.13 If the reported cost is less than
or equal to the threshold cost, the project is accepted; otherwise it is rejected.
Importantly, this modiﬁcation does not change the payoff function of either the
superior or the subordinate. That is, the superior funds accepted projects at the

3.2 High span of control condition
High span sessions consist of 12 subordinates and four superiors. The only
difference between the low span and high span sessions is that each superior has one
subordinate in the low span sessions, while each superior has three subordinates in
the high span sessions. Capital is not rationed, so the superior can choose to fund all
projects.
As in the low span sessions, superiors and subordinates are re-matched each
period. The matching protocol ensures that each subordinate is matched with each
superior only once during periods 1 through 4 and once during periods 5 through 8.
Subordinates are also re-grouped each period. Participants are aware that there are
three subordinates reporting to each superior and that they are re-grouped each
period. We do not limit the number of projects that a superior can fund. Therefore,
the superior can potentially accept, fund, and earn revenues from all three projects
in each period.
As in the low span sessions, project costs are drawn privately and independently
for each subordinate; that is, subordinates only know their own project costs. In
order to facilitate comparisons across conditions, we use the same sets of actual
costs, so that the mean actual cost is the same each period for both the low span and
the high span conditions. Speciﬁcally, we randomly generated 12 sets of actual cost
draws, one for each subordinate in the high span condition. These same sets are used
in the low span sessions. Because there are only eight subordinates in the low span
sessions, we use eight of the 12 cost draws in each session. We rotate the sets to

3.3 Payoff-adjusted low span condition
A consequence of our primary manipulation is that the range of payoffs available to
the superiors varies across our two primary conditions. Recall that in the high span
condition, the superior has the opportunity to earn revenues from all three projects,
for a total of $90, less costs. In contrast, in the low span condition, the superior has
the opportunity to earn revenues of only $30, less cost. For this reason, one might
argue that if we observe a greater willingness to reject projects in the high span
condition, as we predict, the result could be entirely driven by differences in the
superior’s payoff potential. Although we are aware of no theory that would predict
such an effect of payoff potential, we include the payoff-adjusted low span of
control condition to control for this possibility. These sessions are identical to the
low span sessions (including the number of participants and matching protocol)
except that the revenue and cost parameters are multiplied by three. Speciﬁcally,
certain revenue from accepted projects is $90, and project costs range from $3 to
$90. Thus, in comparing this condition to the high span condition, the superior’s
payoff potential is held constant. It should be noted, however, that the payoff
potential of the subordinates varies between this condition and the high span
condition. This is the unavoidable consequence of varying the number of
subordinates matched with each superior—either the superiors’ or the subordinates’
payoff potential must vary across conditions. By including all three conditions, we
eliminate the possibility that payoff potential creates a confound.
3.4 Design choices
In this section we discuss several design choices, focusing on the inferential
advantages of each. First, as described earlier, we ensure that all interactions are
completely anonymous among subordinates and superiors. Further, all information
on actual costs, reported costs, and acceptance decisions is kept private within each
superior-subordinate dyad. Importantly, even in the high span condition, the three
subordinates reporting to a given superior do not learn one another’s information.
This anonymity helps us to rule out implicit competition as an alternate explanation
for subordinate behavior. We deﬁne implicit competition as the desire to perform
better than other subordinates. In our setting this could result in a stronger desire for
project acceptance in the high span condition. However, by maintaining strict
control over information, we prevent participants from being inﬂuenced by
comparing outcomes.
Second, the operationalization of low (high) span at one (three) subordinates
allows for a strong test of our theory, which relies crucially on an assumption of
diminishing marginal utility for wealth. Participants will be particularly adverse to a
decision resulting in only $10 wealth (the participation fee paid to all participants).
By operationalizing the low span setting at the level of one, we thus place our
participants at the point on their utility curves where the change in the marginal

utility for wealth is greatest (that is, the difference in utility associated with the ﬁrst
and second project acceptances is much greater than the difference associated with
the second and third project acceptances). An added beneﬁt of operationalizing the
low span condition with one subordinate is that it is consistent with prior
experimental and analytic research.
Finally, recall that we use two procedures for eliciting the superiors’ decisions
(explicit decisions in periods 1–4 and the strategy method in periods 5–8). We do so
because each method has advantages and disadvantages. The strategy method
allows us to gather more precise data on the superiors’ willingness to reject projects,
because it controls for variance in observed cost reports across periods and
superiors. Further, because this method requires that the threshold be set before the
superior observes any cost reports, it ensures that superiors will not (incorrectly)
assume that information on one participant’s cost is informative of another
participant’s cost.15 For example, if two of the three subordinates reported relatively
low costs and the third reported a high cost, the superior may have incorrectly
assessed the probability that the third was overstating costs at a higher level than if
the same report was made in the low span condition. The strategy method controls
for this potential misperception. The strategy method also ensures that subordinates’
reports are not inﬂuenced by the perception that the superior (in the high span
condition) may compare subordinate reports in order to make comparative
assessments and acceptance decision. Finally, the strategy method allows us to
rule out any potential demand effects that could be created by the perception that the
participant should reject a project in the high span condition.
Although the strategy method has these advantages, it has the disadvantage of
imposing strategic uncertainty on the superior. That is, under the strategy method, the
superior sets the threshold without knowing with certainty the ﬁnancial conse-
quences of his or her decisions. On the other hand, when the superior explicitly
accepts or rejects each project, the superior knows the ﬁnancial consequences of his
or her decision with certainty. Therefore, by using both elicitation procedures we
can capture the advantages of each, ultimately enhancing our ability to make
inferences.

4.4 Discussion of Experiment 1 and introduction of Experiment 2
Experiment 1 provides evidence that increasing the span of control affects the
behavior of both superiors and subordinates. Our theory is that an expanded span of
control increases the superiors’ willingness to reject projects for which slack is
perceived to be excessive, and that, as a result, subordinates reduce their slack.
While we ﬁnd support for both hypotheses, the experimental design of Experiment 1
leaves some ambiguity regarding the interpretation of the results. Speciﬁcally, we
cannot conclude with certainty that the superiors’ behavior, rather than the
subordinates’ behavior, forms the beginning of the causal chain. It is possible that
the span of control directly affects subordinates’ behavior and that any differences
in superiors’ acceptance decisions are driven by differences in subordinate
reporting. Therefore, to strengthen our inferential validity, we conduct Experiment
2 to rule out this reverse causality.
5 Experiment 2

isolate any direct effects of span of control on subordinate behavior (that is, effects

6 Discussion
This study investigates whether increasing a superior’s span of control reduces the
level of slack in a participatory budgeting setting. In contrast to prior accounting
studies that investigate the value of contractual mechanisms in reducing slack, we
extend this research to behavioral mechanisms. Speciﬁcally, we provide theory and
experimental evidence to suggest that increasing the span of control can reduce the
level of slack in subordinates’ budgets through the effects of social norm
enforcement. Speciﬁcally, under an expanded span of control, superiors show a
greater willingness to reject projects for which they believe slack is excessive.
Further, subordinates respond by building less slack into their budgets. The net
result of these two effects is that as the span of control is increased, superiors earn
more proﬁt per subordinate. In contrast to prior research that focuses on moral
hazard (hidden action) problems and assumes a loss of control as the span of control
is increased, our study demonstrates that increasing the span of control can actually
strengthen control over a budgeting problem characterized by asymmetric
information.
Our results are especially relevant given the empirical and anecdotal evidence
suggesting that ﬁrms are getting ﬂatter over time. While the reason for this trend is
unclear, authors conjecture a number of causes, including improvements in
corporate governance, improved information technology, and even consulting fads
(Rajan and Wulf 2006). Regardless of the motives ﬁrms have for ﬂattening their
organizations, our research provides evidence that with regard to the budgeting
process, ﬂattening the ﬁrm may actually improve an aspect of the control
environment.
Although our study ﬁnds that an expanded span of control results in reduced
slack in our setting, this does not suggest that the net beneﬁt of increasing the span
of control is always positive. Our study isolates and investigates one path between
span of control and slack, that of norm enforcement. The effect of this path likely
occurs simultaneously with a number of other effects, the net of which cannot be
unambiguously predicted.28 That is, the various effects of increasing the span of
control are likely to vary across organizations due to different contextual factors.
Therefore, the overall effect of increasing the span of control is unique to the
individual organization. For example, prior organizational design research generally
assumes that increasing the span of control reduces the superior’s ability to
effectively monitor subordinates. In some organizations, monitoring effectiveness
can be increased inexpensively through electronic systems. In such organizations,

In this appendix, we describe a speciﬁc utility function to illustrate the intuition
behind our hypotheses. Our setting is a modiﬁcation of the capital budgeting setting
used in a number of recent experimental studies (for example, Evans et al. 2001;
Rankin et al. 2003; Hannan et al. 2006). Project completion requires the
subordinate’s presence and funding provided by the superior. The cost of the
project is uniformly distributed as c[[cmin, cmax], and revenue, R, is equal to cmax.
The expected total surplus from the project is given by R - E(c). Note, however,
that for all realizations of c [[cmin, cmax], the actual surplus, R - c, is greater than or
equal to zero, and so the superior should prefer funding the project to rejecting it.
