A Panic Attack on the Way to Paradise
There I was, sitting on a train headed to the airport for what should have been a relaxing holiday, when my chest suddenly felt like it was pounding faster than the beat at a techno festival. My heart wasn't just beating, it was practically near the finish of a 100-mile race.
I glanced down at my watch: 88 bpm and climbing fast. Now, for most people, that might sound pretty normal. But here's the thing, my resting heart rate typically hovers around 50-55, maybe creeping up to 60 on those nights when sleep decides to play hard to get. This wasn't just elevated; this was my body on the brink of staging a full-scale rebellion, I looked again it shot up to 97.
I'd never experienced anything like this before. Here I was, supposedly relaxed, about to jet off to Valencia, and my nervous system was acting like I'd just been chased by a bear. I paused the podcast humming in my earphones and tried the breathing technique that usually works wonders: four seconds in, ten seconds out. Deep, intentional breaths.
But my mind? It refused to cooperate. Instead of settling down, it felt like my thoughts were gearing up for an ultramarathon, and I hadn't even tied my freaking shoes!
The Sunday That Changed Everything
Let me rewind to Sunday, because that's when the real story begins. My girlfriend and I had what we called a "future summit" one of those deep, intentional conversations that couples either avoid forever or embrace as relationship gold. We'd both filled out this comprehensive questionnaire the day before (thank you, AI for making these things more structured than just some random questions on a piece of paper), covering everything from financial goals to family visions to those non-negotiable "hell no's" that define our boundaries.
The conversation itself was incredible. We mapped out where our dreams intersected and where they diverged. We talked about freedom numbers, you know, that magical amount where money stops being a daily stress. We discussed where we wanted to plant roots, what family meant to us, and all those big life questions that usually get pushed aside for Netflix and a glass of wine on the weekend.
Then, that evening, it hit me all at once: time.

Not the abstract concept of time we all acknowledge, but the very real, very finite nature of our timelines. As we had laid out our ambitions, aspirations, and must-haves, earlier that afternoon it became crystal clear that we had packed about five years' worth of goals into what realistically might be a two-year window.
You know that feeling when you're looking at your calendar and realize you've somehow scheduled three meetings at the same time? That's what our life plan looked like, except instead of double-booked Tuesday afternoons, we were talking about double-booked life phases.
Being Aware Brings a Sense Beautiful Terror
Here's where it gets interesting, maybe even a bit uncomfortable. That panic attack wasn't really about my heart rate or some random anxiety spike. It was my subconscious finally processing what my rational brain had been trying to organize since Sunday.
I'd spent four days thinking I was being productive and strategic, mapping out this beautiful life plan with my partner. But underneath all that scheduling, my nervous system was doing math. The kind of math that says: "Hey buddy, remember when you thought you had all the time in the world? Well, (duhh) plot twist you don't."
The thing is, this realization terrifying as it felt in that train seat, is a gift (in hindsight obviously). Most people float through life in what psychologists call "time affluence," this vague sense that there's always more time later. We'll start that business next year. We'll have kids when we're "ready" (whatever that means). We'll travel when we retire.
But when you sit down and actually map out your priorities against your timeline, something profound happens. Suddenly, every decision becomes more intentional. Every "yes" means a "no" to something else. Every month that passes isn't just calendar pages, it’s opportunity cost (in real time).
The Science of Time in Perspective
Let's get a bit nerdy here because the psychology behind this is fascinating. What I experienced taps into something researchers call "time perspective theory". In laymen’s terms: How our perception of time affects our behavior and well-being.
When people become acutely aware of time's finite nature, it often triggers what psychologists call "future time perspective anxiety." It's not depression about the past or stress about the present, it's anxiety about fitting everything meaningful into the time you have left. And here's the kicker: it typically hits high achievers and ambitious people harder because we (yes, I know I said it) tend to have more items on our "life bucket list."
Dr. Laura Carstensen's work on socioemotional selectivity theory shows that when people perceive their time as limited, they become more selective about their goals and relationships. They focus more on emotionally meaningful experiences and less on expanding their horizons just for the sake of it.
In other words, that panic attack might have been my brain's dramatic way of saying: "Time to get serious about what actually matters."
The Planning Paradox
Here's something counterintuitive: the more clearly we plan our lives, the more aware we become of time's constraints. It's like finally organizing your messy closet only to realize you have way more stuff than space. Before the organization, the chaos was overwhelming but vague. After organizing, you have clarity, but also the realization that you have limited storage.
That Sunday conversation gave clarity about our shared vision, which was beautiful. But it also gave clarity about our timeline, which was... well, panic-attack-inducing, apparently.
The irony isn't lost on me that I was heading to vacation, that thing we do to slow down and enjoy life, when this realization about life's pace hit me hardest.
Should we Make Time to Schedule our Time?
So here we are, at the end of this exploration, and I'm left with more questions than answers. From where should I start to will I ever be content with the life I have or is it always a journey of persuite? But maybe that's the point. Maybe sometimes we need to slow down before we can speed up again, and maybe the question isn't whether we have enough time (we never do). The question is whether we're brave enough to really look at the time we have and dare to make it count.
