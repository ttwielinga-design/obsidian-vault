Those Fleeting Glimpses
When Someone's Inner Child Breaks Through
Unknown Wisdm
Jun 28, 2025

You know that moment when you catch someone completely off guard, not in a gotcha way, but in something far more precious? It's when their inner child suddenly surfaces, just for a split second, and you get to witness the person they were before life taught them to guard their wonder.
I've been thinking about this a lot lately. As we get older, these glimpses become rarer, more sacred somehow. They're not necessarily someone's "true colors". We're all layered, complex beings. But they're windows into a version of themselves that still carries pure enthusiasm, unfiltered curiosity, or sometimes, old wounds that never quite healed.
What Psychology Tells Us About Our Inner Child
Carl Jung first proposed the concept of an Inner Child after exploring his own childlike inner feelings and emotions. He discovered something profound: after his break with Freud, he became aware he'd lost the creativity and love of building things he'd had as a child. So he started experimenting with something quite remarkable: playing, building with stones like he had as a boy, reconnecting with that forgotten part of himself.
Jung understood that our inner child isn't just nostalgia or immaturity. It's the repository of our earliest emotions, our capacity for wonder (and yes), our deepest hurts. It holds both our light and our shadows, our dreams and our fears, all wrapped up in the psyche of the person we once were.
According to Jung,"nothing has a stronger influence psychologically on their environment and especially on their children than the unlived life of the parent."
Think about that for a moment. The parts of ourselves we've buried, ignored, or forgotten don't just disappear: they influence everything around us.
When Wonder Breaks Through
Let me tell you about two moments that have stuck with me, because they perfectly capture how differently our inner child can show up.
I was sitting with my mother-in-law on a bench at a jazz festival. My girlfriend and father-in-law had gone to grab drinks, leaving us in that comfortable space that sometimes exists between in-laws, not quite family, not quite strangers. We started talking about an upcoming trip to Mexico, and suddenly her whole demeanor shifted.
She began telling me about her own time in Mexico, how she'd been to Cancun, about the Mayan temples she'd seen. But it wasn't just the stories, it was the way her eyes lit up. For those few minutes, I wasn't sitting with the woman I knew. I was sitting with the young traveler she'd once been, someone who had looked at ancient stones and felt genuine awe.
It was beautiful and heartbreaking at the same time. Beautiful because that wonder was still there, just beneath the surface. Heartbreaking because I realized how rarely we get to see that version of the people we know and care about so deeply.
Here's the thing: I don't think I would have noticed that shift if I hadn't spent significant time with her. You need to know someone well enough to spot when they slip into something different, something more unguarded.
When Old Wounds Surface
The second glimpse was darker, but equally revealing.
I was presenting a software system I'd spent five weeks developing to the owner of the company where I worked. He sat across from me, silent as usual, while I went through my slides. When I finished, instead of his typical measured feedback, he said something that caught me off guard: "I did not expect this presentation to come."
Fair enough, I thought. Then: "I do NOT believe in this product and the way you have approached this."
Okay, harsh but within his rights as the boss. But what came next floored me.
He started laughing and said, "I am going to make a picture and send it to the head of development just to show you how stupid you guys are with this idea."
In that moment, I saw it: a glimpse of his inner child. Not the wonder-filled version, but the wounded one. The kid who had learned to build himself up by tearing others down, who had never learned better ways to feel powerful or secure.
It was cruel, yes. But it was also revealing. Beneath all his authority and business acumen was someone who still fought playground battles in boardrooms.
What Triggers These Moments?
I've been thinking about what causes these inner child moments to surface, and I think it comes down to a few key triggers.
Emotional safety or threat. My mother-in-law felt safe in that moment, relaxed, unhurried, talking about something she loved. My boss felt threatened by work he deemed inadequate, and his inner child responded the only way it knew how.
Unexpected connections. When we stumble onto topics that bypass our adult filters (memories of travel, childhood interests, deep fears), our inner child can slip through before our grown-up selves catch up.
Moments of genuine enthusiasm or vulnerability. Whether it's excitement about past adventures or the sting of professional rejection, intense emotions can strip away our carefully constructed adult personas.
Being truly seen by someone who knows us. This is why I think time and familiarity matter so much. Our inner child tends to emerge when we're with people who've seen us across different contexts, who we trust (consciously or not) with our unguarded moments.
What These Glimpses Teach Us
First learning: Everyone carries both light and shadow from childhood. My mother-in-law's inner child held wonder and curiosity. My boss held insecurity and learned cruelty. We all have both aspects—the question is which ones we've nurtured and which ones still need healing.
Second learning: These moments are gifts, even the difficult ones. When someone's inner child surfaces, you're seeing something real and unfiltered. With my mother-in-law, it was a moment of genuine connection. With my boss, it was clarity about who I was dealing with—valuable information, even if it was uncomfortable.
The practical takeaway: Pay attention. Start watching for these moments in the people around you. They'll teach you things about your relationships that years of surface-level interaction never could. When you see someone's inner child, whether it's joyful or wounded, you're being trusted with something precious, even if they don't realize they're sharing it.
And here's what I find most compelling: these glimpses remind us that everyone we meet is carrying around their childhood self, complete with all the dreams, hurts, and wonder that shaped them. That difficult colleague, that distant family member, that neighbor who seems so put-together. They all have an inner child who occasionally breaks through, hoping to be seen, understood, or simply allowed to exist.
Maybe the real gift isn't just recognizing these moments in others, but creating the kind of presence that makes them feel safe enough to happen in the first place.
