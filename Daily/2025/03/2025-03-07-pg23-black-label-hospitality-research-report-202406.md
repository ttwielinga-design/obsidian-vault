Management Summary 
Black Label Hospitality (BLH), a Third-Party White-Label Operator (TPWLO) 
founded by Martin Hendricks, has experienced substantial growth within the Netherlands. To 
capitalize on this success, BLH seeks to expand its market reach and enter Germany. This 
paper aims to analyze the strategic considerations BLH must address for a successful German 
market entry, specifically focusing on optimizing its White-Label positioning within the 
market. The core management question that will be analyzed is: 
“How can Black Label Hospitality optimize its strategy for entering the German 
market, to enhance its White-Label positioning?” 
This question guides the exploration of key factors that influence BLH's success in the 
German market, with a particular emphasis on leveraging its existing strategy to establish a 
strong market presence.  
This paper explores three critical components which have emerged from exploratory 
research to address the management question, namely Growth Pathways, Partnership 
Selection, and Risk Assessment. A qualitative research methodology, employing semi-
structured interviews with various stakeholders, will address the knowledge gaps and develop 
key research questions. These stakeholders include Hotel Chains, Competitors, Industry 
Experts, Developers, Investors, and Consultants.  
Within the component of Growth Pathways, four themes are explored. The first theme 
is about the differentiation among TPWLOs. The second theme investigates the perceptions 
of the competitive landscape for TPWLOs in Germany. The third theme focuses on 
identifying the optimal initial steps for a TPWLO when entering the German market. The last 
theme uncovers the emerging niches within the German TPWLO market. The findings from 
these themes amalgamate in a recommendation for the component Growth Pathways. BLH 

2 
 
should recognize that TPWLOs in the German market are besides operational effectiveness 
and strategic positioning quite similar. BLH should focus on maximizing conversions in the 
saturated German TPWLO market by leveraging their existing brand awareness in A and B 
locations and considering the economic benefits of B and C locations. Dual branding is not 
advisable for BLH, unless in niche and or rare cases with long-stay in A, and (sometimes) B 
locations.  
Within the component of Partnership selection, three themes are explored and 
translated into targeted research questions. The first theme explores the nuances of secondary 
conditions offered by Hotel Chains, specifically focusing on key money structures and 
franchise agreements. The second theme identifies what features make a hotel chain choose a 
specific TPWLO. The final theme uncovers to what extent loyalty/exclusivity plays a role in 
the project allocation by hotel chains. The findings from these themes amalgamate in a 
recommendation for the component Partnership Selection. Effective communication and 
good relationship management with all stakeholders are essential for BLH. Chains and 
investors prefer exclusivity for reliability, but experts suggest a broader portfolio with more 
chains for better terms. Working with two to three hotel chains might offer better negotiation 
possibilities and steady project streams. Managing too many chains can be confusing for a 
TPWLO. Hotel chains varied secondary conditions, with key money structures tailored to 
specific needs and strategic locations. Key money affects contract valuation and, in most 
cases, may worsen franchise fee negotiations. 
Within the final component of Risk Assessment, three themes are explored and 
translated into targeted research questions. The first theme delves deeper into the potential 
challenges of forming strategic alliances. Furthermore, the second theme identifies the key 
indicators of growing too quickly. The final theme within this component analyzes the risks 
of cultural integration when expanding to Germany. The culmination of these findings will 

3 
 
provide actionable insights to inform the Partnership Selection component. Resource 
management and adjusting the structure are important. Ensuring financial stability and 
managing cash flow effectively are crucial. Internal personnel must handle expansion 
skillfully, and BLH should avoid overpaying rent. Forming strategic alliances requires 
knowledge of stakeholders’ financial and credibility perceptions. Address language barriers, 
outsource to local parties, and develop a deep understanding of local business culture. 
To optimize BLH’s strategy for entering the German market and ensure a strong 
white-label positioning, the recommendations derived from primary and secondary research 
are analyzed and are the foundation for the advice given in the advisory report.

Chapter 1. Introduction 
Black Label Hospitality (BLH), a Third-Party White Label Operator (TPWLO), 
specializing in the fast-moving hospitality sector, offering luxury and lifestyle-oriented hotel 
services. BLH has already secured several multi-unit development agreements with the 
leading hotel chains - Marriott, Accor, Hilton, Wyndham, Hyatt, and IHG. The company is 
now looking to expand (Black Label Hospitality, 2023). However, before we discuss 
TPWLOs, we need to know what they are. In short, they are a hotel management company 
without a trademark (Wagner, 2024). As a TPWLO, BLH is the vital bridge between the 
property (developers) owners and hotel chains, thus, it is the key player that influences the 
improvement of its partners' brand value and operational efficiency. The role of the chain 
here is to provide support to the franchisee (e.g., helping with negotiating OTA fees1, finding 
staff, and supply chain) in exchange for a franchise fee. On the contrary, a branded TPWLO 
does not have a franchisor, so they do not need to pay these royalty fees. However, they do 
not have the added benefit that a chain can offer. BLH is looking to broaden its focus, from 
only the Netherlands to now spreading its wings into the German market. Currently BLH 
operates seven properties in the Netherlands with several other projects in the pipeline, since 
they feel the Dutch market is saturated, they want to expand. 
This report is a preliminary study for the advice on how BLH can best enter the 
German market and what are the pitfalls and/or opportunities here. 
BLH emphasizes financially viable projects, striving for a GOP of 35% in the first 
stable year, and fits with their standards and strategic objectives, such as long-term leases of 
20 years or more (Black Label Hospitality, 2023).

2.2.1 Vehicles  
In the strategy diamond framework, 'Vehicles' represent the actions required to 
achieve strategic objectives. They function as the roadmap, guiding you from your desired 
arenas (target markets) and differentiators (competitive advantages) to strategic success 
(Coyne & Whittington, 2015).  
Franchise agreements are a strategic choice for hotel operators seeking success in a 
competitive industry, offering a powerful combination of brand recognition, operational 
support, and financial advantages (Carlton Fields, 2022). Operators retain decision-making 
authority but need to comply with brand standards (Mahroos, 2021). 
Franchisors typically provide expertise, support, training, and access to knowledge 
bases, marketing resources, and best practices, enhancing operational efficiency and brand 
alignment (Mahroos, 2021). Franchising also facilitates market expansion for the hotel 
chains. Chains leverage local franchisees with established networks to penetrate new markets 
(Carpentier & Collins, 2022). 
In essence, a franchise agreement is a license agreement between the hotel operator 
(franchisee) and the brand owner (franchisor) (Carlton Fields, 2022). It outlines the rights and 
obligations for operating under the brand in exchange for fees (Carlton Fields, 2022). While 
offering flexibility, these agreements are personal and cannot be transferred by the current 
owner (Carlton Fields, 2022). 
Franchised hotel operators strategically use key money during franchise applications 
to secure agreements and attract owners/developers (Shindler, 2023; Kornreich, 2017). This 

16 
 
financial incentive benefits the hotel operator and hotel chain involved in hotel transactions. 
For hotel chains, key money secures the right property in a competitive location, 
strengthening their market presence (Ploppa, 2022). TPWLOs are also able to receive key 
money from chains, accepting this however increases the franchise fee that you must pay. 
Therefore, key money for TPWLOs is a long-term loan that you pay overtime through your 
franchise fee.  
The structure of key money agreements varies between hotel companies (Shindler, 
2023). The most common structure is a self-amortizing loan, where the key money is 
forgiven if the agreement is fulfilled. Early termination requires repayment of the remaining 
balance (Shindler, 2023). Some companies use annual reduction, gradually reducing the 
outstanding amount throughout the agreement to discourage early termination (Shindler, 
2023). Alternatively, repayment upon termination requires the unamortized amount to be 
repaid if the agreement ends prematurely (Shindler, 2023). There are also custom structures, 
where some companies use the term "consideration" within the agreement (Shindler, 2023). 
The direction of payment depends on the company type (Shindler, 2023; Hendricks, 
2024; Hodgson et al., 2022). Typically, the franchisor pays the franchisee. In rare cases, an 
operator might pay the owner, particularly in high-demand locations (Hodgson et al., 2022). 
Hotel operators, like BLH, could receive key money from chains (Hendricks, 2024; Hodgson 
et al., 2022). In highly sought-after locations, property owners might demand key money due 
to scarcity and high demand (Hodgson et al., 2022). Overall, key money plays a significant 
role in shaping hotel agreements and attracting owners to specific chains (Shindler, 2023; 
Kornreich, 2017). 
In the hospitality industry, the Step-in clause is a critical safeguard for property 
owners. It ensures that they receive support in the event of a management failure by the 

17 
 
service provider. For BLH, operating as a third-party white-label operator, this clause is 
particularly crucial. It allows the hotel chains to take over management duties if Black Label 
fails to meet its obligations, ensuring continuous support for the property owner and 
uninterrupted hotel operations (Sreenivasan & Ang, 2022). The Step-in clause protects the 
property owner’s interests by facilitating a smooth transition of management responsibilities 
to the hotel chains in case of BLH ’s underperformance (Goodwin, 2019).  
Considering all the secondary conditions above the question remains what the 
differences are offered by the chains to TPWLO’s.  
2.2.2 Differentiators

maintain a sustainable competitive advantage. The remainder of the text will focus on 
competitive analysis to understand how companies within the industry compare and compete. 
The following companies are selected by BLH as their competitors. All the hotel operators

3.3 Data Processing and Analysis 
Following data collection, thematic analysis was used for the data obtained. This 
consists of a mix of inductive and deductive coding, using excel. This method is especially 
useful for detecting, evaluating, and reporting patterns (themes) in data. The methodology 
was separated into eight parts to ensure a thorough and keen understanding of the TPWLO 
market findings. 
Initially, the team reviewed the interview recordings and went on to transcribe them. 
This process involved repeatedly listening to the recordings and writing transcripts. After this 
we reread them to catch any mistakes made, and to make ourselves more familiar with the 
data, acquiring an early idea of the participant's views. 
The next step was to systematically code the data. A form of inductive coding was 
used in these steps, namely thematic coding. This analysis is the process of segmenting and 
categorizing data to find parts relevant to different research questions. This was accomplished 
by highlighting and annotating text fragments within the transcripts that reflected noteworthy 
or intriguing topics relevant to our study's research questions. When a relevant text fragment 
was found it was put into a separate sheet in the coding excel file to connect it to one of the 
research questions (this is what makes it partially deductive). 

34 
 
After organizing the text fragments (that could answer the research questions), the 
team organized each sheet into potential themes (open codes). This entailed grouping similar 
codes into thematic groups that captured the key notions arising from the data. Each 
forthcoming theme was assessed to the coded sections and the complete data set to ensure it 
represented a meaningful pattern. 
The following phase entailed assessing the selected open codes to ensure that they 
followed a consistent pattern and were relevant to the study topics. This process occasionally 
prompted the team to go back over the data and re-code or modify the themes to accurately 
reflect the results. 
After the open codes had been refined, they were combined into new codes (axial) and 
given names. This procedure required writing analyses for each topic, describing what it 
revealed about the data and why it was relevant to the research goals. The codes were named 
to capture their essence. 
After we had gathered the axial codes, we again looked at them from a bird's eye view 
perspective and bundled them into even broader themes (selective codes). We then reviewed 
the whole line of codes to ensure everyone was on board with the ways this specific section 
was coded. 
To ensure the consistency and reliability of the coding process, reliability checks were 
implemented. This process involved the following steps: 
1. Independent Coding: One team member independently coded a subset of transcripts. 
2. Comparison and Discussion: The team members then showed their codes to another 
team member so it could be checked and validated. Any differences in interpretation 
were then discussed. This discussion helped to resolve discrepancies and refine the 
coding scheme. 

35 
 
The final stage was to include the thematic analysis in the research report. This 
involved selecting vivid and representative quotes from the transcripts to highlight each 
concept. All this was clearly structured to make sure all the research questions were answered 
up to standards. The report narratively presented how the themes emerged from the codes and 
how they linked to the research questions, providing a comprehensive, thorough, and 
complicated description of the data.

service to the guest, calls the extent to which TPWLOs differ into doubt. Stakeholders 
frequently face the issue of identifying unique value propositions in a homogeneous 
market. Is a TPWLO fundamentally a commodity, with operators providing nearly 
identical services, or is there considerable differentiation that affects strategic and 
operational effectiveness? The research provides a nuanced viewpoint. While TPWLOs 
have strong commodity features in their basic business models and operational frameworks, 
strategic differentiation based on market focus, brand alignment, and local flexibility 
provides points of uniqueness by combining commodity and differentiated product traits.  
4.1.1 Core Business Model 
The analysis provides a nuanced view of whether TPWLOs are commodities or 
differentiated products. According to the hotel chains and experts, TPWLOs have significant 
commodity features and little distinction amongst operators. A consultant stated "Essentially, 
they are the same,"(CNS_05, Page 2). A different consultant emphasized the importance of 
economic scale, citing the operators' quick scaling and established market presence as truly 
important (CNS_03). This viewpoint was shared by an industry expert, who explained that

substantial competition amongst TPWLO’s. To gain deeper insights on how this competition

4.3 First steps for Market Expansion  
Expanding into the German market presents opportunities and challenges for 
TPWLOs, requiring a smart approach to ensure success. Industry experts, competitors, and 
consultants highlight the importance of understanding local market dynamics, securing 
trustworthy partnerships, and managing operational and financial coordination. Missteps in 
these areas can lead to financial loss and reputational damage. Given these challenges, the 
first steps a TPWLO should take when expanding to Germany are acquiring extensive 
local knowledge, hiring responsive and active leadership, outsourcing non-core businesses 
(human resources, legal, and accounting) to local experts, establishing trust with local 
landlords, forming strategic plans with hotel chains, evaluating real estate opportunities for 
conversion, conducting feasibility studies, focusing on prominent cities, and building strong 
networks through industry events. Furthermore, providing financial stability through well-
structured financial strategies and upholding a good reputation are critical for obtaining the 
trust of local stakeholders (EXP_01; EXP_02). Effective market presence and exposure at 
industry events strengthen the company's position and lead to new prospects. This is a step 
most stakeholders noted, although this step is not considered as a first step when entering the 

43 
 
German market, this will have to come from a good track record and reliable partners within 
the German market in the future (DEV_01; INV_02; HCS_03; HCS_05; HCS_06; CNS_03). 
4.3.1 Human Resources and Market Expansion 
To successfully enter the German market, TPWLOs must emphasize gaining a 
thorough understanding of local market dynamics by outsourcing to local talent with German 
market expertise. Competitors and industry experts underline the necessity of having a 
responsive and active CEO who is well-known and quick to respond to ensure smooth 
operations and growth (HSC_04). Furthermore, outsourcing HR and finance to local

equally important first stages (HSC_06).  
4.3.2 Operational and Financial Management 
Operational and financial management are critical for effective market entry. To 
ensure strong collaborations, industry experts propose developing strategic plans with two to 
three hotel chains (HSC_06). Financial talks require a focus on rent, indexation, and 
guarantees, as well as an understanding of the company's setup. Evaluating real estate options 
for conversions rather than new buildings can result in financial savings and faster market 
entry (HSC_04). A good reputation and a sustainable financial strategy are essential for 
ensuring that the operator is viewed as trustworthy and financially secure (HSC_04, 
HSC_03).

4.6 The Influence of Loyalty and Exclusivity on Project Division

taking advantage of expansion possibilities with other chains (i.e., strategic flexibility). These 
collaborations are made more difficult by the inclinations and tactical goals of hotel chains, 
which can have a range of effects depending on how much exclusivity is maintained. This 
leads to the question addressed in this subchapter: To what extent does loyalty and 
exclusivity between TPWLOs and chains influence the division of projects by the chain? 
According to a response from an investor, the loyalty and exclusivity of TPWLO and hotel 
chain agreements considerably enhance investors' comfort levels (INV_03). This reputation,

4.7 Differentiation in Secondary Conditions 
The goal is to determine the differences in secondary conditions provided to TPWLO 
models across different geographic regions and brand strategies. Key money and franchise 
agreements differ, influencing financial viability, market positioning, and overall strategic 
planning for TPWLOs. This subchapter answers the question: What are the differences in 
secondary conditions offered by chains to TPWLOs, with a focus on key money 
structures and franchise agreements? The main answer is a nuanced one. Chains offer 
varied secondary conditions to TPWLOs, with differences in key money structures often 
tailored to project-specific needs and strategic locations, while franchise agreements typically 
show less flexibility. Geographic market characteristics also influence these conditions, with 
key locations getting preferential treatment in contract types and financial arrangements. 
4.7.1 Contractual and Geographic Strategies 
Chains use a variety of contractual tactics to align with the features of specific local 
markets. For example, flat lease contracts are frequent in Germany, whereas hybrid lease 
contracts are more typical in the Netherlands (EXP_02). This, however, according to an 
investor, might be changing. Hybrid leasing contracts that include participation in turnover or 
profit share are becoming increasingly common in Germany (INV_03). This allows 
owners/investors to profit more during good economic times and dampen the weight of the 
rent during worse economic times (for a TPWLO). Examples that can have an economic 
impact are COVID and high interest rates (INV_03, CNS_03, CNS_04). Furthermore, 
strategic location has a considerable impact on contractual decisions, as seen in Amsterdam, 
where financial conditions (e.g., key money) are sharpened to capitalize on the market 

51 
 
(HCS_01). This sharpening of conditions leads to more expensive leasing contracts in A-
locations. 
4.7.2 Financial and Decision-Making Dynamics 
Financial concerns and decision-making criteria differ between chains. The 
significance of key money is highlighted for developing businesses like BLH, whose initial 
financial strength is modest (DEV_01). However, key money has a significant impact on 
contract valuation and future relationships and may lead to a worse negotiating position 
regarding franchise fees (HSC_04), considering the fact that key money is essentially a fee 
that is paid to secure the lease on a property, serving as a safeguard for the investor/owners 
that the bills are being paid. The allocation of key money is project-specific, with no set 
norm, emphasizing the importance of the geographic location (COM_02). A better 
geographic location leads to more key money and/or better terms on a lease agreement. 
When negotiating fee structures, TPWLOs need to decide how to fund an acquisition. 
When making that decision they have several options: key money, bank loan, assistance from 
shareholders (in the case of BLH this could be Sommerset) or using their own cash when they 
have solid (secured) cashflows.  
4.7.3 Market Position and Strategic Use of Key Money 
The allocation of key money is mainly determined by market position and project

franchising contracts (HCS_01). However, currently it has a limited franchising presence 
within the Netherlands. This indicates that there are region specific policies per chain, 
indicating market-specific adjustments (HCS_01). Key money is frequently granted to 
projects in strategic(-ally) (essential) areas, from chains to owners (COM_02). This stresses 
the value of location and asset attractiveness (COM_02). Comparisons across hotel operators,

Without a clear awareness of these problems, BLH risks meeting unexpected roadblocks that 
could jeopardize its growth goals. Given these complications, it is critical to address the 
following question: What are the potential challenges of forming strategic alliances? 
Understanding these problems will enable BLH to develop effective risk mitigation and 
opportunity-leveraging strategies. Below we will go into the three key aspects that need to be 
looked at when building these alliances. 
4.9.1 Operational Execution and Relationship Management

to go smoothly as trust and confidence grow (HSC_06). Scaling a business while retaining

4.9.2 Partnerships and Alliances 
Forming and managing partnerships and alliances have their own set of challenges.

4.10 Cultural Integration 
Expansion into Germany poses major cultural integration issues for TPWLOs. 
Industry experts, competitors, hotel chains, and consultants have recognized these issues as 
critical elements influencing market entry success. The formal and efficiency-focused 
communication style in the German hospitality sector necessitates TPWLOs developing trust 
by regular actions, accurate communication, and strict adherence to timetables. Furthermore, 
identifying and nurturing local talent capable of bridging cultural gaps is tough. A successful 
entry also requires strategic planning consistent with local regulations and market conditions, 
highlighting the need to develop strategic ties with local businesses. What are the risks of 
cultural integration when expanding into Germany? The rest of this subchapter explores 
five critical themes from the interviews, addressing various aspects of cultural integration 
risks and methods for successful market entry into Germany. These themes encapsulate 
BLH's fundamental areas of interest and concern. 
4.10.1 Business Communication and Relationship Building 
The first theme, Business Communication and, Relationship Building, emphasizes 
how crucial it is to understand and adjust to Germany's formal communication style. A 
diplomatic approach to communication is highly valued over direct methods, which helps in 
building trust and avoiding misunderstandings. This style is not only about being polite but 
also about being strategic in interactions to ensure that relationships are built on a solid 
foundation of mutual respect and understanding. The requirement for precise and 
unambiguous communication was highlighted by one prominent consultant from a big 

55 
 
consultancy firm who stated that "they would at least be able to give directions (...) I think 
language is key there" (CNS_03, Page 12). Naturally, you should not be that straightforward 
in communicating, according to the advice of another interviewee; you should adopt a more 
diplomatic stance in your dealings (CNS_03). Regarding relationship building, another 
consultant said, "building trust takes time, and you need to show consistency in your actions" 
(CNS_04, Page 15) and that "building trust is a gradual process that requires consistent and 
reliable actions". 
4.10.2 Cultural Competence and Adaptation 
The second subject, Cultural Competence and Adaptation emphasizes the need for 
cultural sensitivity as well as the ability to change business practices to adhere to the formal, 
efficiency-driven ideology of German hospitality culture. Success in this market is heavily 
dependent on adapting to these cultural norms to develop strong partnerships. A professional 
in the field said, "I would say that the German hospitality culture is more formal and focused 
on efficiency" (EXP_02, Page 1). This methodical and effective strategy necessitates 
adaptability and flexibility from firms. The expectation for formal interaction is not just a 
preference but a critical component of everyday business operations, reflecting the country's 
broader cultural emphasis on order and precision. "I think if you have the sensitivity to be 
able to adapt to the German style of doing business, you'll do fine," said a hotel chain 
representative (HCS_03, Pages 8-9). Success in the German market also requires flexibility 
and the willingness to adjust strategies to meet local demands. The market is dynamic, and 
being too stiff can be a disadvantage. Another interviewee emphasized the significance of 
adaptability, saying, "Adaptability is essential. You must be prepared to modify your plans in 
response to local demands" (CNS_03, Page 3). Companies that can pivot and adapt to 
changing circumstances or customer needs are more likely to succeed.

Next, the requirement for high levels of Operational Excellence in Germany is the 
third theme. "German operations expect a high level of precision and adherence to 
schedules," said an industry expert with more than 20 years of experience (EXP_03, Page 2). 
There is a high expectation for operational precision and adherence to schedules. Careful 
planning and execution are required to meet the German standards of business operations. 
This extends beyond simple efficiency and into every aspect of business operations, from 
project planning to daily management and customer service. This is a high requirement that 
requires careful preparation and implementation. "If we sign a deal, great," said another 
consultant. "If we don't sign it yet, we keep working towards it," demonstrating the 
dedication needed to live up to these high standards (CNS_03, Page 8). 
4.10.4 Organizational Human Resources Management 
The fourth theme, Organizational Human Resources Management, addresses the 
challenge of finding and training the right talent who can bridge the company's culture with 
local market expectations. Finding and training the right talent who understands both the 
local market and the company's culture is a significant challenge. Local talent not only brings 
knowledge of the local business environment but also carries cultural insights that can bridge 
any gaps that might exist between a foreign company and local business practices. As one HR 
manager pointed out, "Finding the right talent who understands both the local market and 
your company's culture is a challenge" (HCS_01, Page 5). This challenge is compounded by 
the need to train staff to meet the high standards expected in Germany, as another HR 
manager noted (HCS_02).

4.10.5 Strategic Market Entry and Expansion 
The fifth and final theme, Strategic Market Entry and Expansion, emphasizes the need 
for a well-defined strategic plan that considers local regulations and market conditions 
(INV_01). Forming partnerships with local firms can help market entry and help navigate 
local business regulations and practices. These partnerships can provide valuable local 
insights, ease logistical challenges (when relevant), and enhance credibility in a new market. 
Additionally, forming strategic partnerships with local firms can significantly ease the entry 
process and help navigate the local business landscape, as another strategic investor stated, 
"Partnerships with local firms can ease the entry process and help navigate the local business 
landscape" (INV_02, Page 8).

5.3 Risk Assessment 
When looking at the management question through this component the focus lays on 
the risks of expanding to Germany. When aiming for growth and expansion risks are

indicators that signify ‘unhealthy’ growth, such as not having enough resources for the

can avoid hiring the wrong people, that are potentially unqualified due to lack of cultural 
adaptations. Furthermore, it is important to maintain a balance between the open and 
pipelined hotels, to ensure proper cashflow (HCS_06). Here it is also important to mention

perceptions of the relevant stakeholders. To minimize risks, market knowledge is highly 
requisite, making outsourcing of local talent highly advisable.

Chapter 6. Limitations & Recommendations for Further Research 
This chapter discusses limitations and suggests avenues for further research, 
acknowledging new boundaries and constraints essential for rigorous study (AJE & Viera, 
2024).  
Constraints arose from the limited diversity and scarcity of available secondary 
research. This, coupled with the fast-evolving TPWLO market in Germany, resulted in a 
moderate base of secondary sources. The out-run is that primary research is based on 
interviews conducted with stakeholders. 
Our sampling methods (e.g., snowball and purposive sampling) led to a limited 
sample size, as we specifically targeted relevant stakeholders (Developers, Industry Experts, 
Competitors, Investors, Hotel Chains and Consultants). By limiting the interviews of this 
specified group, the potential sample size decreases, leading to the risk of too few 
respondents. Specifically, the number of interviews with Investors, Developers and 
Competitors fell short due to the lack of response (Scriptium, 2022). Also, one of the big six 
hotel chains was not interviewed due to a lack of response. 
The current research acknowledges limitations due to inherent subjectivity and 
potentially unrepresentative data. To address this in future studies, interviewing participants 
until no new information appears, indicating data saturation and a potentially representative 
sample is needed. Beyond data saturation, more research is needed to understand the 
effectiveness of A and B versus B and C locations. Analyze factors like brand awareness 
uplift in A and B locations versus cost benefits of B and C locations. Furthermore, research 
into the long-stay market is recommended. This could involve analyzing guest 
demographics, preferences, and growth projections for this specific niche.  

67 
 
The fast-evolving TPWLO market in Germany needs further research to ensure 
findings remain current and informative for future strategies. This includes expanding 
primary research to gather data from under-represented stakeholders, such as 
Investors, Developers, Competitors. Assessing the generalizability of the current results 
ensures a broader perspective of the market landscape.  
By conducting further research in these areas, BLH can refine its strategy to ensure a 
successful white-label positioning in the German TPWLO market.
