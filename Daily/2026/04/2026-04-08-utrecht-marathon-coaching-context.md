# 5. Recovery & Mobility Protocol
All protocols are mandatory, not optional. The daily PT exercises are the single most important injury-prevention measure in this plan.

## 5.1 Dynamic warm-up (before every run — 5-7 min)
Glute bridges — 10 reps (activates VMO and glutes, directly protects patellofemoral joint)
Leg swings forward + lateral — 15 each side
Hip circles — 10 each direction
Walking lunges with torso rotation — 8 each side
High knees — 2x20m
Butt kicks — 2x20m
A-skips — 2x20m
3x30 seconds easy jog to finish

## 5.2 Post-run static stretching (after every run — 10 min)
Hip flexor lunge stretch — 60s each side (most critical for runners knee management)
Standing quad stretch — 45s each side
Seated hamstring stretch — 60s each side
Calf/Achilles wall stretch — 60s each side (two variants: straight and bent knee for soleus)
Figure-4 glute stretch — 60s each side
Pigeon pose — 60s each side (IT band + hip capsule)
Thoracic rotation — 10 reps each side

## 5.3 Daily runners knee PT (every day without exception — 5 min)
Patellofemoral syndrome is driven by weakness in the hip abductors and VMO underactivation. These exercises are the root-cause fix, not symptom management.
Terminal knee extensions (TKE with resistance band) — 3x20 each side. Directly isolates VMO. Do before every run.
Side-lying clam shells (with resistance band) — 3x15. Hip abductor strength is the primary fix for runners knee.
VMO squats (terminal 30 degrees of extension only) — 3x15. Short-arc quad work targeting the inner quad.
Single-leg glute bridge — 3x12 each side. Glute strength prevents valgus knee collapse during stance phase.
Can be done watching TV. Non-negotiable every day including rest days.

## 5.4 Foam rolling (nightly — 5 min)
IT band — 60s each side (slow, 2 seconds per roll, pause on tender spots)
Quadriceps — 60s each side
Hip flexors — 45s each side
Calves — 45s each side
Thoracic spine — 30s

Focus on posterior chain and hip stability. Protect the knee, build running economy, prevent late-race form breakdown. Reduce volume in weeks 5-6, maintenance only in weeks 7-8.

# 7. Race Day Protocol — May 31, 2026


## 7.1 Pre-race nutrition
3 hours before gun: 800-900 kcal carb-rich meal — white rice or oats + banana + white bread with honey. Minimal fibre and fat. 500 ml water.
45-60 min before gun: 250 mg caffeine. No new foods.
10-15 min before gun: 150 ml water, half a gel optional.

## 7.2 In-race nutrition (target 60-90g carbs/hour)
First gel at km 7-8 (approximately 35-40 min). Do not wait until depleted.
Then every 35-40 min: km 14, 20, 26, 32, 37. Total: 5-6 gels.
Chase every gel with water (not sports drink — avoid gut overload).
Salt tablet or electrolyte drink at km 21 to prevent late-race cramping.
One caffeinated gel at km 28-30 for the final push.
Only use gels practised with in training. Zero new products on race day.

## 7.3 Pacing strategy
0-10 km: feel easy, hold back. HR 145-152. Do not chase the front.
10-21 km: settle into goal pace 4:37/km. HR 148-157. Find a comfortable rhythm.
21-30 km: assessment zone. If HR above 162, back off 3-5 sec/km. If steady, hold pace.
30-35 km: where races are won or lost. Stay relaxed, shorten stride, maintain cadence.
35-42 km: give everything remaining. Push HR to 165-172.
Key rule: do not cross 21 km faster than 1:37:00 regardless of how good you feel.

## 7.4 Carb loading (Thursday-Saturday before race)
Target: 8-10g carbs per kg bodyweight = 576-720g carbs per day for 2 days.
Focus: white rice, white pasta, white bread, bananas, sports drinks.
Avoid: high-fibre foods, cruciferous vegetables, anything new or unusual.
Expect 1-2 kg bodyweight increase (glycogen + water) — correct and intentional.
No alcohol from Monday through race day.

# 9. Coaching Instructions for AI Performance Coach
This section contains specific guidance for any AI coaching assistant using this document as a context file.

## 9.1 What to monitor each week
Weekly running volume vs plan — flag if more than 10% deviation in either direction.
Long run completion and how the MP segments felt (HR, effort, pace).
Knee status — any pain beyond mild stiffness in first 10 minutes is a red flag.
Sleep quality and morning resting HR trends.
Body weight every Monday — target 70.5 kg by race week.
Whether PT exercises (TKE, clam shells, VMO squats) are being done daily.

## 9.2 Red flags requiring plan modification
Knee pain that persists beyond the first 10-15 minutes of a run — cut session, substitute with pool running or cycling.
Resting HR elevated more than 7-8 bpm above normal — skip quality session, replace with easy run.
Body weight dropping below 70 kg — increase daily caloric intake to maintenance, stop deficit.
Cumulative fatigue: pace at Zone 2 HR degrading by more than 15 sec/km vs week 1 baselines.
Any new injury symptom — modify plan conservatively rather than pushing through.

## 9.3 Athlete characteristics to keep in mind
Works in real estate asset management — high-pressure job, stress is a real training variable.
Very disciplined and data-driven — responds well to specific numbers and clear rationale.
Trains early morning (4:30-6:00am) or evening depending on schedule — flexible.
Has a history of pushing hard — needs reminders to keep easy runs truly easy.
Identified as ambitious and driven — will need restraint coaching during taper (taper madness risk).
Lives in Utrecht with flat terrain — good for marathon-pace training, limited hills.

## 9.4 Key performance prediction context
Current Riegel prediction from best HM-distance effort: 3:22:15. The sub-3:15 goal requires closing 7 minutes over the full marathon distance (approximately 10 sec/km).
