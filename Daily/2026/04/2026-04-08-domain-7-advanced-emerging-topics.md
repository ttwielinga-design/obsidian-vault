# Domain 7: Advanced & Emerging Topics
### Evidence synthesized April 2026 | Sources: 2020–2025 peer-reviewed literature

## 7.1 Heat & Cold Adaptation

### Evidence Summary
**Passive heat acclimation via post-exercise sauna** is a highly practical tool for recreational runners. A 2021 PMC study (PMC7862510) demonstrated that **3 weeks of post-exercise sauna bathing significantly improved running performance in trained middle-distance runners** in both hot and temperate conditions. Key adaptations include: increased plasma volume, enhanced sweat response, reduced exercise heart rate, and lower resting core temperature. A 2024 systematic review and meta-analysis (Springer BMC Sports Sci 2024) confirmed post-exercise passive heat exposure produces meaningful performance improvements with a low time investment. Even in temperate race conditions like the Netherlands (Amsterdam/Utrecht), heat adaptations provide benefit through **plasma volume expansion** (equivalent to a natural EPO-like effect).

### Sauna Acclimatization Protocol

| Week | Frequency | Duration | Timing | Temperature |
|------|-----------|----------|--------|-------------|
| Week 1 | 3×/week | 10–12 min | Post-easy run | 75–80°C |
| Week 2 | 4×/week | 15 min | Post-easy run | 80°C |
| Week 3 | 4–5×/week | 15–20 min | Post-easy run | 80–85°C |
| Maintenance | 2–3×/week | 15–20 min | Post-easy run | 80°C |

**Important rules:**
- Sauna ONLY after easy Z1 sessions — never after hard quality work (parasympathetic overload risk)
- Rehydrate with 500 ml electrolyte water afterward
- Begin protocol ≥6 weeks before target race for full adaptation

**Live High/Train Low (LHLT) for Recreational Runners:**
- Requires altitude camp at ≥2,000 m for ≥3 weeks (minimum)
- VO2max improvement possible: +3–5% through EPO-driven red blood cell increase
- **Practical verdict:** Cost-prohibitive for most; 3-week sauna protocol achieves ~50% of the plasma volume benefit without travel

### Heat Adjustment on Race Day

| Temperature | Pace Adjustment | Action |
|-------------|----------------|--------|
| 10–15°C | Optimal — no change | Race at target pace |
| 16–20°C | +5–8 sec/km | Adjust pace target before race |
| 21–25°C | +10–15 sec/km | Accept performance limitation |
| >25°C | +20+ sec/km | Focus on effort, not pace; heavy hydration priority |

**Cold weather running (below 5°C):**
- Expect +3–5 sec/km slower at equal perceived effort (muscle temperature effect)
- Warm-up 15 min before race start
- Layer up for runs below 0°C — core temperature regulation consumes energy

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Going into sauna immediately after hard intervals — dangerous
- Doing only one week of heat acclimatization and expecting full adaptation
- Racing at target pace in 25°C heat → catastrophic blowup is near-certain

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- PMC7862510 (2021) — sauna bathing in trained middle-distance runners
- Springer BMC Sports Sci 2024 — post-exercise heat exposure meta-analysis
- Racinais et al. (2015) — consensus recommendations on heat training

## 7.2 Carbon Plate Shoe Science

### Evidence Summary
The evidence on carbon-plated shoes is the **strongest technological performance enhancement available in running history**. Peer-reviewed research consistently shows **2–4% improvement in running economy** (oxygen cost) vs. traditional racing flats, with the Nike Vaporfly 4% and subsequent models showing consistent replication across labs. Performance improvements are **pace-dependent**: greatest effect at fast paces (>14 km/hr, i.e., sub-4:17/km) and reducing at slower paces. For Thomas (sub-3:30 target, ~4:57/km pace), expected benefit: **2–3% improvement in RE**, which translates to **4–5 minutes off marathon time** when comparing to a conventional racing flat. Key mechanisms remain incompletely understood — foam energy return, plate stiffness, stack height all contribute but no single factor explains the full effect.

### Carbon Shoe Evidence Table

| Pace / Marathon Time | Expected RE Improvement | Time Saving (42.2 km) |
|---------------------|------------------------|----------------------|
| <3:30 (>12.0 km/hr) | 2.7–4.2% | 5–7 minutes |
| 3:30–4:00 (10.5–12 km/hr) | 1.5–2.7% | 3–5 minutes |
| 4:00–4:30 (9.4–10.5 km/hr) | 0.9–1.5% | 2–3 minutes |
| >4:30 (<9.4 km/hr) | ~0.5–1.0% | <2 minutes |

### Practical Guidance

| Question | Evidence-Based Answer |
|----------|----------------------|
| Use in training or only racing? | Primarily racing + 2–3 key race-specific sessions per cycle; daily training wears out the foam (~500 km lifespan) |
| Which shoe? | Nike Vaporfly, Alphafly; Adidas Adizero Adios Pro; Asics Metaspeed — all within 0.5% of each other in head-to-head tests |
| Break-in required? | Yes — wear for 2 easy runs and 1 quality session before race use |
| Injury risk? | Higher calf and Achilles loading in early use; transition gradually if switching from low-stack shoes |
| Should everyone use them? | Yes — for time-crunched recreational runners, carbon shoes are the best legal performance tool available |

### Lower Leg Injury Risk

A 2025 PMC study (PMC11850346) found that different carbon fiber plate shapes alter lower limb biomechanics, particularly under fatigue. Key finding: fatigue alters load distribution in carbon shoes in ways not seen in conventional shoes, potentially increasing Achilles and plantar load in the late stages of a marathon. **Recommendation:** Ensure strong eccentric calf capacity before using carbon shoes for long runs.

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Using carbon shoes for daily training → foam breaks down in 400–500 km
- Switching to carbon shoes 1 week before race without any adaptation runs
- Assuming carbon shoes fix bad form — they amplify existing biomechanics, good or bad
- Buying based on brand/celebrity endorsement rather than testing fit for your foot type

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- PMC12464892 (2025) — persistent RE improvements with advanced footwear
- Preprints.org (2025) — comprehensive carbon shoe mechanisms review
- PMC11850346 (2025) — carbon plate shape and lower limb biomechanics under fatigue

## 7.3 Gut Training

### Evidence Summary
The gut is **highly adaptable** — carbohydrate absorption capacity can be increased through systematic training, allowing runners to absorb closer to **90 g CHO/hr** without GI distress (a level causing nausea and diarrhea in untrained athletes). The mechanism involves upregulation of intestinal SGLT1 glucose transporters and enhanced gastric emptying rate (Jeukendrup 2017; confirmed in 2023 systematic review, Springer Sports Med). A 2025 PMC systematic review (PMC12258207) found that **nutritional strategies reducing GI distress** are effective, with low-FODMAP pre-race diet and progressive gut training producing the best outcomes. Gut training takes **4–6 weeks of consistent practice** to produce meaningful adaptation. GI distress affects ~30–50% of marathon runners — gut training is a race-day multiplier.

### Gut Training Protocol (Starting 6 Weeks Out)

| Week | CHO Intake During Training Run | Session Length | Target |
|------|-------------------------------|----------------|--------|
| Week 1–2 | 30–40 g/hr (1 gel per 30–35 min) | 60–90 min | Baseline tolerance |
| Week 3–4 | 45–60 g/hr (every 25 min) | 90–120 min | Building transporter density |
| Week 5–6 | 60–75 g/hr (every 20 min, mixed sugars) | 100–150 min | Race-like loading |
| Race Day | 70–90 g/hr (every 18–20 min) | 3:28 | Trained absorption |

**Key Rules for Gut Training:**
1. Use your **actual race-day products** — not different gels or foods
2. Practice at **race pace**, not easier — gut blood flow changes at different intensities
3. Drink water (not sports drink) with gels to avoid sugar stacking in the gut
4. Keep a GI log — note bloating, nausea, urgency after each session

### GI Distress Prevention Protocol

| Risk Factor | Prevention Strategy |
|------------|---------------------|
| High-fibre pre-race meal | Switch to low-fibre 48 hrs before race (white rice, white bread, cooked vegetables) |
| New gel brand on race day | Never — use only practiced products |
| Dehydration increasing gut permeability | Maintain hydration strategy throughout training |
| Anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) pre-race | Avoid ibuprofen/aspirin pre-race → increases gut permeability significantly |
| Excessive caffeine | Stick to trained caffeine dose — excess increases gut motility → urgency |
| Anxiety/stress | Mental preparation routine reduces stress hormones → reduced gut motility disruption |

**Low-FODMAP Pre-Race (48 hrs before race):**
- Avoid: apples, pears, watermelon, garlic, onion, beans, lentils, wheat in large amounts
- Good choices: white rice, potatoes, bananas (unripe), eggs, chicken, firm tofu, lactose-free options

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Zero gut training and expecting 90 g/hr on race day → almost guaranteed GI distress
- Practicing with gels but on easy 45-min runs — doesn't simulate race gut stress
- Taking NSAIDs (ibuprofen) the morning of a race — extremely common and very risky

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- Jeukendrup (2017, Br J Sports Med) — gut training for endurance athletes
- PMC12258207 (2025) — GI distress prevention in endurance exercise
- Sports Med 2023 — "The Effect of Gut-Training on GI Status" systematic review (Springer)
