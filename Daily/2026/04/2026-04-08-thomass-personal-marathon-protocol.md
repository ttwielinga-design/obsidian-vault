# Thomas's Personal Marathon Protocol
### Top 20 Evidence-Based Rules | Sub-3:30 | April 2026

---

*These are the highest-leverage, most-supported rules distilled from 7 domains of current sports science research. Internalize these — they compound.*

---

## TRAINING

**Rule 1 — Run easy, actually easy.**
At least 75–80% of your weekly km must be in Z1 (6:00–7:30/km, fully conversational). The biggest mistake recreational runners make is running easy runs too fast. This kills recovery, blunts adaptation, and wastes adaptation budget on the wrong stimulus. Check: if you can't hold a full sentence, slow down.

**Rule 2 — One quality session, one long run, everything else easy.**
Each week has two "hard" sessions max: one quality workout (interval or threshold) and one long run. Every other run is Z1. Piling on Z3 tempo work on easy days is the grey zone trap — it exhausts without adapting.

**Rule 3 — Protect your long run pace.**
The long run should be Z1–Z2 the whole way, except planned MP (marathon pace) segments. Running your long run at Z3 pace turns it into a race, not a training stimulus. The goal is time-on-feet and fat oxidation adaptation, not suffering.

**Rule 4 — Volume is the primary adaptation driver.**
Monthly km count is the single strongest predictor of marathon time in the research. Everything else (quality sessions, supplements, gear) amplifies the base. Protect your volume — don't skip easy days. Even 8 km adds up to 56 km extra per week vs. skipping.

**Rule 5 — Down week every 3–4 weeks, no exceptions.**
Supercompensation requires you to cut volume 20–30% every 3–4 weeks. This is not laziness — it's when adaptation happens. Fight the urge to keep building.

---

## PHYSIOLOGY

**Rule 6 — Threshold training is non-negotiable.**
LT2 (lactate threshold 2) is the strongest predictor of marathon performance in trained runners. One threshold session per week — 20–40 min at 4:40–4:50/km — will deliver more marathon improvement than any supplement, shoe, or gadget. Never skip it.

**Rule 7 — One VO2max session per week.**
4×4 min at 90–95% HRmax (can't speak, RPE 8–9) produces 7.2% VO2max gain in 16 weeks. More than one session/week is counterproductive for recreational runners — parasympathetic suppression kicks in. Trust one quality VO2max workout done well.

**Rule 8 — Strength twice a week in base phase (heavy, not light).**
Light weights with high reps do nothing useful for runners. Heavy squats, Romanian deadlifts, and hip thrusts at ≥80% 1RM improve running economy by 2–4%. Neural adaptations, not muscle mass — load heavy and move well.

---

## NUTRITION

**Rule 9 — Fuel your runs, always.**
For any run over 75 min: 40–60 g of carbohydrate per hour from the first 30 min. Never run a long run or quality session depleted unless it's a deliberate easy Z1 "train low" session. Glycogen-depleted intervals produce sub-par adaptation and injury risk.

**Rule 10 — Race nutrition starts at km 7–8, not km 20.**
The glycogen tank depletes regardless of whether you feel it yet. Take your first gel at km 7–8 and every 20–25 min thereafter. Aim for 70–80 g CHO/hr with glucose+fructose gels. Gut train this exact protocol every long run from week 5 onward.

**Rule 11 — Carb load 3 days before the race.**
8–12 g/kg/day × 3 days = full glycogen saturation. For 75 kg: 600–900 g CHO/day. This is a lot of food. Use familiar sources. Do not cut calories during taper — maintain intake, just shift to carbohydrate-dominant.

**Rule 12 — Caffeine is your legal performance drug.**
225–300 mg, taken 45–60 min before race start = 1–3% time improvement. Non-negotiable race-day tool. Practice this timing in 1–2 key long training runs. Reduce caffeine intake in the 5 days before to reduce tolerance.

**Rule 13 — Test ferritin every training cycle.**
Iron deficiency is the hidden epidemic in runners. Sub-30 µg/L ferritin = significantly impaired oxygen transport = unexplained fatigue and inability to hit training paces. Test before every marathon build cycle. Supplement with vitamin C if iron is marginal.

---

## RECOVERY

**Rule 14 — Sleep is the single highest-ROI recovery tool.**
8–9 hours per night during training blocks. No supplement, no modality, no protocol compensates for chronic 6-hour nights. If you only change one recovery habit, make it sleep. Build up to 9 hours starting 7–10 days before your race.

**Rule 15 — HRV guides the grey area days.**
On clear-green HRV days: train as planned. On amber/red days: downgrade to easy run or rest. This is not soft — it's the difference between functional overreaching (planned) and non-functional overreaching (injury, illness, stale legs). Use Oura Ring or WHOOP.

**Rule 16 — Avoid ice baths during training blocks; save them for races.**
Cold water immersion post-training may blunt mitochondrial adaptation signals. Use it after races and isolated recovery emergencies. During base and build phases, active recovery (easy Z1 run the day after) is superior to cold.

**Rule 17 — Sauna 3–5 times per week after easy runs.**
15–20 min at 80°C post-Z1 run: expands plasma volume (natural altitude effect), improves cardiovascular efficiency, accelerates recovery. Start 8+ weeks before target race. Stop 4 weeks out to reduce accumulated heat stress.

---

## RACE EXECUTION

**Rule 18 — Go out slower than feels comfortable.**
First 5 km: 5:05–5:10/km (5–10 sec/km slower than goal). It will feel embarrassingly easy. This is correct. Every 10 sec/km faster in km 0–5 costs you 20–30 sec/km at km 35+. Negative split to the finish.

**Rule 19 — Prepare your brain as seriously as your legs.**
Write "if-then" plans for your hardest moments: "If I feel terrible at km 30, THEN I will shorten my stride, focus on arm drive, and count to 100 before considering slowing." Visualize the full race — including suffering — in the week before. Self-talk mantras practiced in training, not improvised during the race.

**Rule 20 — Taper 3 weeks, trust the process.**
3-week taper with 40–60% volume cut saves a median 5.5 minutes in studies of 158,000+ recreational runners. Phantom injuries, flat legs, and low energy in the taper are nearly universal and mean nothing. Do not add extra runs. Sleep more. Eat more carbs. Race ready.
