The Art of Voluntary Struggle
There I was, running in the dawn light. It was 7 am on a Saturday. The whole city was sleeping off their Friday night hangovers, and what was I doing? I was running in the crisp morning air, suffering during my 5k tempo run while the rest of the city was probably face-down in their pillows, dreaming about some breakfast pancakes. But here's the thing: I wasn't doing this because I had to. I was doing it because I got to.
That shift in language sounds like motivational poster bullshit, right? The kind of thing you'd see in some corporate wellness seminar. But I've been obsessing over this distinction lately, and it's revealed something I can't unsee: the suffering you choose is fundamentally different from the suffering that chooses you.
The Privilege of Chosen Pain
Here's what I'm not talking about: trauma, loss, the kind of pain that breaks people. I'm talking about the deliberate discomfort of discipline. The burn in your legs during the last mile of a run. The mental resistance when you sit down to write instead of scrolling. The cold shock of that ice bath.
This is suffering as privilege. Because here's what nobody talks about: having the option to choose your pain means you're already in a position most people would kill for. You have enough stability to pursue growth. Enough safety to take risks. Enough health to push your body.
Remember that panic attack I had on the train to Valencia? Heart racing at 97 bpm while I'm supposed to be relaxing on vacation? That was involuntary suffering. My nervous system doing math I didn't ask it to do, ambushing me with time's finite nature. But what came after, the decision to actually do something about that awareness, that was voluntary. I could have let that anxiety paralyze me, or I could have used it as fuel.
I chose fuel. And that choice made all the difference.
Comfort Is the Real Cage
You know what's interesting? The same restlessness I wrote about in "Everything's Going Right So Why Do I Feel Restless" is actually a signal. Your nervous system knows something your conscious mind is trying to ignore: comfort is eating you alive.
When everything's humming along nicely, when the career is solid and the relationship is good and the house is paid for, there's this seductive voice that whispers: "Just maintain this. Don't rock the boat, don't fuck it up." And that voice, friendly as it sounds, is trying to kill the part of you that's capable of more.
I landed a dream job at 24. Got my own place. Should have felt like a massive win, right? And it was. But it also created its own trap. Suddenly, I had something to protect. A comfortable routine. Predictable weekends. The golden cage from that restlessness I wrote about.
The cage isn't made of bars. It's made of bubble wrap. Layers and layers of comfort insulating you from anything that might challenge or change you. And here's the fucked up part: you don't even realize you're trapped until you've been in there long enough that breaking out feels terrifying.
That's when voluntary suffering becomes not just useful, but necessary. You need to choose discomfort before comfort chooses your entire life for you. Here's the thing, though: you can't just think your way out of the comfort trap. You have to language your way out.
Reframing the Language of Discipline
"I have to work out" versus "I get to test myself."
Same action. Completely different relationship to it.
When I have to do something, it's an obligation. A burden. Something to check off and forget about. But when I get to do something, it becomes an opportunity. A privilege. Something that adds to my life instead of draining from it.
Two and a half years ago, I made myself a stupid-simple promise: show up once per day. Didn't matter what that looked like. Some days I felt like shit and literally just forced myself to walk around the block. I wasn't thinking about it as "getting to" do anything. I was full-on forcing. Treating discipline like punishment for not being good enough yet.
But somewhere along the way, the frame shifted. Those days when I felt terrible and just walked to the mailbox? Those weren't failures. They were choices. I was choosing to maintain my identity as someone who shows up, even when showing up looked pathetic.
The frame you put around your suffering determines whether it destroys you or builds you. That's it. That's the whole fucking game.
Last Tuesday morning, I'm at kilometer 3 of my tempo run. My legs are screaming. My watch says I'm supposed to hold this pace for two more kilometers. And my brain starts its usual bullshit: "Why are you doing this? You could be in bed right now. This is stupid."
But then the frame shifts. I'm not enduring this. I'm testing whether I can push past the voice that says stop. That's different. That's voluntary.
Moving Forward Without Comfort
At 24, I'm learning that maybe the point isn't to make life easier. Maybe the point is to make yourself more capable of handling difficulty. Not because difficulty is noble, but because difficulty is inevitable. And the version of you that emerges from chosen struggle is fundamentally different from the version that emerges from avoided discomfort.
The restlessness I feel? I'm starting to see it as my inner child, the one I wrote about in "Those Fleeting Glimpses," asking a simple question: "Are we playing it safe, or are we playing for real?"
That kid who built things and tested limits before life taught him to stay comfortable. He's still in there. And he's bored as fuck with maintenance mode. The answer to his question determines everything.
So here's what I'm doing: every day, I'm looking for one thing I can choose to make harder. One conversation I'd rather avoid but will have anyway. One workout that feels too challenging but I'll attempt. One creative risk that might fail but I'll take.
Not because I hate comfort. But because I've tasted both kinds of suffering, and I know which one I can live with.
The suffering you choose builds you. The suffering you avoid buries you.
And here's the most beautiful part: you always get to pick. Even right now. Especially right now.
So what's your voluntary struggle going to be today?
