# Domain 2: Physiological Performance Metrics
### Evidence synthesized April 2026 | Sources: 2020–2025 peer-reviewed literature

---

## 2.1 VO2max Development

### Evidence Summary
VO2max, lactate threshold, and running economy together explain the majority of marathon performance variance — with **LT and running economy arguably more trainable and more predictive** than VO2max alone in already-trained runners (Joyner model; Frontiers Physiology 2025). For improving VO2max, high-intensity aerobic intervals are clearly superior to easy running: **4×4 min at 90–95% HRmax produced a 7.2% VO2max gain** vs ~3% for steady easy running (Helgerud et al.; confirmed PMC12218014, 2025). The optimal interval protocol for VO2max sits around **140 seconds work / 165 seconds recovery** based on meta-regression (PMC11022784, 2024). For a trained 25-year-old, a **realistic 16-week VO2max gain is 4–8%** with consistent HIIT.

### VO2max Protocol Table

| Method | Protocol | Frequency | Expected VO2max Gain (16 wks) |
|--------|----------|-----------|-------------------------------|
| 4×4 min @ 90–95% HRmax | 4 reps, 3 min recovery jog | 1×/week | +5–7% |
| 30/30s (Billat) | 30s @ vVO2max, 30s jog × 12–20 | 1×/week | +4–6% |
| 40/20s | 40s hard, 20s float × 15–20 | 1×/week | +4–5% |
| Easy volume alone | Zone 1 running | Daily | +2–3% |

**Field VO2max Estimation for Thomas:**
- **Cooper test (12-min run):** VO2max ≈ (distance in metres − 504.9) / 44.73
- **Garmin estimate:** Validated within ±5 ml/kg/min for trained runners; use as trend data, not absolute
- **Race-time estimate:** VO2max ≈ 0.1894 + 0.3328 × speed (m/min) from Daniels formula

**VO2max vs. Other Determinants in Marathon Performance:**

| Factor | % Variance Explained | Trainability (trained runner) |
|--------|---------------------|-------------------------------|
| VO2max | 25–35% | Moderate (+5–8%) |
| Lactate Threshold (%VO2max) | 40–50% | High (+10–15%) |
| Running Economy | 20–30% | High (strength + plyos) |

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Doing VO2max sessions at the wrong intensity — they should be genuinely hard (RPE 8–9)
- More than 1 HIIT session/week for recreational runners → parasympathetic suppression
- Confusing Garmin VO2max estimates with lab-level precision

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- Helgerud et al. (2007) — the definitive 4×4 study
- PMC12218014 (2025) — systematic review comparing all interval formats
- Joyner & Coyle (2008) — the endurance performance determinants model

## 2.2 Lactate Threshold Optimization

### Evidence Summary
Lactate threshold (LT2, corresponding to ~4 mmol/L blood lactate or "onset of blood lactate accumulation") is **the strongest single predictor of marathon performance** in trained runners — it dictates the pace you can sustain for 3–4 hours. LT1 (~2 mmol/L) approximates marathon race pace for sub-elite runners. Recent accuracy research shows **speed-derived field estimates** have the smallest error for predicting LT1 and LT2 without lab testing (European Journal of Applied Physiology, 2025). Threshold training can shift LT2 pace by **10–25 sec/km over a 12–16 week block**, which translates to massive marathon time improvements.

### LT Field Test Methods (No Lab Required)

| Method | How to Do It | Accuracy | LT Estimate |
|--------|-------------|----------|-------------|
| Talk test | LT1 = last pace where you can hold full conversation | Simple; ±10% | LT1 |
| 30-min time trial | Average pace from min 10–30 of all-out 30-min effort ≈ LT2 | ±5% | LT2 |
| 10K race pace formula | LT2 pace ≈ 10K race pace + 15–20 sec/km | ±3–5% | LT2 |
| HR deflection (Conconi) | HR plateaus before max = LT2 | Moderate | LT2 |
| Breathing-based (VT2) | Ventilatory threshold where breathing becomes laboured | Good with practice | LT2 |

**Thomas's LT2 Estimation (working from sub-3:30 goal = 4:58/km race pace):**
- LT2 pace target: ~4:35–4:45/km
- LT1 pace: ~5:10–5:25/km (should feel like comfortable marathon rhythm)

### Threshold Training Methods

| Method | Protocol | Duration | Evidence Rating |
|--------|----------|----------|-----------------|
| Continuous tempo run | Steady LT2 pace run | 20–40 min | ★★★★★ (gold standard) |
| Cruise intervals | 4–6 × 8–10 min @ LT2, 2 min recovery | 40–60 min total | ★★★★★ |
| Sub-threshold steady-state | 45–75 min @ LT1 | Long runs | ★★★★☆ |
| Short threshold intervals | 8–12 × 3 min @ LT2+5sec, 90s jog | 35–50 min | ★★★★☆ |

**How threshold changes over a 16-week block:**
- Weeks 1–4: Establish baseline, 1× threshold session/week (20 min)
- Weeks 5–8: Progress to 30–35 min continuous or cruise intervals
- Weeks 9–12: Peak threshold work (40 min continuous or 5×10 min)
- Weeks 13–16: Race-specific MP work replaces pure threshold volume

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Running threshold sessions too fast (becomes VO2max work → different adaptation)
- Running threshold sessions too slow (feels easy, no LT stimulus)
- Doing threshold work when accumulated fatigue is high — adaptation is blunted

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- Faude et al. (2009) — lactate threshold review, gold standard reference
- European J Applied Physiology (2025) — field test accuracy comparison
- Billat et al. on vLT and training prescription

## 2.3 Running Economy

### Evidence Summary
Running economy (RE) — the oxygen cost of running at a given speed — is highly trainable and explains **20–30% of marathon performance variance among runners with similar VO2max** (Joyner & Coyle, 2008). Heavy strength training (≥85% 1RM, lower body) is the most consistently evidence-backed RE improver, with **2–4% improvements in 8–12 weeks** (multiple meta-analyses). Carbon-plated shoes improve RE by **1.4–4.2%** depending on pace (more effective at >14 km/hr = sub-4:17/km pace). The **180 spm cadence target** is oversimplified — research supports a **5–10% increase from individual baseline** to reduce overstriding and injury risk, not a universal target.

### Running Economy Intervention Table

| Intervention | Effect Size (RE improvement) | Evidence Level | Duration to Effect |
|-------------|------------------------------|---------------|-------------------|
| Heavy strength training (2×/week) | 2–4% RE improvement | ★★★★★ | 8–12 weeks |
| Plyometric training (2×/week) | 1–3% RE improvement | ★★★★☆ | 6–10 weeks |
| Carbon-plated race shoes | 1.4–4.2% at race speeds | ★★★★★ | Immediate |
| Cadence increase (+5–10%) | 0.5–2% RE improvement | ★★★★☆ | 4–8 weeks |
| Altitude training (live high/train low) | 2–4% via plasma volume | ★★★★☆ | 3–4 weeks minimum |
| Easy running volume (long runs) | Gradual, cumulative | ★★★☆☆ | Months |

### Biomechanical Targets for RE

| Metric | Evidence-Based Target | How to Measure |
|--------|----------------------|----------------|
| Cadence | Your baseline + 5–10% (not a fixed 180) | Garmin/watch |
| Vertical oscillation | <8 cm | Garmin running dynamics pod |
| Ground contact time | <250 ms at threshold pace | Stryd pod / Garmin pod |
| Forward lean | Slight — ~5–10° from vertical | Video analysis |
| Foot strike | Midfoot preferred at marathon pace | Video analysis |

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Forcing 180 spm on short legs/stride — increases HR, decreases efficiency
- Doing explosive plyometrics without strength base — injury risk high
- Wearing carbon shoes in daily training — ~500 km lifespan, save for races + key workouts

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- Blagrove et al. (2018) — heavy resistance training for endurance athletes
- Barnes & Kilding (2015) — running economy: measurement, norms, determinants
- PMC12464892 (2025) — persistent RE improvements from advanced footwear
