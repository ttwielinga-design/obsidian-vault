# Domain 5: Recovery Science
### Evidence synthesized April 2026 | Sources: 2020–2025 peer-reviewed literature

## 5.1 HRV-Based Training Adjustment

### Evidence Summary
HRV-guided training (using daily rMSSD to adjust session intensity) shows **modest but consistent advantages** over fixed periodization — a 2021 systematic review with meta-analysis (PMC8507742) found HRV-guided training produced better maintenance of cardiac-vagal modulation and aerobic fitness with **less likelihood of negative adaptations**. However, the group-level performance benefit is small; the primary value is in **catching overreaching early** and individualizing recovery. The **rMSSD metric** (root mean square of successive differences) from overnight measurement is the most validated and practically useful HRV measure. Device accuracy ranking: **Oura Ring Gen 4 > WHOOP 4.0 > Garmin Fenix** based on 2025 validation studies (Physiological Reports, Wiley 2025).

### HRV Protocol for Thomas

| Day | HRV Reading vs. 7-day Avg | Action |
|-----|--------------------------|--------|
| Green (+5% or more above avg) | Proceed with planned quality session | Normal or slightly more demanding |
| Normal (within ±5% of avg) | Train as planned | Stick to plan |
| Amber (5–10% below avg) | Downgrade intensity | Convert interval day to Z1 easy run |
| Red (>10% below avg, 2+ consecutive days) | Recovery priority | Rest, easy walk, or swim only |

**Measurement Best Practices:**
- Measure at the same time every morning, lying still, before getting up
- Use 5-minute morning reading OR overnight average from Oura
- Look at **7-day rolling trend** more than single-day values
- Don't over-react to single low readings — patterns matter

### Device Recommendation

| Device | HRV Accuracy (CCC) | Recommendation |
|--------|-------------------|---------------|
| Oura Ring Gen 4 | 0.99 (excellent) | Best choice for sleep + HRV monitoring |
| WHOOP 4.0 | 0.94 (good) | Good choice; better wrist comfort for some |
| Garmin Fenix | 0.87 (acceptable) | Adequate if already owned; upgrade for serious use |

### Metrics Beyond HRV

| Marker | What to Watch | Frequency |
|--------|--------------|-----------|
| Resting HR | Elevated >5 bpm above baseline = stress signal | Daily (wearable) |
| Mood/motivation score | Self-rate 1–5 daily; consistent <3 = overreaching | Daily |
| Sleep duration + quality | <7 hrs repeatedly = recovery deficit accumulating | Daily |
| Training performance | Cannot hit planned paces at normal RPE | Each session |

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Checking HRV once and panicking — context is everything
- Using HRV as the only recovery marker (combine with subjective well-being)
- Measuring HRV inconsistently (different times, body positions, stress states)

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- PMC8507742 (2021) — HRV-guided training meta-analysis
- Physiological Reports / Wiley (2025) — consumer wearable HRV validation
- Buchheit (2014, Int J Sports Physiol Perform) — monitoring training with HRV

## 5.2 Sleep & Recovery Optimization

### Evidence Summary
Sleep is the **single highest-return recovery tool** with zero cost. A 2023 systematic review (PMC10354314, Sports Medicine Open) found that **sleep extension** (increasing to 8–10 hrs/night) was the most effective intervention for improving athletic performance — including reaction time, sprint speed, and endurance markers. Sleep deprivation causes approximately **0.4% performance decrease per additional hour awake** beyond normal sleep (Frontiers Physiology meta-regression, 2025). For marathon runners, disrupted sleep during heavy training blocks is a key underrecognized overtraining contributor. **Napping (20–60 min) at 13:00–14:00** restores partial performance decrements from sleep restriction and has no evidence of disrupting nighttime sleep quality.

### Sleep Protocol for Thomas

| Strategy | Target | Evidence |
|----------|--------|----------|
| Nightly duration | 8–9 hrs/night during training blocks | ★★★★★ (Sleep extension study; athletes in 7-hr habits) |
| Sleep timing | Consistent bed/wake times (±30 min) | ★★★★☆ |
| Room temperature | 16–19°C for optimal sleep quality | ★★★★☆ |
| Pre-sleep light exposure | No screens 60 min before bed OR blue-light glasses | ★★★★☆ |
| Pre-sleep nutrition | Light snack with casein protein (200–300 kcal) on heavy training days | ★★★☆☆ |
| Daytime nap (if sleep restricted) | 20–30 min nap at 13:00–14:00 | ★★★★☆ (PMC9744144) |
| Caffeine cutoff | No caffeine after 13:00 | ★★★★★ |

### Sleep Extension Protocol (Before Key Training Blocks)

Begin **7–10 days before** a key training block or race:
1. Move bedtime 30 min earlier each night (not an abrupt shift)
2. Target 9 hrs in bed per night
3. No alarm if possible — natural wake-up accumulates extra sleep debt repayment
4. Maintain during taper period — sleep quality directly predicts race performance

### Accumulated Fatigue Markers (Beyond HRV)

| Marker | Normal | Warning Sign |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| Morning mood | 4–5/5 | Consistently ≤2/5 for 3+ days |
| Performance on easy runs | Should feel easy | Feeling heavy/laboured at Z1 pace |
| Appetite | Normal | Sudden decrease or excessive hunger |
| Illness frequency | 1–2 mild illnesses/year | 3+ infections in 12-week block |
| Sleep quality | Refreshed waking | Waking tired despite 8+ hours |

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Using training as an excuse for 6-hour sleep nights — this blunts all adaptation
- Long naps (>90 min) → can cause sleep inertia and disrupt nighttime sleep
- Melatonin misuse — effective for jet lag, NOT proven for general sleep improvement in people without circadian disruption

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- PMC10354314 (2023) — sleep intervention systematic review for athletes
- PMC12610528 (2025) — sleep mechanisms in athletic performance (multidimensional review)
- Mah et al. (2011, Sleep) — sleep extension in collegiate athletes

## 5.3 Active Recovery & Modalities

### Evidence Summary
The **adaptation-blunting controversy** is the most important nuance in recovery for marathon runners: cold water immersion (CWI) used immediately post-training may **blunt endurance and strength adaptation signals** (inflammatory response suppression) — but this effect is primarily relevant for **resistance training adaptation**, with little-to-no negative effect on aerobic endurance adaptations per se (systematic review + meta-analysis, PubMed 33146851; PMC11897523 2025). The strategic recommendation: **avoid CWI during base and build phases** when adaptation accumulation is the priority; **use after races or isolated heavy blocks** when acute recovery matters most. Easy running (Z1) the day after hard sessions is superior to complete rest for clearing lactate and maintaining neuromuscular readiness.

### Recovery Modality Evidence Table

| Modality | Evidence for Recovery | Evidence for Performance | Recommendation |
|----------|----------------------|-------------------------|----------------|
| Easy Z1 run (next day) | ★★★★★ | ★★★★★ | Default recovery tool after hard sessions |
| Sleep (8–9 hrs) | ★★★★★ | ★★★★★ | Non-negotiable priority over all other modalities |
| Cold water immersion | ★★★★☆ (acute soreness) | ★★★☆☆ (can blunt adaptations) | Use post-race or key race simulations only; NOT during training base |
| Contrast therapy (hot/cold) | ★★★☆☆ | ★★★☆☆ | Reasonable for acute recovery; no strong advantage vs CWI alone |
| Foam rolling | ★★★☆☆ (ROM + soreness) | ★★☆☆☆ | 10 min post-run: reduces soreness, zero injury prevention evidence |
| Compression garments | ★★★☆☆ | ★★★☆☆ | Useful for post-race travel; limited training benefit |
| Sauna (post-easy run) | ★★★★☆ (plasma volume, heat adapt) | ★★★★☆ | High value — also a heat adaptation tool; post-easy runs only |
| Massage | ★★★☆☆ | ★★☆☆☆ | Reduces subjective soreness; limited performance evidence |
| Normatec compression boots | ★★★☆☆ | ★★☆☆☆ | Likely effective; expensive; similar outcome to elevation |
| Infrared light therapy | ★★☆☆☆ | ★★☆☆☆ | Emerging; not enough high-quality evidence for strong recommendation |

### Weekly Recovery Protocol for Thomas

| Day Type | Recommended Recovery Action |
|----------|-----------------------------|
| After VO2max session | Easy 30–40 min Z1 run next day; foam roll + cold shower (not full CWI) |
| After long run | Easy walk or rest; full nutrition repletion; sleep extension priority |
| After threshold session | Light Z1 jog next day; magnesium at night; no alcohol |
| After race simulation | Full rest day + cold bath (CWI acceptable here); 48-hr sleep priority |
| Weekly sauna | 15–20 min at 80°C after Tuesday/Thursday easy runs |

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Ice baths after every training session → potentially blunting mitochondrial and LT adaptations
- Replacing sleep with recovery tools (sauna, CWI) — no modality compensates for sleep deficit
- Skipping easy runs in favour of "complete rest" — active recovery outperforms passive rest for trained athletes

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- PubMed 33146851 — CWI and endurance adaptation meta-analysis
- PMC11897523 (2025) — optimal CWI dose/temperature network meta-analysis
- Frontiers in Physiology 2025 — post-exercise heat exposure systematic review

## 5.4 Overtraining & Overreaching

### Evidence Summary
Overtraining syndrome (OTS) is rare but debilitating — recovery can take **6–12 months**. More common for recreational marathon runners is **non-functional overreaching (NFOR)**, where performance declines and mood deteriorates for weeks. The progression is: Functional Overreaching (FO, intended training stress) → NFOR (too much, too fast) → OTS (entrenched). Key distinguishing factor: **FO resolves in days with rest; NFOR takes weeks; OTS takes months.** Blood biomarkers provide supporting data but no single marker is diagnostic — cortisol:testosterone ratio, ferritin, and HRV trends together are the most informative combination.

### Overtraining Diagnostic Table

| Stage | Duration to Recover | Performance | HRV | Mood | Action |
|-------|--------------------|-----------|----|------|--------|
| Functional overreaching (FO) | 1–3 days rest | Normal post-rest | Temporarily suppressed | Temporary drop | Normal — this is planned |
| Non-functional overreaching (NFOR) | 2–6 weeks | Declining despite rest | Chronically suppressed | Persistent low mood | Stop intensity; 2-week recovery block |
| Overtraining syndrome (OTS) | 6–12 months | Severely impaired | Dysregulated | Depression-level symptoms | Medical review required |

### Biomarker Monitoring (Every 4–6 weeks during heavy training)

| Biomarker | Normal Range | Overreaching Signal |
|-----------|-------------|---------------------|
| Ferritin | 50–200 µg/L | <30 µg/L = iron deficiency → fatigue |
| Testosterone | 300–1000 ng/dL (male) | Declining trend over training block |
| Cortisol | 6–23 µg/dL (morning) | Elevated AM cortisol + low testosterone = OTS signal |
| CRP (inflammation) | <3 mg/L | Chronically elevated = systemic inflammation |
| Creatine kinase (CK) | <200 U/L at rest | Persistently elevated post-rest = muscle damage accumulation |
| HRV (rMSSD) | Individual baseline | 7-day average >10% below norm for 2+ weeks |

### Recovery Protocol (if NFOR detected)

**Week 1–2:** Zero intensity — replace with Z1 walking, swimming, easy cycling (no running)
**Week 3:** Return to Z1 running only (max 30 min/day)
**Week 4:** Gradual return to normal easy volume
**Week 5+:** Reintroduce one quality session, monitor HRV/mood closely

**Never:** Return to intensity before HRV, mood, and resting HR have normalized

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Pushing through NFOR with "mental toughness" → converts to OTS
- Not tracking any objective markers → NFOR sneaks up gradually
- Using stimulants (pre-workout, extra caffeine) to mask fatigue signals

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- Meeusen et al. (2013) — European College of Sport Science consensus on OTS
- Buchheit (2014) — HRV as early overreaching detection tool
- Kreher & Schwartz (2012, Sports Health) — overtraining syndrome review
