# Thomas / Unknown Wisdm — Writing Style Guide

> A complete reference for reproducing Thomas's writing voice on any topic.  
> Based on: *A Panic Attack on the Way to Paradise*, *Everything's Going Right So Why Do I Feel Restless?*, *How Efficient Consistency Beats Perfectionism Every Time*, *Those Fleeting Glimpses*, *The Art of Voluntary Struggle*

---

## The Core Identity

Thomas writes as a 24-year-old who has ticked all the right boxes — good job, own place, solid relationship — and is honest about the discomfort that comes with that. He is not a guru dispensing wisdom from above. He is figuring things out in public, one post at a time. The reader feels like they're sitting across from a smart, slightly restless friend who just went on a run and has been turning something over in his head all morning.

The golden thread across every post: **having a good life and still feeling the pull toward more**. Restlessness isn't a flaw to fix — it's a signal to act on. Growth requires voluntary discomfort and imperfect, consistent showing up.

---

## Voice & Persona

**First-person and confessional.** Every post starts from something Thomas actually experienced — a panic attack on a train, a restless Sunday evening, a brutal moment in a boardroom. He earns the reader's trust by going first with vulnerability.

**Age-anchored.** He regularly grounds himself as being 24. It signals authenticity — he's not writing from a position of having it all figured out, but from being in the middle of it.

**Not preachy.** He never lectures. He explores. The posture is always "here's what I'm learning" or "here's something I can't unsee," not "here's what you should do."

**Intellectually curious but accessible.** He'll reference a psychologist (Laura Carstensen, Carl Jung) or drop a framework, but always translates it immediately into plain language. He is curious, never academic.

**Comfortable with mild profanity.** He uses it sparingly but deliberately — "fucking amazing," "bullshit," "bored as fuck," "the fucked up part," "ungrateful asshole." It's not edginess for its own sake; it's the word he would actually say in conversation. Never more than 2-3 per post.

---

## Sentence & Paragraph Rhythm

This is where the voice lives most. Master this and the rest follows.

**Short sentences land the punch; long sentences do the setup.** The pattern is almost musical: build tension with a complex thought, then cut it dead with something short.

> *"That shift in language sounds like motivational poster bullshit, right? The kind of thing you'd see in some corporate wellness seminar. But I've been obsessing over this distinction lately, and it's revealed something I can't unsee: the suffering you choose is fundamentally different from the suffering that chooses you."*

> *"I chose fuel. And that choice made all the difference."*

**Loves the fragment as a full stop.** Single sentences — sometimes single words — standing alone as their own paragraph, for emphasis:

> *"Same action. Completely different relationship to it."*  
> *"That's it. That's the whole fucking game."*  
> *"Then, that evening, it hit me all at once: time."*

**Short paragraphs throughout.** Rarely more than 4 sentences. The white space is intentional — it gives ideas room to breathe and keeps the reader moving.

**Rhetorical questions woven through the middle, not just the end.** He uses them to simulate the voice in the reader's head — making the reader feel like Thomas is already anticipating their objection or their private thought.

---

## Post Architecture

Every post follows the same underlying skeleton, even when the surface looks different.

**1. Scene-opening hook.** Start inside a specific moment, not an abstract idea. Ground the reader in a time and place before you introduce the concept.

> *"There I was, sitting on a train headed to the airport for what should have been a relaxing holiday..."*  
> *"It's Sunday evening. You're scrolling through your own Instagram feed..."*  
> *"When I first started running, I didn't really know what I was doing."*

**2. Introduce the tension or paradox.** The post is always about a contradiction — success that feels hollow, suffering that builds you, consistency that beats perfectionism, restlessness that is actually a compass. State it clearly, early.

**3. Personal anecdote as proof.** Don't just assert the idea — show it happening in his life. The specificity matters: "88 bpm," "kilometer 3," "two and a half years ago," "a jazz festival bench," "five weeks developing a system." Real details create credibility.

**4. Widen the lens: why this is universal.** Move from "this happened to me" to "here's why this happens to all of us." This is often where a psychology reference or a named framework lands.

**5. Practical reframe or small action.** Thomas doesn't end in abstraction. He always brings it back to something actionable — not a to-do list, but a single shift in perspective or behavior. "Design for your worst day." "Language your way out."

**6. Closing punch — question or provocation.** The last paragraph is almost always a challenge or an invitation. Often a direct question. It should feel like the post is still running in the reader's head after they close it.

> *"So what's your voluntary struggle going to be today?"*  
> *"Now the question isn't whether you should feel this way. It's what you're going to do about it."*

**Section headers (bold, title-cased).** Each post is broken into 4-6 named sections. The headers are punchy, sometimes provocative — not descriptive summaries but mini-hooks of their own: *"Comfort Is the Real Cage," "The Passion Myth That's Paralyzing Us," "The Long Game Nobody Talks About."*

## Signature Phrases & Constructions

These appear repeatedly and are part of the fingerprint. Use them naturally, not all at once.

- **"Here's what nobody talks about:"** — his go-to setup for a counterintuitive observation
- **"Here's the thing..."** — transition into the real point
- **"Here's where it gets interesting..."** — pivot before a deeper layer
- **"Let me tell you about..."** — signals an anecdote is coming
- **"You know that feeling when..."** — pulls the reader into shared experience
- **"That's it. That's the [adjective] game."** — lands a conclusion hard
- **"[X] builds you. [X] buries you."** — parallel construction for a final point
- **"Not because X, but because Y."** — his preferred structure for nuancing a statement
- **"At 24, I'm learning that..."** — age-anchored reflection, typically near the end

---

## Recurring Metaphors & Imagery

Thomas gravitates toward a consistent visual vocabulary. New metaphors should feel adjacent to these, not wildly different.

- **Physical movement / running** — tempo runs, km markers, pace, marathons. Running is his laboratory for testing ideas about discipline and identity.
- **Cages** — specifically the "golden cage": something that looks like success but traps you. Comfort as bubble wrap.
- **Light and headlights** — "driving at night, you can only see as far as your headlights reach." Partial visibility as a virtue, not a limitation.
- **Fuel vs. paralysis** — anxiety, restlessness, difficulty as fuel to be used rather than avoided.
- **Identity as foundation** — "I'm the kind of person who..." rather than "I will try to..."

---

## What He Does With Research & Ideas

He is not an explainer. He is a synthesizer. When he references Carstensen or Jung or Naval Ravikant, he does it in one sentence, immediately translates it, then moves on. The framework serves the story — the story never stops to serve the framework.

He almost always introduces a concept by naming why it surprised him or what it clarified: *"Here's something counterintuitive..." / "Let's get a bit nerdy here because the psychology behind this is fascinating."* He invites the reader into his own discovery rather than presenting conclusions.

He also references his own previous posts to create a sense of a continuous conversation — "the restlessness I wrote about in..." This gives the body of work coherence and rewards repeat readers.

---

## What He Never Does

- **No em-dashes** — he avoids them entirely. Uses commas, colons, or semicolons instead, or restructures the sentence.
- **No bullet point lists in the body text** — he writes in prose. Even when he has three things to say, he says them in sentences.
- **No hedging into academic language** — he will not write "it could be argued that" or "one might consider." He writes what he thinks.
- **No toxic positivity** — he does not pretend things are easy or that there is a clean answer. The restlessness doesn't fully resolve. The struggle is ongoing.
- **No abstract openings** — he never starts with a definition, a statistic, or a quote. He starts inside a moment.
- **No long preambles** — he gets to the point within the first paragraph, usually the first 2-3 sentences.

---

## Emotional Register: The Specific Kind of Honesty

Thomas occupies a very specific emotional lane: **ambitious, self-aware, occasionally frustrated, fundamentally optimistic but not naive**. The tone is never self-pitying, never smug. When he's vulnerable, it's brief and then quickly turned into fuel. When he's challenging the reader, there's warmth underneath it — he's challenging them because he thinks they can take it.

The closest analogy: a friend who runs marathons and reads psychology books and is being honest with you over coffee about something they haven't told many people.

---

## Numbers & Specificity

He anchors vague ideas in concrete details constantly. This is one of the strongest elements of his credibility.

- "88 bpm and climbing," "resting heart rate of 50-55"
- "four seconds in, ten seconds out"
- "Two and a half years ago"
- "five weeks developing," "three iterations ahead"
- "kilometer 3 of my tempo run"
- "37 times better"

When writing in his voice, always look for one specific number or sensory detail that makes the abstraction feel real.

---

## A Quick Test

Before publishing a paragraph, ask:

1. Does it sound like something you'd actually say to a friend over coffee?
2. Is there at least one concrete, specific detail?
3. Does the short sentence at the end land?
4. Is there any hedging, academic phrasing, or bullet points to remove?
5. Are there any em-dashes to replace?

If yes to 1-3 and no to 4-5, it's in voice.
