### Evidence synthesized April 2026 | Sources: 2020–2025 peer-reviewed literature

## 6.1 Pacing Strategy

### Evidence Summary
The **negative split strategy** (running the second half slightly faster than the first) is supported by the strongest body of evidence for recreational marathon runners — it reduces early glycogen depletion, minimizes metabolic acidosis accumulation, and is psychologically sustainable (Frontiers in Physiology, 2025, PMC12307312). A 2024 systematic review of 39 pacing studies (PMC11400961) confirmed negative splitting is associated with better overall times. However, an important counterpoint: elite runners with official pacemakers may achieve optimal times with **slight positive splits (2.4–2.6% faster in the first half)** — but this is elite-specific and not applicable to recreational runners. For Thomas at sub-3:30, an even split with a slight negative (1–2%) is optimal.

### Sub-3:30 Pacing Table (Target: 3:28:XX)

| Segment | Target Pace | Target Split Time | Notes |
|---------|-------------|------------------|-------|
| km 0–5 | 5:05–5:10/km | 25:30–25:50 | Hold back — feels too easy |
| km 5–10 | 5:00–5:05/km | 25:00–25:30 | Settling in, relaxed |
| km 10–20 | 4:58–5:02/km | 49:40–50:20 | Marathon effort locked in |
| km 20–30 | 4:55–5:00/km | 49:10–50:00 | Hold rhythm, start fueling aggressively |
| km 30–35 | 4:55–5:00/km | 24:35–25:00 | The wall zone — mental fortitude |
| km 35–42.2 | 4:50–5:00/km | 34:35–36:00 | Negative finish if possible |
| **Total** | **4:57–4:59 avg** | **3:28:30–3:30:00** | |

**Temperature Adjustments:**
- +10 sec/km per 5°C above 15°C (e.g., 20°C race = +10 sec/km target adjustment)
- Wind: +5 sec/km per 5 m/s headwind component
- Elevation: Kipchoge's rule — 1 sec/km per 10m net gain over 10 km

### Glycogen and "The Wall" Physiology

The classic km 30–35 "wall" is primarily **glycogen depletion** combined with accumulated central fatigue. Proper pacing + aggressive fueling (60–90 g CHO/hr from km 7 onward) dramatically reduces wall risk. The wall also has a **psychological component** — runners who associate with their body (internal focus) manage late-race suffering better than those who dissociate.

### Race Time Prediction Formulas

| Input | Formula | Example (Thomas) |
|-------|---------|-----------------|
| 10K race time | Marathon ≈ 10K time × 4.65 | 43:00 × 4.65 = 3:19:55 |
| Half marathon time | Marathon ≈ HM time × 2.11 | 1:38 × 2.11 = 3:27:38 |
| VO2max (ml/kg/min) | Marathon time (min) ≈ (-2.682 × VO2max) + 285 | VO2max 55 → 3:09 |

Note: These formulas assume well-rested, properly fueled, optimal conditions racing.

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Going out 10–15 sec/km too fast in the first 5 km (race excitement) → pays double at km 30+
- Checking watch every 30 seconds → creates anxiety; use km splits instead
- Ignoring weather adjustments → overestimating target pace in heat

## 6.2 Tapering Science

### Evidence Summary
A **3-week taper with 40–60% volume reduction** is the most evidence-supported strategy for recreational marathon runners. A 2021 Frontiers Sports analysis of 158,000+ recreational marathon runners (PMC8506252) found that a **strict 3-week taper saved a median 5 minutes 32 seconds** vs. minimal taper. A general meta-analysis (PMC10171681, 2023) confirmed the optimal taper reduces volume by 41–60% while maintaining intensity and frequency. Strength training can be maintained at **low volume** during taper to preserve neuromuscular adaptations — 1 light session/week at reduced load.

### 3-Week Taper Protocol for Thomas

| Week | Volume | Intensity Sessions | Long Run | Focus |
|------|--------|--------------------|----------|-------|
| T–3 (3 weeks out) | 70% of peak | 1× threshold + 1× MP | 22–24 km (easy) | Last hard volume week |
| T–2 | 50% of peak | 1× short threshold (20 min) + strides | 16–18 km | Freshening up |
| T–1 (race week) | 30% of peak | 1× MP strides session (20 min) | 10–12 km (3 days out) | Pure freshness |
| Race day | 0 training | — | **RACE** | Execute |

**Nutrition Shift During Taper:**
- Maintain total calories — do NOT reduce eating proportionally to training
- Begin carbohydrate loading 3 days out (8–12 g/kg/day)
- Reduce fibre and fat to minimize gut volume

**Managing "Taper Madness":**
- Phantom injuries and low energy are normal and nearly universal
- Legs feeling heavy in week T–1 is normal (glycogen loading effect)
- Do NOT add extra easy runs to "feel better" — trust the process

### Physiological Changes During Taper

| Marker | Change During Taper | Interpretation |
|--------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Muscle glycogen | Increases +20–40% | Fuel reservoir fills |
| Muscle damage markers | Decrease | Recovery from accumulated damage |
| Fast-twitch fiber size | Slight increase | Neuromuscular freshness |
| VO2max | Maintained or slight improvement | Aerobic engine preserved |
| HRV | Should rise significantly | Parasympathetic recovery |

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Reducing intensity too much in the taper (keep quality, just reduce volume)
- Reducing calories too much → glycogen stores don't load properly
- Panicking about fitness loss and adding extra sessions

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- PMC8506252 (2021) — longer disciplined tapers improve marathon performance
- PMC10171681 (2023) — tapering meta-analysis for endurance athletes
- Mujika & Padilla (2003, Sports Med) — tapering science foundation paper

## 6.3 Mental Performance & Pain Tolerance

### Evidence Summary
The **psychobiological model** of endurance performance (Marcora, 2008–2022) posits that **perceived effort (RPE), not physiological limits, is the primary performance governor** in well-trained runners — meaning mental skills directly improve marathon performance. Research supports: (1) **positive self-talk** improves cycling TT performance by 18% in experimental conditions (Blanchfield 2014, Med Sci Sports Exerc); (2) **associative attentional strategies** (focusing on body signals) outperform dissociation at marathon pace in trained runners; (3) **implementation intentions** ("if-then" mental plans for bad patches) are the most practically effective tool for race execution. Visualization has moderate evidence, strongest as a race rehearsal tool pre-race.

### Mental Skills Protocol for Thomas

| Skill | When to Use | Protocol |
|-------|------------|---------|
| **Motivational self-talk** | Hard intervals, km 30+ | Prepare 3–5 short mantras. "Strong and smooth." "This is what you trained for." "One km at a time." |
| **Association strategy** | During marathon pace runs + racing | Focus on breathing rhythm, cadence, arm swing — internal body signals |
| **Dissociation** | Long easy runs, taper madness | Music, podcast, new route — preserve mental energy |
| **Visualization** | Evening before race + 1–2 weeks out | Mental walkthrough of entire race: start line, km 20 feeling, km 32 suffering, finish line |
| **If-then implementation plans** | Race planning session | "If I hit km 30 and want to slow down, THEN I will shorten my stride and focus on arm drive for 2 minutes before adjusting pace." |
| **Pre-race routine** | Morning of race, consistently | Same every time: breakfast, warm-up sequence, check gear, arrival timing, activation music |

### RPE as Performance Tool

**RPE calibration in training is the key to race-day execution:**
- In base runs, learn what RPE 5 feels like (Z1) — this is your "all day" effort
- In threshold sessions, learn RPE 7–8 (LT2) — this is race effort for marathon
- In VO2max sessions, learn RPE 9 — so you know you have reserves
- Use RPE alongside HR and pace — if RPE is high at target pace, it's a warning sign

**RPE vs. Pace Decision Framework on Race Day:**

| Situation | Decision |
|-----------|----------|
| Target pace feels RPE 5–6 in first 10 km | You may be going too slow — but hold back anyway |
| Target pace feels RPE 8+ at km 5 | Slow down 10 sec/km immediately |
| RPE spikes at km 30 but pace is still on target | This is normal — reduce stride length, maintain cadence |
| RPE 9+ at km 35 | Survival mode — shorten to 2 km checkpoints |

### Building Race-Specific Mental Toughness in Training

| Training Tool | Method | Frequency |
|--------------|--------|-----------|
| Race effort runs | 8–16 km at marathon pace during long run (in build phase) | 1×/week in build block |
| "Dark place" intervals | VO2max intervals taken to genuine failure occasionally | 1× every 3–4 weeks |
| Simulated race conditions | Long run on race course or equivalent terrain, with race-day nutrition | 2–3 times in build |
| Competition training | Join parkruns / local races as tempo efforts | 1× every 4–6 weeks |

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- No mental preparation → "improvising" at km 32 when suffering begins
- Dissociating throughout the marathon → disconnection from effort signals, leading to blowups
- Catastrophizing phantom pains in taper → interpret correctly as normal

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- Marcora (2010) — psychobiological model of exercise tolerance
- Blanchfield et al. (2014) — self-talk and exercise performance RCT
- Brick et al. (2016) — attentional focus during endurance performance
