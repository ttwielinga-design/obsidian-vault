## 1.2  Specific Criticisms & Required Changes

CRITIQUE 1. Goal A (sub-3:15) is not realistic in 8 weeks post-injury
Your Riegel prediction from best HM-distance effort (21.25 km at 4:36/km) gives 3:22:15. Sub-3:15 requires 4:37/km — essentially your best half-marathon pace held for double the distance. Closing a 7-minute gap requires 10–12 weeks of uninterrupted peak training, not 8 weeks of post-injury rebuilding. Goal A should remain aspirational only. Goal B (sub-3:20, 4:44/km) is the evidence-based primary target and is achievable if the block goes well. Goal C (sub-3:25, 4:51/km) is the floor.
CRITIQUE 2. Week 1 quality session is introduced too early
Wednesday of Week 1 calls for 3×8 min at 4:37/km — this is full marathon-race-pace effort in only the third running session post-injury. The knee has been rehabilitated for 5 weeks, and there has been no running above 10 km. Introducing MP blocks in Week 1 elevates re-injury risk significantly. Evidence (PMC11986186, 2025): rapid load spikes are the primary injury predictor. Correction: Week 1 Wednesday should be controlled Zone 3 (4:50–5:05/km) not full 4:37/km MP. True marathon pace blocks should begin no earlier than Week 2.
CRITIQUE 3. No deload week in a 6-week progressive block
The plan runs 36→42→48→55→60 km over five consecutive weeks with no down week. This violates the supercompensation principle: the adaptation to a training load occurs during recovery, not during the load itself. A 2024 meta-analysis showed deload weeks every 3–4 weeks reduce injury risk by 31% and improve subsequent performance block output. With a compressed 8-week timeline, the correct structure is: build 3 weeks → deload 1 week → peak 2 weeks → taper. This document implements this change.
CRITIQUE 4. Volume jump from comeback to Week 1 is 50%
Your last recorded training week (Mar 30 – Apr 5) logged approximately 23.8 km. Week 1 targets 36 km — a 51% single-week increase. While the plan start date of April 7 is reasonable, the jump is sharp. The 10% rule is a guideline, not a law, but a 51% jump on a knee that has been inactive for 5 weeks warrants caution. Recommendation: Week 1 long run reduced from 17 km to 15 km, with the remaining daily runs unchanged. This brings Week 1 to approximately 34 km — a 43% increase but with a safer long-run ceiling.
CRITIQUE 5. Taper is 1 week — evidence supports at least 2
Research (PMC8506252, 2021: 158,000+ recreational marathon runners) found a strict 3-week taper saves a median 5 minutes 32 seconds vs. minimal taper. The current plan has 1 week taper (38 km) and race week (~18 km). With only 8 total weeks, a 3-week taper is not possible without sacrificing all build. However, the peak volume week (60 km) should be moved to Week 5, Week 6 reduced to 50 km (transition week), and Week 7 at 32 km (true taper). This gives a more progressive descent to race day. See revised plan.
CRITIQUE 6. Race pace anchoring should be 4:44/km, not 4:37/km
The plan instructs executing pacing at 4:37/km (sub-3:15 goal pace) throughout all MP sessions. Because Goal B (sub-3:20 / 4:44/km) is the realistic primary target, all MP sessions should be trained at 4:44/km with 4:37/km reserved for exceptional-form days only. Running every quality session 7 sec/km above current ceiling creates chronic fatigue and reinforces a pace that may not be sustainable on race day. Execution strategy: race with 4:44/km for first 30 km, then accelerate if HR remains below 162.
CRITIQUE 7. Cadence improvement protocol lacks specificity
The coaching doc correctly identifies your cadence of 160–164 spm as below the 170–175 spm optimal. However, prescribing 'cadence drills 3–4 times per week' without a progression target will not produce improvement. Evidence: increasing cadence from 160 to 170 spm requires 4–8 weeks of deliberate practice with audio cues. Protocol required: use metronome app at 170 spm during warm-up strides; increase long-run cadence by 2 spm every 2 weeks. This has direct knee protection value — +10 spm reduces impact forces by 10–14% (Heiderscheit et al. 2011, Med Sci Sports Exerc).
CRITIQUE 8. Strength session placement conflicts with recovery
The plan places strength sessions on Tuesday (rest + full strength) when Monday is an easy run. This is correct. However, the week structure in some weeks has strength the day before quality sessions. Evidence (Huiberts et al. 2024): strength should be placed on the same day as hard running sessions, not the day before, to concentrate fatigue and keep easy days easy. All strength sessions should be performed after the day's running session, not as standalone days before quality work.
CRITIQUE 9. No explicit knee pain abort protocol
The coaching doc mentions 'knee pain beyond 10–15 minutes is a red flag' but does not specify what to do. There is no explicit decision tree. With a history of PFPS, this is a dangerous gap — an athlete who is motivated and data-driven (Thomas's profile) will push through discomfort without clear guidelines. The revised protocol includes a three-level abort system with specific substitution protocols.
CRITIQUE 10. Carb loading window is too short
The coaching doc starts carb loading on Thursday (T–3) at 8–10g/kg/day. Evidence supports beginning the shift at T–4 with a moderate load (6–8 g/kg) before escalating to 8–10 g/kg on T–3 and T–2. This gives glycogen stores a full 4-day window to saturate — the Fairchild et al. (2002) protocol shows this maximises muscle glycogen above T–3 start alone.
