# Domain 4: Nutrition & Fueling
### Evidence synthesized April 2026 | Sources: 2020–2025 peer-reviewed literature

## 4.1 Daily Nutrition Framework

### Evidence Summary
For a 75 kg male running 8–12 hours/week, energy availability (EA) must stay **above 45 kcal/kg lean mass/day** to avoid RED-S (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport) — which affects male runners more often than commonly assumed, primarily through suppressed testosterone and impaired bone health (Sports Nutrition literature 2022–2024). Carbohydrate periodization — adjusting daily CHO intake to match training load — has strong mechanistic support through the "train low, compete high" model, but **must be applied selectively** (only on low-intensity days) to avoid blunting adaptation. **Protein at 1.6–2.0 g/kg/day** is the current consensus for endurance athletes doing strength work (Sport Science meta-analysis, Morton et al. 2018; confirmed in IOC consensus).

### Daily Macronutrient Targets for Thomas (~75 kg)

| Nutrient | Target | Heavy Training Day | Easy/Rest Day |
|----------|--------|--------------------|---------------|
| Carbohydrates | 5–8 g/kg/day | 7–8 g/kg (525–600g) | 4–5 g/kg (300–375g) |
| Protein | 1.6–2.0 g/kg/day | 2.0 g/kg (150g) | 1.6 g/kg (120g) |
| Fat | 1.0–1.5 g/kg/day | 1.0 g/kg (75g) | 1.5 g/kg (112g) |
| Total Calories | ~3,000–4,200 kcal | ~3,800–4,200 kcal | ~2,800–3,200 kcal |

**Train Low, Compete High — When to Use It:**
- Deliberately train glycogen-depleted on **Z1 easy runs only** (not intervals, not long runs)
- Method: Skip breakfast before easy AM run OR do easy evening run after normal dinner
- **Never** do VO2max or threshold sessions in a depleted state — performance and adaptation suffer

**RED-S Risk in Male Runners:**
- Real risk at >10 hrs/week training if not deliberately eating enough
- Warning signs: unexplained performance plateau, mood changes, persistent illness, stress fracture history
- Monitor morning HRV and resting HR as proxy markers

### Meal Timing Protocol

| Timing | Recommendation |
|--------|---------------|
| 3–4 hrs pre-run | Main carb meal (rice, oats, pasta) — 1–2 g/kg CHO |
| 30–60 min pre-run | Light snack if needed (banana, toast) — 0.5 g/kg CHO |
| During run >75 min | 40–60 g CHO/hr (gels + sports drink) |
| Within 30 min post-run | Fast CHO + protein (3:1 CHO:protein ratio, ~20–30g protein) |
| Evening (heavy training day) | Prioritize CHO replenishment for next-day sessions |

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Eating "clean" and inadvertently under-fueling training volume
- Ignoring protein — runners underestimate protein needs vs. strength athletes
- Using same carbohydrate intake on rest days as hard training days → unnecessary fat gain

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- Morton et al. (2018) — systematic review on protein requirements for endurance athletes
- Burke et al. (2011, J Sports Sci) — carbohydrate for training and competition
- IOC consensus on RED-S (2023 update)

## 4.2 Race Day Fueling & Carbohydrate Loading

### Evidence Summary
Carbohydrate loading (3–4 days before the marathon at 8–12 g/kg/day) **fully saturates glycogen stores** (~500–600g body glycogen) and has strong meta-analytic support for marathon performance (Burke, 2011; confirmed in 2022–2024 reviews). On race day, consuming **60–90 g CHO/hr** using **multiple transportable carbohydrates** (glucose:fructose ≈ 2:1) is the current gold standard — 90 g/hr is achievable only with dual-transporter fueling since SGLT1 (glucose transporter) caps at ~60 g/hr. A 2025 PMC study confirmed that athletes meeting 60–90 g/hr CHO were significantly more likely to finish sub-3 hrs. **Caffeine at 3–6 mg/kg, taken 45–60 min before race start,** has the strongest ergogenic evidence in sport.

### Carbohydrate Loading Protocol (3 Days Out)

| Day | Carbohydrate Target | Training | Food Focus |
|-----|--------------------|---------|----|
| T–3 | 6–8 g/kg (450–600g) | Easy 30 min | Pasta, rice, bread, oats |
| T–2 | 8–10 g/kg (600–750g) | Rest or 20 min jog | Add sweet potato, bananas, sports drinks |
| T–1 | 8–10 g/kg | Rest | Familiar foods only — no fibre bombs |
| Race morning | 1.5–2.5 g/kg, 3 hrs before | — | Oats, toast, banana, sports drink |

**Avoid race week:** high-fibre foods, cruciferous vegetables, beans, lactose (for sensitive individuals), unfamiliar foods.

### Race Day In-Run Fueling Protocol

| Time Marker | Action | Why |
|-------------|--------|-----|
| Start | Nothing — let carbo-loading carry you | Conservation |
| 30–40 min in (km 7–8) | First gel (25–30g CHO) | Get transport primed before deficit |
| Every 20–25 min after | Gel or chews (25–30g CHO) | Maintain 60–90g/hr |
| Water stations | Take water WITH gels, not sports drink (to avoid excessive sugar overload in gut) | GI protection |
| km 30+ | Caffeine gel if not already using it | Performance protection in wall zone |

**For Thomas (sub-3:30, ~4:58/km pace, ~3:29 total):** Target 70–80 g CHO/hr with mixed glucose+fructose gels. 5–7 gels total. Practice this exact protocol on every long run over 90 min.

**Caffeine Protocol:**
- Dose: 3–4 mg/kg = **225–300 mg for 75 kg** Thomas
- Timing: 45–60 min before start (morning coffee + pre-race gel)
- Note: Avoid tolerance — reduce caffeine intake 5–7 days before race (controversial, but reduces chance of non-response)

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Carb loading with greasy / heavy foods → gut sluggishness
- Taking first gel too late (km 18–20) → already in deficit
- Using gels only with other sugary drinks → SGLT1 saturates, GI crisis
- Never having practiced race fueling in training → first time on race day = disaster

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- PMC11911277 (2025) — nutritional intake influence on marathon finishing time
- Jeukendrup (2014, Sports Med) — multiple transportable carbohydrates
- Graham (2001) — caffeine and exercise performance review

## 4.3 Hydration Science

### Evidence Summary
The "drink before thirsty" advice is now **outdated** — current consensus supports **"drink to thirst"** for events under 4 hours in trained athletes (Sawka et al. 2007; confirmed in recent marathon hydration reviews). Overhydration is a **more common cause of hyponatremia** (dangerously low sodium) in recreational marathon runners than dehydration — particularly slower runners who drink too much water relative to sweat rate. Sweat rates vary enormously (0.5–2.5 L/hr) and must be **individually measured** to optimize. Pre-race sodium loading (300–500 mg sodium 60 min pre-race) may reduce hyponatremia risk in vulnerable runners and has emerging evidence.

### Individual Sweat Rate Estimation

**Field Protocol:**
1. Weigh naked before run (to nearest 0.1 kg)
2. Run 60 min at marathon effort with no drinking
3. Weigh naked after
4. Sweat rate (L/hr) = (pre-weight − post-weight) in kg = litres lost
5. Add any fluid consumed during test

**For 75 kg runner at 16°C (Utrecht typical race conditions):**
- Estimated sweat rate: 0.8–1.2 L/hr at marathon pace
- Aim to replace ~75–80% of sweat lost (not 100% — mild dehydration of <2% BW does not impair performance in trained athletes)

### Hydration Protocol Table

| Phase | Target | Fluid | Notes |
|-------|--------|-------|-------|
| 24 hrs pre-race | Well-hydrated (pale yellow urine) | Water + electrolytes | Do not over-drink |
| Race morning | 500 ml in the 2 hrs before start | Sports drink or water + sodium | Last 200 ml ≈ 30 min out |
| During race | Drink to thirst, max ~400–600 ml/hr | Water + electrolytes (sodium ≥300 mg/hr) | Take at water stations, do not carry excess |
| Post-race | 1.5× fluid volume lost | Water + sodium-rich food | Rehydrate over 2–4 hrs, not all at once |

**Hyponatremia Risk Reduction:**
- Always consume sodium alongside fluid during race (gels, sports drink, salt tabs)
- Never drink beyond thirst
- Higher risk: slower runners (>4 hrs), female sex, hot weather, NSAIDs use pre-race

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Drinking 600–800 ml/hr "just in case" → sodium dilution → hyponatremia
- Using plain water only with no electrolytes over a 3+ hour event
- Pre-race hyperhydration with plain water → heavier, sloshy, GI issues

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- Sawka et al. (2007, Med Sci Sports Exerc) — fluid replacement in athletic activity
- Speedy et al. (2001) — hyponatremia in marathon runners
- Precision Hydration sweat sodium testing protocols

## 4.4 Supplements with Evidence

### Evidence Summary
The IOC's consensus framework (2022) ranks supplements by evidence level. Only a small number of supplements have **strong, consistent evidence** for endurance performance. The rankings below are based on the most recent systematic reviews and meta-analyses, including the 2025 Frontiers Nutrition systematic review and the 2025 Frontiers endurance supplement combination review.

### Supplement Evidence Ranking Table

| Supplement | Evidence Rating | Mechanism | Protocol for Thomas | Expected Effect |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|---------------------|-----------------|
| **Caffeine** | ★★★★★ Gold | Adenosine receptor blockade | 225–300 mg, 45–60 min pre-race | 1–3% time improvement |
| **Nitrates (beetroot)** | ★★★★☆ Strong | NO-mediated vasodilation, O2 efficiency | 400–500 mg nitrate, 2–3 hrs pre-race (or 3-day loading) | 1–2% in sub-elite; less in elites |
| **Iron** (if deficient) | ★★★★★ (if deficient) | O2 transport via hemoglobin | Test ferritin; supplement if <30 µg/L | Large — restores function |
| **Vitamin D** | ★★★★☆ (if deficient) | Immune, bone, muscle function | Test serum 25(OH)D; maintain >75 nmol/L. 2,000 IU/day Oct–Apr | Injury and illness protection |
| **Creatine** | ★★★☆☆ Moderate | Phosphocreatine resynthesis | 3–5 g/day during strength phase | Strength benefit; adds 0.5–1 kg body water |
| **Beta-alanine** | ★★★☆☆ Moderate | Muscle carnosine, buffers H+ | 4–6 g/day, loading 10–12 weeks | Aids high-intensity bursts >60s; minimal pure endurance benefit |
| **Omega-3 (EPA+DHA)** | ★★★☆☆ Moderate | Anti-inflammatory, muscle repair | 2–3 g/day combined EPA+DHA | Recovery support, anti-inflammatory |
| **Magnesium** | ★★★☆☆ (if deficient) | Muscle function, sleep quality | 300 mg magnesium glycinate at night | Sleep + cramping (if deficient) |
| **Collagen + Vitamin C** | ★★★☆☆ Emerging | Collagen synthesis in tendons/ligaments | 15–20 g collagen hydrolysate + 50 mg Vit C, 30–60 min pre-run | Tendon resilience; best in base phase |
| **Sodium bicarbonate** | ★★★☆☆ (short events) | Buffering blood lactate | 0.3 g/kg, 60–90 min pre-effort | Relevant for 800m–5K; minimal marathon benefit |
| **Ashwagandha** | ★★☆☆☆ Weak | Cortisol modulation, stress | Not recommended as primary supplement | Small, inconsistent effect |
| **HMB** | ★★☆☆☆ Weak | Anti-catabolic | Not recommended | Not well-supported in trained athletes |

**Overhyped supplements to deprioritize:** Ashwagandha, HMB, BCAA supplementation (sufficient from food), glutamine, and most "fat-burning" supplements.

### Priority Stack for Thomas (sub-3:30 Marathon)

| Priority | Supplement | Timing |
|----------|-----------|--------|
| Must | Caffeine on race day | 45–60 min pre-race |
| Must (if deficient) | Iron — test ferritin | Daily with Vitamin C |
| Must (winter months) | Vitamin D | Daily |
| High value | Nitrates (beetroot) | 2–3 hrs pre-race + 3-day loading |
| During strength phase | Creatine | 3–5 g/day |
| Daily maintenance | Omega-3 | 2–3 g/day with meals |
| Connective tissue | Collagen + Vit C | 30–60 min before key runs |
| Sleep/cramping | Magnesium glycinate | Evening |

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Testing supplements for the first time on race day
- Taking nitrates from beet supplements with antibacterial mouthwash (kills NO-converting bacteria)
- Mega-dosing Vitamin C (>1 g/day) may blunt endurance adaptation signal
- Iron supplementation without deficiency → no benefit; potential toxicity

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- IOC Consensus on Dietary Supplements (2022 update, PMC5867441)
- Frontiers Nutrition 2025 — elite athlete supplement efficacy systematic review
- Dietary Nitrate umbrella review (PMC12106159, 2025)
