# Domain 1: Training Structure & Periodization
### Evidence synthesized April 2026 | Sources: 2020–2025 peer-reviewed literature

---

## 1.1 Annual Plan Architecture

### Evidence Summary
Highly trained and elite marathon runners are encouraged to adopt a **traditional periodization pattern** that shifts from a **pyramidal training intensity distribution** during preparatory and pre-competitive periods toward a **polarized training intensity distribution** during the competitive period (IJSPP Systematic Review, 2022). A 2025 machine-learning study of recreational marathon runners found polarized training produced **30% greater marathon time improvement** (11.3 ± 3.2 min vs. 8.7 ± 2.8 min) over 16 weeks compared to pyramidal. However, **individual response clustering** revealed four groups: polarized responders (31.5%), pyramidal responders (31.9%), dual responders (18.7%), and non-responders (17.9%) — meaning the "best" approach is partially individual.

### Practical Protocol

| Phase | Duration | Approach | Key Focus |
|-------|----------|----------|-----------|
| Base Building | 12–16 weeks | Pyramidal (Z1-heavy) | Aerobic foundation, volume accumulation |
| Build / Pre-Competitive | 8–10 weeks | Pyramidal → shift to Polarized | LT work, marathon pace blocks |
| Race-Specific | 6–8 weeks | Polarized | VO2max intervals + easy running |
| Taper | 2–3 weeks | Low volume, intensity maintained | Freshness, glycogen top-up |
| Recovery / Off-season | 3–4 weeks | Unstructured easy movement | Full physiological recovery |

**For Thomas (sub-3:30 target, 8–12 hrs/week):** Run **1–2 goal marathons per year maximum**. Plan an 18–20 week build from true aerobic base. Use pyramidal (Z1/Z2 dominant) for the first 10 weeks, then shift intensity toward polarized (hard sessions get harder, easy stays easy) for the final 8 weeks.

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Skipping a true base phase and going straight to race-specific work → injury risk rises sharply
- Trying to peak more than twice per year → systemic fatigue, no supracompensation
- Keeping "moderate" intensity (zone 3) too high throughout the year → the "grey zone trap"

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- Stöggl & Sperlich (2014, IJSPP) — original polarized vs. pyramidal RCT
- Machine learning TID personalization (Nature Scientific Reports, 2025)
- Block periodization for recreational runners vs. traditional linear

## 1.2 Weekly Volume & Progression

### Evidence Summary
Monthly training volume is the **most important single predictor of marathon time**, but only when individual session length exceeds ~10 km (Interactions study, 2022; PubMed 36207466). A 2025 Boston Marathon training analysis confirmed that volume and frequency increases in the 16 weeks pre-race are significantly associated with finishing time. The classic "10% rule" has no direct RCT support — current evidence suggests a **safer and more effective approach is 10% per 3 weeks** (one step back every 3–4 weeks), allowing for super-compensation.

### Practical Protocol

| Training Level | Weekly Km Target | Session Min Distance | Down Week Frequency |
|---------------|-----------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Base (current) | 50–65 km/week | 10 km | Every 4th week (−20%) |
| Build Phase | 65–80 km/week | 12 km | Every 3rd week (−25%) |
| Peak Block | 80–95 km/week | 14 km | Every 3rd week (−30%) |
| Taper | 40–50% of peak | Any | Final 2–3 weeks |

**Volume Distribution (10 hrs/week example):**
- 5–6 days running, 1 full rest day
- Long run: 25–35% of weekly volume
- Easy/recovery runs: 50–60%
- Quality sessions: 15–20%

**Cross-training equivalents:** Cycling = ×0.5 km equivalent; Swimming = ×0.8; Elliptical = ×0.7

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Jumping 20–30% in a single week during excitement phases
- Never taking down weeks → HRV depression, stale legs, injury
- Counting every step (errands, etc.) — count structured training only

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- Bragada et al. (2010) on training volume dose-response in trained runners
- PMC12441744 — training volume/frequency changes and Boston performance
- Optimal long-run-to-total-volume ratio in marathon preparation

## 1.3 Intensity Distribution

### Evidence Summary
The **80/20 principle** (≥80% easy, ≤20% hard) is well-supported across observational and interventional literature. Elite marathon data shows the fastest runners achieve high volumes **primarily by increasing Zone 1 time** — not by adding more threshold or tempo work. Critically, the proportion of time in Zone 2 and 3 was **correlated with slower marathon times** in a 2024 performance-level study (PubMed 39616560) — the "moderate zone" does the least for marathon-specific adaptation per training stress unit. The polarized model (Z1 + Z4/5, minimal Z3) outperforms threshold-dominant training for VO2peak in meta-analysis (PMC11329428, 2024).

### Training Zones (for Thomas, approximate)

| Zone | Name | % Max HR | Pace (sub-3:30 target ~5:00/km) | Feel |
|------|------|----------|----------------------------------|------|
| Z1 | Easy/Recovery | 60–72% | 6:00–7:30/km | Fully conversational |
| Z2 | Aerobic | 72–82% | 5:20–6:00/km | Conversational with effort |
| Z3 | Tempo/Threshold | 82–89% | 4:30–5:10/km | 2–3 word answers |
| Z4 | Hard/Interval | 89–95% | 3:55–4:25/km | Cannot speak |
| Z5 | Max effort | 95–100% | <3:55/km | Survival mode |

**Intensity Distribution Target:**

| Phase | Z1 | Z2 | Z3 | Z4–Z5 |
|-------|----|----|----|--------|
| Base | 75% | 15% | 5% | 5% |
| Build | 70% | 10% | 8% | 12% |
| Race-specific | 75% | 5% | 5% | 15% |

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Running easy runs too fast (Z2 creep) — kills the polarization benefit
- Too much Z3 work (the "junk miles" intensity) — feels productive, isn't
- Judging intensity by pace alone on hilly terrain — use HR or perceived effort

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- Seiler & Kjerland (2006) — original intensity distribution work
- Stöggl & Sperlich (2014) — RCT showing polarized superiority for VO2max
- PMC12568352 — recent theoretical framework update (2025)

## 1.4 Key Workouts

### Evidence Summary
Each workout type has a specific physiological target. Research confirms that **long runs (90–180 min)** at easy-to-moderate pace are the primary driver of fat oxidation capacity and mitochondrial density (PMC12082016, 2025). **VO2max intervals** at 95–100% vVO2max (4×4 min format) produce 7.2% VO2max gains vs ~3% for easy running alone (Helgerud et al., original Norwegian data; confirmed in meta-analysis PMC12218014, 2025). **Threshold/tempo work** should be kept to 1×/week and aimed at LT2 pace.

### Workout Protocol Table

| Workout Type | Frequency | Duration/Volume | Target Pace | Physiological Goal |
|-------------|-----------|-----------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Easy run | 4–5×/week | 40–75 min | Z1 (6:00–7:30/km) | Recovery, aerobic base, fat oxidation |
| Long run | 1×/week | 90–180 min | Z1–Z2 (6:00–7:00/km) | Mitochondrial density, glycogen management |
| Tempo / Threshold | 1×/week | 20–40 min continuous | Z3 (4:40–5:00/km) | LT2 adaptation, lactate clearance |
| VO2max intervals | 1×/week | 4–6 × 4 min @ Z4/5 | 3:55–4:20/km | VO2max, cardiac output |
| Marathon pace (MP) | 1×/week in build | 6–16 km within long run | 4:58/km (sub-3:30) | Race-specific neuromuscular / metabolic tuning |
| Strides/neuromuscular | 2–3×/week | 6–10 × 20 sec | Near max, full recovery | Running economy, cadence, form |

**Long run fueling:** Practice race-day nutrition on all runs >90 min. Target 40–60 g carbs/hr from km 30 of training long runs.

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Running long runs too fast (becomes a tempo run, kills recovery capacity)
- Doing VO2max intervals too close to a long run (48+ hrs separation minimum)
- Neglecting strides — the cheapest running economy gain available

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- PMC12082016 — 90 vs 120 min long run effect on marathon determinants
- Helgerud et al. (2007) — 4×4 interval training protocol
- Jack Daniels' vVO2max and interval prescription science
