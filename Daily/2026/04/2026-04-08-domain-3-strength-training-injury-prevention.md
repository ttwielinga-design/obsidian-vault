# Domain 3: Strength Training & Injury Prevention
### Evidence synthesized April 2026 | Sources: 2020–2025 peer-reviewed literature

---

## 3.1 Strength Training for Runners

### Evidence Summary
Heavy strength training (loads ≥85–90% 1RM) consistently outperforms plyometric-only and low-load work for running economy improvement (Track & Field News systematic review 2024). Concurrent training (combining strength + endurance) shows **minor interference effects** in untrained individuals, but in **trained runners this interference is minimal** — VO2max is not blunted and RE improvements stack (Huiberts et al. 2024). The key is timing: strength sessions should be placed on **hard running days (same day)** to concentrate fatigue, keeping easy days truly easy. **2 sessions per week** is the sweet spot for recreational marathon runners — 1 during base week, reduced to 1 in build, and 0 in the final taper weeks.

### Strength Session Design

| Phase | Frequency | Focus | Example Exercises |
|-------|-----------|-------|-------------------|
| Base (weeks 1–12) | 2×/week | Heavy compound strength (neural) | Heavy squats, RDL, step-ups, hip thrusts |
| Build (weeks 13–18) | 1–2×/week | Strength + explosive | Strength base + box jumps, bounding |
| Race-specific (weeks 19–22) | 1×/week | Maintenance only | Reduced volume, keep intensity |
| Taper (weeks 23–24+) | 0 | Allow fresh legs | None |

### Exercise Priority Table (Highest Transfer to Running)

| Exercise | Primary Running Benefit | Sets × Reps | Load |
|----------|------------------------|-------------|------|
| Heavy back squat / goblet squat | Quad/glute power, RE | 3–4 × 4–6 | ≥80% 1RM |
| Romanian deadlift (RDL) | Posterior chain, Achilles loading | 3–4 × 4–6 | ≥80% 1RM |
| Single-leg press / Bulgarian split squat | Unilateral power transfer | 3 × 6–8 | Heavy |
| Hip thrust | Glute max activation, propulsion | 3 × 8 | Heavy |
| Single-leg calf raise (eccentric focus) | Achilles tendon resilience | 3 × 12–15 | BW + pack |
| Copenhagen adduction plank | Hip adductor, ITBS prevention | 3 × 10–12/side | BW |
| Nordic hamstring curl | Hamstring eccentric, injury prevention | 2–3 × 6 | BW |
| Pallof press / plank variations | Core stability, transfer efficiency | 3 × 10–15 | Cable/band |

**Key principle: Prioritize heavy strength (neural adaptations) over hypertrophy work.** The goal is improved motor unit recruitment and tendon stiffness, not muscle mass. Mass = weight to carry.

### Concurrent Training Timing

| Day | Running | Strength |
|-----|---------|----------|
| Monday | Quality (intervals) | Strength in PM (same day OK) |
| Tuesday | Easy | — |
| Wednesday | Threshold | Strength in PM |
| Thursday | Easy/Rest | — |
| Friday | Easy | — |
| Saturday | Long run | — |
| Sunday | Rest / easy | — |

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Doing high-volume leg strength on easy run days → kills recovery
- Pure machine work (leg press, leg extension) without single-leg work — poor specificity
- Stopping strength training at race-specific phase — RE gains deteriorate in 4–6 weeks

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- Blagrove, Howatson & Hayes (2018, Sports Med) — strength training for endurance athletes
- Huiberts et al. (2024) — concurrent training interference in trained athletes
- PMC11986186 (2025) — injury prevention scoping review

## 3.2 Injury Prevention Protocols

### Evidence Summary
Marathon training injury incidence is **30–50% over a full training cycle** for recreational runners. The top predictors of injury are: **previous injury** (strongest predictor), rapid load increases, and insufficient recovery. A 2025 injury prevention scoping review (PMC11986186) found that **load management is the most evidence-supported intervention**, outperforming prehab exercises, orthotics, and stretching alone. The AOPT updated its Achilles tendinopathy clinical practice guidelines in 2024 with 9 identified risk factors including prior lower limb tendinopathy, cold-weather training, and decreased plantar flexor strength.

### Injury Risk + Prehab Protocol Table

| Injury | Prevalence in Marathon | Primary Cause | Evidence-Based Prevention |
|--------|----------------------|---------------|--------------------------|
| IT Band Syndrome | ~10% of all running injuries | Hip abductor weakness, rapid volume increase | Hip abductor strengthening (HAS exercises) × 3/week |
| Plantar Fasciitis | ~8% | High arch stress, tight Achilles, sudden increase | Calf stretching + eccentric calf raises; heel drop protocol |
| Achilles Tendinopathy | ~11% | Sudden load spike, cold training, weak plantar flexors | Eccentric calf raises (Alfredson), heavy slow resistance (HSR) |
| Medial tibial stress (shin splints) | ~10% | Bony load, overpronation, hard surfaces | Gradual progression, hip and calf strengthening |
| Runner's knee (PFPS) | ~17% | Hip weakness, overstriding, quad weakness | VMO exercises, hip abductor work, cadence adjustment |
| Stress fractures | ~3% | Bone stress, RED-S, sudden volume jump | Nutrition (calcium, D3), progressive load, monitor bone markers |

### Weekly Prehab Routine (15 min, every running day)

**Pre-run (5 min activation):**
1. Hip circles × 10/side
2. Banded clamshells × 15/side
3. Single-leg glute bridge × 12/side
4. Ankle circles + calf pumps × 15

**Post-run (10 min maintenance):**
1. Eccentric calf raise off step × 15 × 3 (slow descent 3 sec)
2. Copenhagen adduction plank × 30s/side
3. Hip flexor stretch × 60s/side
4. Plantar fascia stretch (foot dorsiflexion) × 30s/foot

### Early Warning Signs (Act 2–4 Weeks Before Injury Gets Serious)

| Signal | Meaning | Response |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Consistent post-run stiffness lasting >30 min | Tendon loading stress | Reduce tempo/intervals by 30%; add eccentric work |
| Sharp pain at start of run that warms up | Tendinopathy or bony stress | See physio; do not run through bone-site pain |
| Unilateral leg fatigue asymmetry >15% in strides | Compensation pattern | Strengthen the weaker side; check gait |
| HRV trending down 3+ consecutive days | Systemic overload | Cut training 20–30%; prioritize sleep |
| Gradual knee pain when descending stairs | PFPS developing | Reduce downhill, add VMO work |

### Evidence on Footwear, Orthotics, Foam Rolling
- **Orthotics:** Weak evidence for injury prevention in runners without existing deformity or pain
- **Barefoot/minimalist:** Increases calf/Achilles load — can be useful as gradual conditioning but not recommended as a switch during marathon training
- **Foam rolling:** Improves short-term ROM and reduces soreness; no strong evidence for injury prevention per se — but zero cost to include post-run

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Ignoring early warning signals and training through pain
- Relying on orthotics without addressing the underlying muscle weakness
- Zero prehab until something hurts → reactive, not preventive

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- PMC11986186 (2025) — running injury prevention scoping review
- JOSPT Achilles CPG revision (2024)
- Plantar fasciitis CPG revision (JOSPT 2023)

## 3.3 Plyometrics & Neuromuscular Training

### Evidence Summary
Plyometric training improves running economy by **1–3% in trained runners** through enhanced neuromuscular stiffness and tendon elastic energy return (Frontiers Sports Active Living 2021). The key mechanism is **muscle-tendon unit stiffness** — stiffer tendons store and release more elastic energy per stride. Evidence supports **low-volume, high-quality plyometric work** (not high-volume bounding typical in track athletes). **Base phase is the correct time for heavier plyometric loading** — race-specific phase shifts to lighter, faster neuromuscular work. Injury risk is low when built progressively from a strength base.

### Plyometric Protocol Table

| Phase | Exercise | Volume | Frequency | Notes |
|-------|----------|--------|-----------|-------|
| Base (foundation) | Pogos, double-leg hops, skipping | 3 × 10–15 contacts | 2×/week | Start bilateral; build slowly |
| Build | Single-leg hops, bounding, jump squats | 3 × 8–12 contacts/leg | 2×/week | Add unilateral complexity |
| Race-specific | Fast high-knees, quick strides, ankle stiffness drills | 2 × 20 m × 4–6 | 1–2×/week | Speed > volume; pre-quality sessions |

**Key plyometric exercises for marathon runners:**
1. Ankle pogo jumps (minimal knee bend, ankle spring) — 3 × 20 contacts
2. Single-leg pogos — 3 × 10/leg
3. Skip A-drill with exaggerated arm drive — 3 × 20 m
4. Bounding (long, powerful strides) — 4 × 30 m in base phase
5. Drop jumps (step off box, reactive landing) — 3 × 6 in build phase

### Red Flags / Common Mistakes
- Doing plyometrics without strength base → Achilles/plantar overload
- High contact counts (>100/session) for a beginner to plyos
- Doing heavy bounding the day before or after long run

### Further Research Rabbit Holes
- Beattie et al. (2017) — plyometric training for endurance performance
- Saunders et al. (2006) — plyometric training and running economy
- Frontiers Sports Active Living systematic review (2021)
