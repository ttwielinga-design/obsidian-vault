## 4.8 The Influence of Loyalty/Exclusivity on Project Division by Hotel Chains
Hotel chains and Third-Party White Label Operators (TPWLOs) form important agreements based on exclusivity and loyalty. These elements have a big influence on how hotel businesses divide up their projects in addition to shaping partnerships. However, the dynamics of these exclusive partnerships are intricate. Although exclusivity raises investor confidence and credibility, it can also prevent TPWLOs from taking advantage of expansion possibilities and strategic flexibility. These collaborations are made more difficult by the inclinations and tactical goals of hotel chains, which can have a range of effects depending on how much exclusivity is maintained. This leads to the question addressed in this subchapter: To what extent does loyalty/exclusivity between TPWLOs and chains influence the division of projects by the chain? According to a response from an investor, the loyalty and exclusivity of TPWLO and hotel chain agreements considerably enhance investors' comfort levels (INV_03). This reputation, which stems from extensive screening by major companies, is critical for investor confidence. Hotel chains frequently prefer exclusive contracts, which correspond with their strategic interests and ensure dedicated partnership, as indicated by a hotel chain that was interviewed (HCS_03). However, exclusivity might result in limits. Restricting to one brand, as a consultant observed, may limit expansion opportunities, making it harder for TPWLOs to compete with operators who have a larger selection of agreements (CNS_04). As a result, although exclusivity secures early projects and investor confidence, combining it with strategic flexibility is critical for long-term success.
### 4.8.1 Exclusivity in Relationships
The loyalty and exclusivity in partnerships between TPWLOs and hotel chains greatly enhance the credibility and comfort of investors. As an investor noted, "I think at the outset it helps you because you get kind of the most street credit and by you being aligned with a major brand and being selected by them for whatever a territory or a joint development agreement gives the comfort to the investor" (INV_03, Page 5). A major brand selecting TPWLO implies that it has been thoroughly vetted, which gives investors confidence. Other hotel chains, as mentioned by a representative of a major hotel chain, prefer exclusive contracts because they align with their strategic interests, ensuring TPWLOs' commitment to collaboration and focused efforts (HCS_03). Then he can choose which brand he wants to work with. If we go in, we would love to go in exclusively with him or someone else, so he can leave that option open, which is not in our favor (HCS_03). However, this exclusivity can also lead to limitations. A consultant highlighted that restricting to one brand might limit growth opportunities, as TPWLOs may struggle to compete with operators who have a broader range of partnerships (CNS_04). "The thing is when you limit yourself on one brand you know then you can only grow with this one brand and all of your competition mainly has a brighter basket you know and then you can't react on that" (CNS_04, Page 9).
### 4.8.2 Partnership Dynamics
TPWLO's partnership dynamics with hotel chains are impacted by several elements, such as financial incentives, brand choices, and market competitiveness. As a competitor put it, "You just pick the brand based on the brand you think fits" (COM_01, Page 8). A hotel chain representative drew attention to how different contract terms—like rent indexation and preopening fees—affect partnerships' appeal and vary among brands: We observe variations in indexation, or how the index influences the rent and how inflation modifies the rent over the term of the agreement (HCS_03). Furthermore, as a different hotel chain noted, "I have several partners who are just very loyal. They are satisfied with how it goes and they see no reason to change (hotel chains)" (HCS_01, Page 12). Although this dedication may lead to less flexibility, it guarantees a steady and predictable collaboration atmosphere.
### 4.8.3 Strategic Flexibility
TPWLOs must exercise strategic flexibility to survive in the cutthroat hospitality industry. TPWLOs can take advantage of various opportunities and adjust to changing market situations when they are flexible. This was illustrated through the following quote by an investor, "so it's not even to be against your main partner, but it just creates opportunity" (INV_03, Page 5). However, as a big hotel chain notes, "I think if you have a lot of companies that you're dealing with, you may get confused" (HSC_05, Page 8), this flexibility needs to be weighed with the potential complexity and confusion of handling many brand standards. As noted by a developer, having a portfolio of brands or the capacity to switch between them can give an advantage over competitors, but doing so also calls for careful management of brand standards and market positioning. "I have seen that a few white label operators used to only work with one brand. Thus, they are now expanding their portfolio and looking, for instance, to have multiple chain brands in one city because they know they can then have the potential business, even though they were only able to or willing to work, for example, with Marriott or only willing to work with Accor or very dedicated to Hilton" (DEV_02, Page 4).
