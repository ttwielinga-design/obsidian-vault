Business Process Improvement Project – Student instruction 
 
Purpose of the project 
Do you recognize starting a new job and after only two weeks understanding that some colleagues are 
not happy with the way certain things run in the organization or clients complain about aspects of the 
service or that other departments seem to be doing similar things but in a completely different way? 
These observations are indications that certain ways of working can be optimized. Optimizing a business 
process is exactly what we envision with this business process improvement project.  
What is a Business Process? 
A process is a series of repeatable steps, tasks, and decisions that accomplishes an organizational 
objective. Sometimes known as workflows, processes are everywhere in an organization and come in 
many forms: 
• 
Simple, like fulfilling an order. 
• 
Complex, like developing a new product. 
• 
Short-term, like onboarding an employee, vendor, or partner. 
• 
Ongoing, like regulatory compliance. 
• 
Function-specific, like proposal management. 
• 
Industry-specific, like guest journey management. 
Some processes, such as billing, can exist within a single department. Other processes, such as strategic 
sourcing, run throughout an entire enterprise. They may even extend beyond the organization and 
across the value chain, as is the case with supply chain management. 
Box 1 
Business processes can be categorized into different buckets, with the most common three as follows: 
1. Operational processes. Also called primary processes, these processes deal with the core business and 
value chain and deliver value to the customer by helping to produce a product or service. Operational 
processes represent essential business activities that accomplish business objectives such as generating 
revenue. Examples of this include the following: 
a. taking customer orders 
b. processing product payments 
c. managing bank accounts 
d. operating (aspects of) the guest journey 
e. cleaning a room 
f. processing F&B orders 
2. Supporting processes. Also known as secondary processes, these involve back-office processes within the 
business functions that keep the organization running. One key difference between operational and 
supporting processes is that supporting processes do not directly provide value to customers. Examples of 
supporting processes include the following: 
a. Administrating transactions (Accounting) 
b. Administration of sick leave (HR management) 
c. HACCP registration (workplace safety) 
3. Management processes. These processes measure, monitor and control the activities related to business 
procedures and systems. Like supporting processes, management processes do not provide value directly to 
the customers. Some examples of management processes include the following: 
a. Daily management meeting (internal communications) 
b. Dispute settlement mechanism (governance)

Relevance of business Process Improvement? 
Business process improvement is an essential element in Business Process Management (BPM). BPM is a 
structured approach to improving the processes organizations use to get work done, serve their 
customers and generate business value. A business process is an activity or set of activities that helps 
accomplish an organization's goals.  
 
Box 2 
In general, the primary objective of BPI is to continuously improve the performance of processes 
within an organization. By applying business process improvement, organizations can identify 
bottlenecks, friction, inefficiencies and other problems within their processes and take corrective 
actions, which can result in the following: 
• 
cut costs; 
• 
eliminate redundant efforts; 
•

Business process Improvement Project: 6 Steps to take 
 
Your BPI-project should result in a BPM lifecycle existing of 6 steps, comparable to the one depicted in 
figure 2. After identifying a process with ample room for improvement in step 1, steps 2 through 6 
should be executed perpetually on a regular basis, as indicated with the arrows. 
 
 
 Figure 2: The Business Process Management Lifecycle (Dumas et al., 2018, p. 23)2 
 
The Business Process Management Lifecycle includes the following phases: 
1. Process identification: Identify a single process that needs improvement no matter how small. 
Focus on one process that needs (immediate) improvement, reach out to stakeholders and team 
members to determine which tasks and activities cause frustrations, problems, seem to be 
unnecessarily challenging, does not add value et cetera (see box 2). Gather their recommendations 
on how the process (of that task/activity) can be improved. Choose one process to optimize that 
will show immediate result (ROI), regardless how small it is. Ensure that you can list more 
indicators/performance criteria that proof the relevance and urgency for improving this particular 
process other than just the input of the stakeholder/team member.  Small optimization projects 
can still show significant results, provide easy wins for stakeholders and gain buy-in for 
implementing the change. 
2. Process discovery: Once a process has been identified for improvement, define the purpose of 
optimizing the process. Set the overall goals as well as smaller immediately measurable goals (KPI), 
and devise a plan to proceed with the next steps. Have a clear understanding of what optimization 
will achieve -- for example, financial savings, resource savings or time savings. You can relate to

Clearly communicate the goals of your process improvement to executives and stakeholders so 
everyone is on the same page and in agreement. In addition, visually map the business process 
using suitable business process modelling methods3. These tools can provide an easily understood, 
standardized way of defining how a current process flows through different departments, the 
various steps within the process, and the components within processes that run sequentially or in 
parallel. 
Creating a business process map involves the following five steps: 
1. Identify the process to be documented. 
2. Document significant points, such as the start and end points of the process and each task in 
between those two points. As part of the process documentation, indicate decision points; these 
are points where a yes or no decision is required, with each answer leading the process in either 
one of two directions in the document process. 
3. Gather information. You must observe the process or gather information from participants and 
stakeholders in the process to create an accurate understanding of what tasks or activities are 
involved in the process and the sequence in which they happen. 
4. Involve participants and stakeholders by asking them to help identify inefficiencies and 
redundancies. 
5. Map the process, keeping information clear and simple. 
 
3. 
Process analysis: Process mapping helps analyse the current process and locate areas of 
inefficiency or ineffectiveness that can be improved and optimized. Also use the feedback provided 
by stakeholders to analyse which steps in the process take the most time, create bottlenecks, cause 
communication breakdowns, fail to provide value and can be reworked or eliminated, for example, 
by making some tasks conditional, integrating certain tasks, adapting data permissions or 
automating manual tasks.  
4. 
Process redesign: Be sure the process redesign will meet the established business goals and 
significantly impact the objected improvement goals (e.g. process efficiency, resource savings and 
ROI). Make sure you consider how to measure the impact and provide recommendations on where 
the process can be improved while still in the pilot stage. 
5. 
Process implementation: Testing and quality assurance are equally important steps to ensure the 
new process will work. Optimized business processes can often be hindered by unforeseen 
circumstances. Testing the entire process before full implementation helps avoid re-examination 
and iterative redesign after deployment. 
6. 
Process monitoring and controlling: Once fully implemented, measure the new process for 
efficiency and effectiveness. As market conditions and business operations change, so do business 
processes. Therefore, a business process requires continuous evaluation and monitoring to ensure 
it is delivering more value compared to the old process. Gather feedback from stakeholders and 
monitor the process for any new pain points that need to be addressed.

What is expected of your BPI project during your internship? 
 
You are asked to identify a small scale project related to your daily activities during the internship. You 
should ask yourself: “Looking at all the processes I am involved in within my department, which business 
process can/should be improved?”  
Based on measurable objectives you are asked to evaluate and improve an existing business process in 
your department (or organization).  
Some processes in an organization are quite complex and consist out of multiple smaller processes. If 
that is the case then first identify all the sub processes that are part of this complex process. After that, 
you can determine which sub process is most relevant to you to improve, think for example about your 
personal interest, the (urgent) need for change or the impact an improvement will have for the 
organization.  
At the moment that you explore the web, you will find various ways to map business processes or 
determine the quality of these processes. These insights can support you in identifying processes (or sub 
processes) within your organization for which you can contribute significantly.  
The aim is to put all knowledge and skills you gained during your study into practice by giving a hands-
on advice to your internship company.  
Underpinning your decisions with solid analyses is key. In the various steps of the project you should 
consider the following (also refer to the general assessment form rubrics BPI-project on Moodle): 
1) The relevance of the business process that you aim to improve. 
2) Which stakeholders are actors in and/or affected by the identified business process? 
3) The performance criteria that can assess the current quality of the process and at the same time 
measure the effects of your improvements. 
4) How the suggested improvements were developed: which methods and which people were 
involved 
5) How you managed to convince the organization of the quality of your newly designed improved 
business process? 
6) How you implemented the improvement and how you determined the level of improvement? 
7) Your final advice to the company. 
The steps to take to improve a business process in a sustainable way can be found in figure 2 above. You 
should aim at finalizing the business process improvement by the reporting deadline (see Moodle: Time 
line management internship): taking all 6 steps in figure 2. This works best if you involve your colleagues 
in identifying the potential business processes that have a need for improvement and together 
determine the success criteria and options for improvement.  
The more relevant your improvement project is to your stakeholders, the bigger the chance it will be 
permanently implemented, and you can actually complete all 6 steps of the business process 
improvement project.  
Finishing all 6 steps provides you with the great experience of making a managerial contribution to the 
internship organisation. Along with a curious and critical attitude, this project also requires an attitude 
of pro-activeness and creating support within the organisation. This attitude may make the difference 
between being able to taking steps 5 and 6, or not. 
However, in case you can’t apply step 5 and 6 by yourself, as a minimum result you should be able to 
provide I.) an implementation plan for the actual business process improvement, including II.) an 
instruction how to evaluate the effect of these improvements, based on measurable objectives.  
This means that you need to have at least finished steps 1 through 4 by yourself, and have described in 
what way steps 5 and 6 can be executed based on the content of your report.

Time to be spent 
The time spent on this project is approximately 80 hours (approx. 4 hours per week). You can ask your 
internship company to grant you the time to work on this project during office hours as the result is 
directly applicable to the daily process. Please, discuss this with your company supervisor and use the

which you participate yourself during your internship can contribute to efficiency and saving time when 
doing this project. Throughout the internship you follow the cycle described in figure 2 and you
