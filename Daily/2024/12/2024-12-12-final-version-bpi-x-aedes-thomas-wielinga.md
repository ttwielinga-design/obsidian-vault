1. Introduction 
Aedes, a leading property investor and hospitality company in Amsterdam, has 
operated in real estate for nearly three decades (Aedes, 2024). With its first own branded 
hotel launched two years ago, Aedes is positioned for growth, evident in its two large-scale 
hotel projects (Aedes, 2024). To support expansion, the company is transitioning from a 
small-scale family-run to a more structured mid-scale organization, scaling its workforce 
(OPS_01, 2024; OPS_02, 2024). This shift presents both opportunities and challenges, 
including optimizing communication between Development and Hospitality teams in the 
hotel-building process (OPS_01, 2024; DEV_03, 2024). Clear communication ensures 
efficient, timely, and budgeted project completion (DEV_01, 2024; DEV_03, 2024; DEV_04, 
2024). 
Hotel development follows five phases: Concept, Planning, Building, Interior & Pre-
Opening, and Operations—this also applies to Aedes (Autin, 2020; Piliouris, 2020; Noordzy 
& Whitfield, 2021; DEV_02, 2024). Each phase is critical to ensuring timely completion, 
alignment with brand identity, and financial performance (Piliouris, 2020; Noordzy & 
Whitfield, 2021). 
In larger hotel chains (e.g., Marriott), the planning phase involves clear divisions of 
responsibility between internal and external teams (AltexSoft, 2024). For example, design 
guidelines, licensing, and project management are handled by a mix of employees and ECs 
(Martin, 2024; AltexSoft, 2024). Contracts like TSA and handover documents ensure 
accountability and adherence to timelines (ContractsCounsel, 2024). Aedes, however, follows 
a unique approach (OPS_01, 2024; DEV_03, 2024). As both developer and operator, its 
hospitality team is involved early in the process, tightly linking development and operations 
(Aedes, 2024; DEV_02, 2024; OPS_01, 2024; OPS_03, 2024). 
While this integration enhances communication and goal alignment, it also extends 
feedback cycles, blurs roles, and reduces accountability without formal contracts (OPS_01, 
2024; DEV_05, 2024). 
Addressing these challenges is crucial for Aedes' project success (PMI, 2021). By 
emphasizing accountability, process structure, and clear roles, Aedes can minimize delays, 
improve productivity, and create an efficient framework for future developments (PMI, 
2021).

2. Background and Process Identification 
Founded in 1995, Aedes wants to create spaces with heart and soul—leaving a lasting 
impact on people, culture, and the environment. Guided by a vision to renew urban areas by 
blending history and sustainable innovation, Aedes designs vibrant spaces that endure across 
generations. Its strategy emphasizes urban redevelopment, sustainability, and relaxed 
hospitality, merging cultural heritage with modern needs into timeless narratives (Aedes, 
2024). 
Aedes develops visually appealing and sustainable buildings. From luxury hotels and 
apartments to innovative workspaces, its projects inspire human connection, enrich those who 
visit or reside there, and align with long-term sustainability goals (Aedes, 2024). 
Unlike larger hotel chains, which often outsource aspects of the development process, 
Aedes in-sources operations, involving hospitality teams from the earliest development 
phases. This ensures properties reflect their core values and promotes stronger collaboration 
between design and operational teams, delivering spaces that meet the highest standards of 
hospitality and sustainability (Aedes, 2024). 
2.1 Current State of the Hotel Building Process 
The hotel development process comprises five phases: Concept, Planning, Building, 
Interior & Pre-Opening, and Operations (Autin, 2020; Piliouris, 2020; Noordzy & Whitfield, 
2021; Madelon Interview). Each phase involves essential activities and stakeholders critical 
to project success.

The Concept phase assesses the project's feasibility, covering market research,

hotel is operationally and guest-ready (Piliouris, 2020; Noordzy & Whitfield, 2021). 
In the final phase, the hotel opens, generating revenue for the first time (Noordzy & 
Whitfield, 2021). The hospitality and operations teams can now work to meet service and 
financial expectations.

resource costs (Aladpoosh, 2012; DEV_04, 2024). Furthermore, documenting and improving

3.1 As-Is Flowchart 
The VO-process involves seven distinct stages, lasting approximately 6 months for 
the Amstel 111 project (BNA & NLingenieurs, 2014; DEV_03, 2024). These stages were 
verified with both the development and hospitality departments at Aedes to ensure alignment 
and consistency during interviews (DEV_03, 2024; OPS_01, 2024). Below is a summary of 

12 
 
these stages, with estimated time allocations derived from stakeholder input3 (DEV_05, 2024; 
OPS_03, 2024).  
Stage 1: 15-20% 
When the VO-process starts, the project team's first stage is to evaluate the site and its 
surrounding environmental factors. The goal is to ensure the design integrates well with the 
physical and contextual environment. This phase sets the foundation by addressing site 
conditions, regulations, and potential challenges that could impact the overall design. 
Stage 2: 15-20% 
The second stage involves utilizing the SO and creating the preliminary architectural 
concept, including floor plans, sections, and visual design elements. This is done by utilizing 
the company's internal database of information, such as ICCF4 and additional DNR 
documentation.  
Stage 3: 15-20% 
In stage three, the preliminary structural and installation plans are developed. This 
includes key technical and infrastructure elements like plumbing, electrical systems, and 
other installations critical for the building's functionality. The design should be prepared for a 
detailed review, but it should remain flexible for changes based on upcoming feedback. 
Stage 4: 20-25% 
The fourth stage is a key "Go/No-Go" point in the process. After the initial structural 
and installation plans are complete, there is a review and feedback round with, among others, 
the hospitality team. Their input is crucial in determining whether the design can progress or 
needs further revisions. If significant changes are required, the project needs to revert to stage 
2 for adjustments. If the feedback is positive and only minor changes are necessary, the 
process moves on to the next stage. 
Stage 5: 15-20% 
Once the hospitality review is completed, and a go-ahead is given, the development 
team works on refining the functional and spatial layout of the project. This stage focuses on 
creating the functional and spatial layout, ensuring the design supports efficient operations, 
guest comfort, and compliance with design standards. 
Stage 6: 20-25%

Stage six involves a final review with all relevant stakeholders. Here, stakeholders

If the feedback requires small adjustments, the process may revert to stage 5. However, if

Additionally, three matrices were created by different department heads, the HH, HD, 
and Project Director (Appendices 3-5). Two comparisons should be pointed out.  
First, a comparison of the HD’s RACI with the theoretical RACI highlights key 
discrepancies in Accountability and stakeholder engagement. In stage 1, their RACI assigns 
dual Accountability to development and the owner, whereas the theoretical model designates 
development as Accountable, and the owner as Consulted. This dual accountability risks 
inefficiency and conflicts in decision-making, particularly regarding site integration (Kerzner, 
2017). Stage 4 further excludes ECs in their RACI, undermining their role in ensuring 
technical feasibility and project alignment (Macmillan et al., 2001). In stage 6, they assign 

16 
 
both Client and Development as Responsible, creating ambiguity in stakeholder consultations 
and risking delays (Olander & Landin, 2008). 
Second, the HH’s RACI diverges from the theoretical model, notably in role clarity. 
Stage 4 in their RACI gives hospitality a dual Accountable/Responsible role, reducing 
development to Informed. This contradicts the theoretical model, which assigns Hospitality as 
Responsible and Development as Consulted to ensure technical input (Freeman, 1984). In 
stage 5, they give Development a Consulted/Responsible role instead of making them solely 
Accountable, leading to potential delays and conflicting priorities (Kerzner, 2017). Lastly, in 
stage 6, they assign Accountability to Hospitality rather than designating Development as 
Responsible and Hospitality as Informed. This role reversal risks operational concerns 
overshadowing technical expertise, jeopardizing long-term project outcomes (Olander & 
Landin, 2008). 
These discrepancies highlight accountability and stakeholder management issues. 
Dual responsibilities, such as HD’s stage 6 and HH’s stage 1, dilute accountability and hinder 
task completion (Kerzner, 2017). Similarly, assigning dual Accountable roles, as seen in 
HD’s stage 1, creates conflicts, particularly when development and the owner disagree on key 
decisions (Macmillan et al., 2001). 
Excluding EC, as in HD’s stage 4, neglects critical expertise, while HH’s 
overemphasis on hospitality accountability risks overshadowing technical priorities. 
Resolving these issues requires aligning RACIs with the theoretical framework to establish 
clarity, collaboration, and balanced stakeholder engagement. This alignment enhances 
decision-making, feedback processes, and project success (Freeman, 1984; Olander & 
Landin, 2008). 
3.4 As-Is Conclusion 
 
The analysis of the VO-process reveals inefficiencies and risks, including 
communication breakdowns, overlapping responsibilities, and inadequate stakeholder 
integration. Specifically: 
• Feedback loops during stages such as the hospitality review and stakeholder 
consultations create bottlenecks, delaying progress and reducing project efficiency 
(DEV_01, 2024; DEV_05, 2024). 
• The RACI analysis identifies dual accountability and unclear role assignments, such 
as assigning both development and owner accountability for site integration. This 

17 
 
ambiguity results in conflicting decisions and delays in task execution (Kerzner, 2017; 
Macmillan et al., 2001). 
• Excluding ECs during critical stages, such as in Task 4, increases the risk of technical 
oversights and misalignment with project goals (Olander & Landin, 2008). 
• Misplaced accountability, particularly in the HH’s RACI, prioritizes operational 
concerns over technical requirements, jeopardizing long-term project outcomes 
(Freeman, 1984). 
This exposes Aedes to risks i.e., extended project timelines, increased resource 
consumption, and reduced alignment with operational objectives. To mitigate these risks, the 
VO-process must be aligned with both theoretical and real-world applicability. This should 
be done by combining both in a To-Be situation as will be done in the next chapter. 
4. Desired Situation 
 
This chapter explains how the VO-process can be improved to increase its efficiency 
for everyone involved. A new flowchart, SIPOC, and RACI have been co-created to fix 
issues in the old process. These changes focus on doing the important work earlier, making 
roles clearer, and improving communication between development, hospitality, and other 
stakeholders. The goal is to ensure the VO-process in future runs smoother and meets 
everyone’s needs.

1. At the start of each VO phase, HD, and HH should revise the timeline to

4.2 To-Be SIPOC 
 
Focusing on the desired SIPOC what is seen is similar to the Flowchart, most of the 
workload has been frontloaded to the start of the process instead of spreading out. 
Furthermore, even though in theory there are the same amount of feedback cycles, in 
practice, there are fewer because the need for reiteration has been reduced—all the feedback 
cycles in the VO follow after VO 1.0 is complete.

Even though this SIPOC is made with a quite high abstraction level, the flow of the 
process is clear. While Development and Hospitality teams are both actively involved from 
the beginning, delivering their inputs for stage 1, Development’s roles become more concrete 
as the process progresses. In the first stages, HD creates the VO 1.0, ensuring the PoRs, 
project specifics, financing, P&L, and ICCF are kept in mind. The clarity of input from ECs 
and the development team helps in building a solid foundation for this first version of the 
VO. 
The feedback loops are more optimized in this structure because most of the critical 
decisions, such as technical design and initial structural plans, are addressed early (when 
creating VO 1.0), which reduces the need for back-and-forth revisions later. Frontloading 
allows for a smoother transition into the later stages where only (necessary) refinements are 
made. As the process nears completion, the VO package is reviewed and signed off with 
(hopefully) minimal revisions. 
In essence, this SIPOC highlights the importance of clear delineation of roles between 
HD and HH, as Womack and Jones (2010) emphasize an efficient, frontloaded approach that

maintaining oversight of finances and strategic objectives (Freeman, 1984). 
During the second stage, HD remains Accountable/Responsible for reviewing the VO, 
ensuring adherence to technical, legal, and developmental standards (ISO 19650, 2018). ECs 
are Consulted for technical validation, the owner remains Responsible for budget and strategy 
alignment, and Hospitality is Consulted to confirm operational feasibility (Olander & Landin, 
2008). 
In the third stage, HH assumes Accountable/Responsible roles, ensuring VO content 
aligns with operational and guest experience goals (Freeman, 1984). Development is 
Consulted for technical feasibility, the owner is Responsible/Consulted for financial and 
operational alignment, and ECs are Informed as their role diminishes with a shift to 
operational/hospitality focus. 
In the final stage, the owner becomes Accountable, approving or reevaluating the VO 
package, reflecting their authority over financial commitments and project success (Kerzner,

Consulted to confirm design feasibility, and ECs remain Informed as their role concludes 
(ISO 19650, 2018). 
4.3.2 Addressing Owner Priorities 
The owner’s perspective (appendix 6) emphasizes their final authority over the VO-
process, supported by input from HH and HD. Assigning HD as Accountable for stage 1 
ensures execution oversight, while HH takes Accountability for content-driven tasks like 
stage 3 to reflect their operational expertise (Freeman, 1984). The owner is Accountable for 
final sign-off, acknowledging their financial stake and authority to realign priorities as 
needed (Olander & Landin, 2008). 
This RACI balances collaboration with clear accountability. It prevents overlapping 
roles, ensuring tasks like VO creation (stage 1) and content review (stage 3) are handled by 
specific stakeholders based on expertise. This structure reduces delays and misaligned 
priorities often caused by shared roles (Kerzner, 2017). By aligning with the owner's 
expectations and theoretical best practices, the RACI promotes communication, 
accountability, and decision-making, creating a workable To-Be framework. 
4.4 To-Be Conclusion 
 
This chapter improved the VO-process by introducing a To-Be flowchart, SIPOC, and 
RACI, addressing unclear roles, poor communication, and fragmented feedback loops. The 
updates prioritize frontloading tasks, streamlining collaboration, and reducing revisions, 
aligning with Womack and Jones’s (2010) focus on business value and waste elimination. 
The To-Be flowchart ensures early feedback loops, with documentation tested and 
refined alongside stakeholders before transitioning to operations. The SIPOC framework 
clarifies roles and responsibilities, enhancing transparency and accountability (Kerzner, 2017; 
Macmillan et al., 2001). 
The proposed RACI adjustments distribute tasks clearly, in-line with Olander and 
Landin (2008) and Kerzner (2017). This structure minimizes confusion, maintains strategic 
oversight for the owner, strengthening project outcomes (Freeman, 1984). 
These refinements create a robust VO that improves communication, collaboration, 
and project delivery. The system ensures a smooth transition from Development to 
Operations, maintaining strategic alignment and operational efficiency (ISO 19650, 2018; 
Womack & Jones, 2010).

Appendix 1. Detailed Analysis of Key Findings from the VO-Step 
Lack of Defined Criteria for Project Completion 
Unclear standards for determining when projects meet acceptable benchmarks cause 
endless refinements. This issue plagued Durgerdam, where undefined design standards 
triggered repeated revisions and delayed a brand identity shift. Missing benchmarks include 
design standards, which fail to specify requirements for aesthetics, functionality, or quality, 
and brand identity, which lacks explicit guidelines on embodying the brand’s image and 
values. Without clear criteria, teams face uncertainty, wasting time and resources. 
Ineffective Accountability Management 
Attempts to establish accountability falter due to misalignment with Aedes’ culture 
and insufficiently structured processes. The absence of formal contracts weakens 
commitment to deadlines, while broad employee roles create ambiguity, complicating 
responsibility assignments. Discrepancies in RACI matrices, where roles (Responsible, 
Accountable, Consulted, Informed) are inconsistently interpreted across departments, further 
confusing tasks. For instance, overlapping accountabilities between Hospitality and 
Development impair operations. These issues hinder task ownership, causing delays and 
reduced performance. 
Cultural Misalignment with Structured Processes 
Aedes’ clan culture fosters collaboration and a family-run atmosphere but clashes 
with the need for structured processes as the organization scales. The lack of formalized 
procedures and vague role definitions undermines efficiency and accountability during 
growth. 
Ambiguity in Responsibilities and Authorities 
Unclear responsibilities and decision-making authorities significantly impact project 
efficiency. Broad employee roles blur accountability, while the absence of formal agreements 
fosters uncertainty regarding decision-making authority. Conflicting RACI matrices 
exacerbate the problem, with departments interpreting roles differently, causing confusion 
and overlap. For example, unclear documentation of changes to the Program of Requirements 
(PoR) delays progress and leads to mismanagement. 
Consequences for Processes and Stakeholders 
These challenges severely affect internal processes and customer satisfaction. Endless 
project refinements waste time and resources. Delays and inefficiencies stem from unclear

responsibilities, extended feedback cycles, and poor communication. Increased costs, as seen 
in Durgerdam’s delayed brand identity shift, are another consequence. Operational 
oversights, such as inadequate storage planning, highlight misaligned requirements. 
Conflicting decisions arise from dual accountabilities, and misplaced accountability in RACI 
matrices hampers technical decision-making by prioritizing operational concerns over 
expertise. 
In short, these issues cause inefficiencies, inflated costs, and dissatisfaction among 
internal stakeholders, such as hospitality teams, and external clients, including end-users. 
Resolving these problems is essential for enhancing project execution and ensuring long-term 
success.

project meets regulations, brand standards, and local expectations.

implementation. The owner plays a key role in balancing the design with the budget and 
project goals. This teamwork and continuous improvement lead to a preliminary design that 
combines vision, feasibility, and functionality, laying the foundation for a successful hotel
