# 1. Introduction
Aedes, a leading property investor and hospitality company in Amsterdam, has operated in real estate for nearly three decades (Aedes, 2024). With its first own branded hotel launched two years ago, Aedes is poised for growth, evident in its two large-scale hotel projects (Aedes, 2024). To support expansion, the company is transitioning from a family-run style to a more structured organization, scaling its workforce (OPS_01, 2024; OPS_02, 2024). This shift presents both opportunities and challenges, including optimizing communication between development and hospitality teams in the hotel-building process (OPS_01, 2024; DEV_03, 2024). Clear communication is vital to ensure projects are completed efficiently, on time, and within budget (DEV_01, 2024; DEV_03, 2024; DEV_04, 2024).
Hotel development typically follows five phases: Concept, Planning, Building, Interior & Pre-Opening, and Operations—this also applies to Aedes (Autin, 2020; Piliouris, 2020; Noordzy & Whitfield, 2021; DEV_02, 2024). Each phase is critical to ensuring timely completion, alignment with brand identity, and financial performance (Piliouris, 2020; Noordzy & Whitfield, 2021).
In larger hotel chains (e.g., Marriott), the planning phase involves clear divisions of responsibility between internal and external teams (AltexSoft, 2024). For example, design guidelines, licensing, and project management are handled by a mix of employees and ECs (External Consultant) (Martin, 2024; AltexSoft, 2024). Contracts like Technical Service Agreements and handover documents ensure accountability and adherence to timelines (ContractsCounsel, 2024). Aedes, however, follows a unique approach (OPS_01, 2024; DEV_03, 2024). As both developer and operator, its hospitality team is involved early in the process, tightly linking development and operations (Aedes, 2024; DEV_02, 2024; OPS_01, 2024; OPS_03, 2024).
This integration offers benefits, faster communication and alignment of goals (OPS_03, 2024). However, it also presents challenges: longer feedback cycles, unclear roles, and reduced accountability in meeting deadlines due to the absence of formal contracts (OPS_01, 2024; OPS_03, 2024; DEV_05, 2024).
Addressing these challenges is crucial for Aedes to ensure smooth execution and success in its projects now and in the future (Project Management Institute, 2021). By emphasizing accountability, process structure, and clear roles, Aedes can minimize delays, improve productivity, and create an efficient framework for future developments (Project Management Institute, 2021).
This paper is structured as follows: Chapter 2 discusses the current state, challenges, and opportunities in hotel building. Chapter 3 analyzes the As-Is VO using flowcharts, SIPOC, and RACI, highlighting flaws. Chapter 4 defines the To-Be state, outlining suggested process changes and reasoning. Chapter 5 covers implementation, and monitoring. Rounding off with key findings and further research, with appendices supporting where needed.
# 2. Background and Process Identification
Founded in 1995, Aedes wants to create spaces with heart and soul—leaving a lasting impact on people, culture, and the environment. Guided by a vision to renew urban areas by blending history and sustainable innovation, Aedes designs vibrant spaces that endure across generations. Its strategy emphasizes urban redevelopment, sustainability, and relaxed hospitality, merging cultural heritage with modern needs into timeless narratives (Aedes, 2024).
Aedes has consistently developed visually appealing, meaningful, and sustainable buildings. From luxury hotels and apartments to innovative workspaces, its projects inspire human connection, enrich those who visit or reside there, and align with long-term sustainability goals (Aedes, 2024).
Unlike larger hotel chains, which often outsource aspects of the development process, Aedes in-sources operations, involving hospitality teams from the earliest development phases. This ensures properties reflect their core values and promotes stronger collaboration between design and operational teams, delivering spaces that meet the highest standards of hospitality and sustainability (Aedes, 2024).
## 2.1 Current State of the Hotel Building Process
The hotel development process comprises five phases: Concept, Planning, Building, Interior & Pre-Opening, and Operations (Autin, 2020; Piliouris, 2020; Noordzy & Whitfield, 2021; Madelon Interview). Each phase involves essential activities and stakeholders critical to project success.

The Concept phase assesses the project's feasibility, covering market research, business case development, and initial sustainability considerations (Piliouris, 2020). Aedes collaborates with stakeholders like market and financial analysts and consultants to determine if the project can meet market demand and provide returns (Aedes, 2024). At this stage, a brand or operator may also be selected to collaborate with on the project minimizing risk (Piliouris, 2020).
The Planning phase typically involves land acquisition, consultant selection (for the design), architectural planning, and securing financing (Noordzy & Whitfield, 2021). Key stakeholders include legal advisors, investors, and banks, alongside the internal team (Autin, 2020). This phase ensures all regulatory and financial frameworks are in place (Autin, 2020; Noordzy & Whitfield, 2021).
In the Building phase, physical construction begins, with activities managed by the project management and development teams (Noordzy & Whitfield, 2021). Communication is crucial to prevent delays and maintain project timelines (Noordzy & Whitfield, 2021).
The fourth phase focuses on interior design, IT installation, and final outfitting. Stakeholders include project management, consultants, and the operations team, ensuring the hotel is operationally and guest-ready (Piliouris, 2020; Noordzy & Whitfield, 2021).
In the final phase, the hotel opens, generating revenue for the first time (Noordzy & Whitfield, 2021). The hospitality and operations teams can now work to meet service and financial expectations.
## 2.2 Aedes’ Current Situation
Aedes’ insourced development and hospitality model enables alignment between operations and development. However, the lack of formal contracts and broad employee roles creates challenges, as highlighted in preliminary interviews (DEV_03, 2024; OPS_01, 2024; OPS_02, 2024). Without structured agreements, feedback cycles lengthen, and accountability weakens (DEV_05, 2024; OPS_01, 2024). Additionally, broad employee roles often blur responsibilities, causing delays in decision-making and execution (DEV_05, 2024; OPS_01, 2024). These factors contribute to communication breakdowns across all five project phases (DEV_05, 2024; OPS_01, 2024). Addressing these issues is critical as Aedes works to formalize operations and streamline its development process (DEV_05, 2024; OPS_01, 2024).
Before addressing problem identification, the relevance of these issues must be clarified. Stakeholder optimization is vital for ensuring smooth operations in the complex hotel development process (Ram, 2018; World Sustainable Hospitality Alliance, 2020). My findings, detailed in Section 3.3, show that delays frequently occur due to misaligned stakeholder tasks and communication breakdowns (DEV_05, 2024; OPS_01, 2024). Optimizing stakeholder interactions can eliminate unnecessary delays, reducing time and resource costs (Aladpoosh, 2012; DEV_04, 2024). Furthermore, documenting and improving these processes will yield immediate benefits, such as fewer delays and cost savings (Aladpoosh, 2012).
## 2.3 Problem Identification

Delays here often cascade, affecting timelines and projected costs for subsequent phases (BNA & NLingenieurs, 2014; Noordzy & Whitfield, 2021).

## 3.1 As-Is Flowchart
The VO-process involves seven distinct stages, lasting approximately 6 months for the Amstel 111 project (BNA & NLingenieurs, 2014; DEV_03, 2024). These stages were verified with both the development and hospitality departments at Aedes to ensure alignment and consistency during interviews (DEV_03, 2024; OPS_01, 2024). Below is a summary of these stages, with estimated time allocations derived from stakeholder input (DEV_05, 2024; OPS_03, 2024).
Stage 1: 15-20%
When the VO-process starts, the project team's first stage is to evaluate the site and its surrounding environmental factors. The goal is to ensure the design integrates well with the physical and contextual environment. This phase sets the foundation by addressing site conditions, regulations, and potential challenges that could impact the overall design.
Stage 2: 15-20%
The second stage involves utilizing the SO and creating the preliminary architectural concept, including floor plans, sections, and visual design elements. This is done by utilizing the company's internal database of information, such as ICCF and additional DNR documentation.

Stage 3: 15-20%
Following this stage, in stage three, the preliminary structural and installation plans are developed. This includes key technical and infrastructure elements like plumbing, electrical systems, and other installations critical for the building's functionality. The design should be prepared for a detailed review, but it should remain flexible for changes based on upcoming feedback.
Stage 4: 20-25%
The fourth stage is a key "Go/No-Go" point in the process. After the initial structural and installation plans are complete, there is a review and feedback round with, among others, the hospitality team. Their input is crucial in determining whether the design can progress or needs further revisions. If significant changes are required, the project needs to revert to stage 2 for adjustments. If the feedback is positive and only minor changes are necessary, the process moves on to the next stage.
Stage 5: 15-20%
Once the hospitality review is completed, and a go-ahead is given, the development team works on refining the functional and spatial layout of the project. This stage focuses on creating the functional and spatial layout, ensuring the design supports efficient operations, guest comfort, and compliance with design standards.
Stage 6: 20-25%
Stage six involves a final review with all relevant stakeholders. Here, stakeholders from the hospitality and development teams provide input as well as the managing director (for Aedes also the owner). This stage represents the second key "Go/No-Go" decision point. If the feedback requires small adjustments, the process may revert to stage 5. However, if more significant changes are required, the project could go back to stage 2, which should be avoided (at all costs) due to the time and resources that this will cost. Ideally, after this phase, the project should move forward to completion.
Stage 7: 1-5%
In the final stage, the design is finalized and signed off by all stakeholders. Once the final version is approved, the VO-process is considered complete, and the project moves into the next step (the DO).

Additionally, three matrices were created by different department heads, the Head of Hospitality, Head of Development, and Project Director (Appendices 1-3). Two comparisons should be pointed out.
First, a comparison of the Head of Development’s (HD) RACI with the theoretical RACI highlights key discrepancies in Accountability and stakeholder engagement. In Task 1, their RACI assigns dual Accountability to development and the owner, whereas the theoretical model designates development as Accountable and the owner as Consulted. This dual accountability risks inefficiency and conflicts in decision-making, particularly regarding site integration (Kerzner, 2017). Task 4 further excludes EC (External Consultants) in their RACI, undermining their role in ensuring technical feasibility and project alignment (Macmillan et al., 2001). In Task 6, they assign both Client and Development as Responsible, creating ambiguity in stakeholder consultations and risking delays (Olander & Landin, 2008).
Second, the Head of Hospitality’s (HH) RACI diverges from the theoretical model, notably in role clarity. Task 4 in their RACI gives hospitality a dual Accountable/Responsible role, reducing development to Informed. This contradicts the theoretical model, which assigns Hospitality as Responsible and Development as Consulted to ensure technical input (Freeman, 1984). In Task 5, they give Development a Consulted/Responsible role instead of making them solely Accountable, leading to potential delays and conflicting priorities (Kerzner, 2017). Lastly, in Task 6, they assign Accountability to Hospitality rather than designating Development as Responsible and Hospitality as Informed. This role reversal risks operational concerns overshadowing technical expertise, jeopardizing long-term project outcomes (Olander & Landin, 2008).
These discrepancies highlight accountability and stakeholder management issues. Dual responsibilities, such as HD’s Task 6 and HH’s Task 1, dilute accountability and hinder task completion (Kerzner, 2017). Similarly, assigning dual Accountable roles, as seen in HD’s Task 1, creates conflicts, particularly when development and the owner disagree on key decisions (Macmillan et al., 2001).
Excluding EC, as in HD’s Task 4, neglects critical expertise, while HH’s overemphasis on hospitality accountability risks overshadowing technical priorities. Resolving these issues requires aligning RACIs with the theoretical framework to establish clarity, collaboration, and balanced stakeholder engagement. This alignment enhances decision-making, feedback processes, and project success (Freeman, 1984; Olander & Landin, 2008).
## 3.4 As-Is Conclusion
The analysis of the VO-process reveals inefficiencies and risks, including communication breakdowns, overlapping responsibilities, and inadequate stakeholder integration. Specifically:
Feedback loops during stages such as the hospitality review and stakeholder consultations create bottlenecks, delaying progress and reducing project efficiency (DEV_01, 2024; DEV_05, 2024).
The RACI analysis identifies dual accountability and unclear role assignments, such as assigning both development and the owner accountability for site integration. This ambiguity results in conflicting decisions and delays in task execution (Kerzner, 2017; Macmillan et al., 2001).
Excluding ECs during critical stages, such as in Task 4, increases the risk of technical oversights and misalignment with project goals (Olander & Landin, 2008).
Misplaced accountability, particularly in the HH’s RACI, prioritizes operational concerns over technical requirements, jeopardizing long-term project outcomes (Freeman, 1984).
This exposes Aedes to risks i.e., extended project timelines, increased resource consumption, and reduced alignment with operational objectives. To mitigate these risks, the VO-process must be aligned with both theoretical and real-world applicability. This should be done by combining the both in a To-Be situation as will be done in the next chapter.
# 4. Desired Situation
This chapter explains how the VO-process has been improved to increase its efficiency for everyone involved. A new flowchart, SIPOC, and RACI have been co-created to fix issues in the old process. These changes focus on doing the important work earlier, making roles clearer, and improving communication between development, hospitality, and other stakeholders. The goal is to ensure the VO-process runs smoothly and meets everyone’s needs.
## 4.1 To-Be VO Flowchart
After the first flowchart was presented, it was shared with all stakeholders, who were invited to suggest changes to align the flowchart closer with their needs in the VO-process. The result of this feedback; an improved flowchart (DEV_01, 2024; DEV_02, 2024; DEV_04, 2024; DEV_05, 2024; OPS_01, 2024; OPS_03, 2024). In combination with this flowchart, several key suggestions for the VO-process were made. These will be further elaborated later in this subchapter.

There are several significant differences when comparing the new flowchart to the As-Is. First, the creation of the first VO version has been frontloaded to the first step in the process (DEV_05, 2024). Once this step is complete, development has VO 1.0. This is then tested against all of the documentation that development has used until this point with all relevant stakeholders (DEV_04, 2024; DEV_05, 2024). This loop is repeated until development has all VO documentation up to standard and VO 2.0 can be presented to Hospitality.
Hospitality then tests the VO against all of their documentation and with their stakeholders. If HH agrees with VO 2.0 and the desired changes have been made, both HD and HH can sign off on the VO package, bringing the VO-process to an end. In addition to this revised flowchart there were, as mentioned before, three additional changes to the process:
At the start of each VO phase, HD, and HH should revise the timeline to ensure it aligns with current project goals. This allows both teams to stay on track and adjust the timeline as necessary to meet project needs (DEV_02, 2024).
Alongside this timeline, an estimate of the weekly costs associated with delays in the VO-process should be provided before initiating the process. This will help stakeholders understand the financial impact of any delays in addition to the time lost, promoting better accountability and urgency in meeting deadlines (DEV_02, 2024).
A structured update meeting should be held with Hospitality every 2-4 weeks (interval decided when creating the timeline i.e., suggestion 1) with Development on a set day and time (DEV_02, 2024; DEV_04, 2024). This meeting will:
Provide an opportunity for development to ask hospitality questions regarding interpreting the PoRs or project specifics.
Allow for changes or additions to project specifics if needed.
Share key updates on the progress, ensuring both teams remain aligned on project goals and timelines.
It is important to note that the estimated time of the process still is estimated to be six months. However, since the bulk of the work is front-loaded the feedback cycles should be more efficient since they are working with a finished VO instead of only elements of a VO. The potential for delays in the process is agreed by the stakeholders to be less likely (DEV_02, 2024; DEV_04, 2024; DEV_05, 2024; OPS_01, 2024; OPS_03, 2024).

Focusing on the desired SIPOC what is seen is similar to the Flowchart, most of the workload has been frontloaded to the start of the process instead of spread out. Furthermore, even though in theory there are the same amount of feedback cycles. In practice, there are fewer because the need for reiteration had been reduced—all the feedback cycles in the VO follow after VO 1.0 is complete.

Even though this SIPOC is made with a quite high abstraction level the flow of the process is clear. While development and hospitality teams are both actively involved from the beginning, delivering their inputs for step 1. development’s roles become more concrete as the process progresses. In the first stages, HD creates the VO 1.0, ensuring the PoRs, project specifics, financing, P&L, and ICCF are kept in mind. The clarity of input from EC and the development team helps in building a solid foundation for this first version of the VO.
The feedback loops are more optimized in this structure because most of the critical decisions, such as technical design and initial structural plans, are addressed early (when creating VO 1.0), which reduces the need for back-and-forth revisions later. Frontloading allows for a smoother transition into the later stages where only (necessary) refinements are made. As the process nears completion, the VO package is reviewed and signed off with (hopefully) minimal revisions.
In essence, this SIPOC highlights the importance of clear delineation of roles between HD and HH, as Womack and Jones (2010) emphasize an efficient, frontloaded approach that focusses on creating Business Value and removing Spillage is key. Now, the process is set up to be iterative but streamlined, ensuring that all stakeholders have clarity on expectations and deliverables at each step.

### 4.3.1 Rationale for RACI Adjustments
First, HD is Accountable for overseeing the VO's creation, ensuring alignment with project goals (Kerzner, 2017). ECs are Responsible/Consulted, providing technical expertise and compliance checks (Macmillan et al., 2001). HH is Consulted to offer functional requirements, ensuring alignment with operational needs, while the owner is Responsible for maintaining oversight of finances and strategic objectives (Freeman, 1984).
During the second step, HD remains Accountable/Responsible for reviewing the VO, ensuring adherence to technical, legal, and developmental standards (ISO 19650, 2018). EC are Consulted for technical validation, the owner remains Responsible for budget and strategy alignment, and Hospitality is Consulted to confirm operational feasibility (Olander & Landin, 2008).
In the third step, HH assumes Accountable/Responsible roles, ensuring VO content aligns with operational and guest experience goals (Freeman, 1984). Development is Consulted for technical feasibility, the owner is Responsible/Consulted for financial and operational alignment, and ECs are Informed as their role diminishes with a shift to operational/hospitality focus.
In the final step, the owner becomes Accountable, approving or reevaluating the VO package, reflecting their authority over financial commitments and project success (Kerzner, 2017). HH is Responsible for ensuring functional and guest experience alignment, HD is Consulted to confirm design feasibility and ECs remain Informed as their role concludes (ISO 19650, 2018).
### 4.3.2 Addressing Owner Priorities
The owner’s perspective (appendix X) emphasizes their final authority over the VO-process, supported by input from hospitality and developmental teams. Assigning HD as Accountable for Task 1 ensures execution oversight, while HH takes Accountability for content-driven tasks like Task 3 to reflect their operational expertise (Freeman, 1984). The owner is Accountable for final sign-off, acknowledging their financial stake and authority to realign priorities as needed (Olander & Landin, 2008).
This RACI balances collaboration with clear accountability. It prevents overlapping roles, ensuring tasks like VO creation (Task 1) and content review (Task 3) are handled by specific stakeholders based on expertise. This structure reduces delays and misaligned priorities often caused by shared roles (Kerzner, 2017). By aligning with the owner's expectations and theoretical best practices, the RACI promotes communication, accountability, and decision-making, creating a workable To-Be framework.
## 4.4 To-Be Conclusion
This chapter improved the VO-process by introducing a To-Be flowchart, SIPOC, and RACI, addressing unclear roles, poor communication, and fragmented feedback loops. The updates prioritize frontloading tasks, streamlining collaboration, and reducing revisions, aligning with Womack and Jones’s (2010) focus on business value and waste elimination.
The To-Be flowchart ensures early feedback loops, with documentation tested and refined alongside stakeholders before transitioning to operations. The SIPOC framework clarifies roles and responsibilities, enhancing transparency and accountability (Kerzner, 2017; Macmillan et al., 2001).
The proposed RACI adjustments distribute tasks clearly, in-line with Olander and Landin (2008) and Kerzner (2017). This structure minimizes confusion, maintains strategic oversight for the owner, strengthening project outcomes (Freeman, 1984).
These refinements create a robust VO that improves communication, collaboration, and project delivery. The system ensures a smooth transition from development to operations, maintaining strategic alignment and operational efficiency (ISO 19650, 2018; Womack & Jones, 2010).
# 5. Implementation
The following section outlines an actionable plan to take the proposed solutions and make them into concrete steps with measurable outcomes. With the focus remaining on accountability, structured processes, and clear role definitions to ensure effective collaboration among development and hospitality teams.
## 5.1 Implementation Steps
The revised VO-process begins with an initial meeting involving key stakeholders—development, hospitality, and the owner. During this session, the updated flowchart, SIPOC, and RACI are presented to establish a shared understanding of roles, responsibilities, and deliverables.
A timeline and cost estimation for potential delays (expressed in EUR per week) are defined for each new project. Development takes the lead by preparing VO 1.0, integrating inputs from PoRs, project details, and insights from ECs. Regular bi-weekly meetings between hospitality and development facilitate consistent communication, adjustments, and progress tracking.
The process includes a structured feedback loop where development refines the VO iteratively based on stakeholder input. Once VO 2.0 aligns with expectations, hospitality reviews it to ensure operational compatibility, consulting development, and ECs when needed.
Upon final agreement, the VO package undergoes owner review to confirm strategic and financial alignment. The RACI framework ensures accountability by defining task ownership and documenting handovers at each stage.
## 5.2 Define Targets
The flowchart provides a visual guide for process adherence, while the SIPOC model and RACI framework define inputs, outputs, and roles. A project management platform, like Asana or Monday.com, could track progress, assign tasks, and document feedback. Timelines would be managed with Gantt charts, and updated during bi-weekly meetings to reflect changes.
Three metrics should measure impact: adherence to the six-month timeline, with delays quantified in weekly cost estimates; the number of feedback cycles per VO version, indicating process efficiency; and stakeholder satisfaction, assessed through post-process surveys on roles, communication, and effectiveness. These tools ensure alignment with project objectives and enable continuous improvement through data-driven insights.
## 5.3 Stakeholder Collaboration and Revisions
Future projects could start with a workshop involving development, hospitality, the owner, and relevant consultants to align all parties. This session could introduce the revised flowchart, SIPOC model, and RACI framework, gathering feedback regarding project specific goals. Regular meetings every two to four weeks could maintain communication, refine deliverables, and address challenges. Feedback loops should be tested, allowing iterative revisions to the VO-process. The collaboration process should remain dynamic, empowering stakeholders to suggest modifications, and ensuring the framework adapts with project needs.
## 5.4 Evaluation and Impact Assessment
An evaluation framework can measure the effectiveness of the revised VO-process. Key metrics include adherence to timelines, fewer feedback cycles, and improved stakeholder satisfaction, evaluated through post-project surveys and reviews. Financial impacts of delays and the role of structured updates on communication and task clarity can also be assessed.
Following the first project using this approach, a debriefing session with all stakeholders should gather feedback to identify opportunities for further improvement. This iterative process should refine the VO framework, enhancing its effectiveness in future applications.
# 6. Conclusion and Recommendations
This BPI presents a revision of the VO-process to address inefficiencies, improve role clarity, and enhance collaboration among hospitality and development stakeholders. The analysis identified key challenges in the Planning phase, such as poor communication, unclear accountability, and delayed feedback, which threaten project timelines (DEV_01, 2024; DEV_05, 2024). The proposed To-Be framework, incorporating the revised VO flowchart, SIPOC model, and RACI adjustments, addresses these issues through structured processes, clear roles, and transparent collaboration (Kerzner, 2017; Olander & Landin, 2008).
By prioritizing early-stage task completion, employing structured tools, and strengthening accountability, the new approach aims to create a smoother, more cooperative workflow (Project Management Institute, 2021; Womack & Jones, 2010). Key recommendations include testing the revised process in upcoming projects, actively engaging stakeholders in collaborative steps, and conducting regular evaluations to measure efficiency, satisfaction, and project outcomes (Macmillan et al., 2001; DEV_03, 2024).
If successful, this updated VO-process can serve as a scalable model for future Aedes projects, ensuring better communication, accountability, and on-time delivery across all development phases. These changes will strengthen operational efficiency, align stakeholder expectations, and maintain project timelines, supporting Aedes’ mission to create meaningful spaces while driving sustainable growth in Amsterdam’s hospitality market (Aedes, 2024; Piliouris, 2020).
The proposed improvements focus on the VO-process. For wide spread applicability research should extend across the entire DNR, since the VO step is only one of ten steps in the DNR. Furthermore, it is essential to explore how communication and accountability frameworks can adapt to broader phases like Concept, Building, Interior & Pre-Opening, and Operations.
Additionally, the limitations of relying solely on a case study should be addressed. Research across multiple projects and locations would provide a more comprehensive view of challenges and solutions, helping refine the frameworks into adaptable strategies that ensure scalability and consistency across Aedes’ projects.
