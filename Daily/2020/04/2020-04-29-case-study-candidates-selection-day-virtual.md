Introduction The Visitor Journey
This paper reports on an innovative and ambitious new approach to developing tourism strategies called the Visitor 
Journey. It was ﬁrst trialled in 2003 when the Regional Development Agency (RDA) One NorthEast contracted 
LiveTourism to develop a new regional tourism framework to complement its Regional Economic Strategy (RES). Since 
then, it has been adopted by a number of key tourism organizations, including the London Development Agency. There 
are two key principles that underpin it 
(1)Visitors are the very life blood of tourism and need to be at the heart of any strategy. To succeed, the whole visitor 
experience must match and exceed expectations. This means right from the point when a visitor starts to think 
about where to go, through to returning home and beyond. 
(2)The choice of process that is used to develop a tourism strategy is as important as the strategy itself. On their 
journey, a visitor can easily encounter the services of a whole range of different organizations. Whilst these 
organizations may be entirely separate entities, collectively they deliver the experience for the visitor. This will be 
greatly enhanced if they can all be engaged meaningfully in developing and delivering a strategy together.
The Visitor Journey framework can be used by any size or type of organization in the tourism industry. It can be used 
by individual organization to improve their own performance. It is particularly powerful when used as a tool to help 
organization work more effectively together.

Background
Conventional tourism strategies are often structured around key functional areas such as research, training, information, 
marketing and product development, but in doing so, they can become stand alone, and ultimately dysfunctional. To be effective 
these areas must be driven seamlessly by visitor needs and priorities. Richin (1997) demonstrated the role that our emotions play 
in driving our needs. Building on this work, Bennett et al. (2004) showed that consumers will pursue a service-encounter gone 
wrong for days or months, feeling intense emotions that create physical or psychological consequences even when only small 
amounts of money are involved. The tourism industry needs to understand the range of emotions a visitor may go through on a 
journey in order to be able to meet their needs. For example – Fear –“I need to feel safe,” Anticipation – “I need to trust what you 
say is what you deliver”. Clawson and Knetsch as far back as 1966 were pursuing the ideal of putting the visitor ﬁrst (Clawson and 
Knetsch, 1966). More recently, Pine and Gilmour (1998) coined the phrase the “experience economy” to encapsulate how 
consumers are no longer seeking products and services, but instead a whole experience. These ﬁndings present a particular 
challenge for the tourism industry where the experience is actually delivered by a whole range of organization from B&B owners, 
restaurateurs, transport providers, local authorities and attractions to shopping centre managers, to name but a few examples. No 
single organization is responsible for the whole journey, yet the overall experience for visitor is only as good as the weakest link in 
the chain. LiveTourism has developed a framework that addresses these issues called the Visitor Journey. The framework follows 
the visitor through a sequence of events and emotions from the initial planning stages, to leaving the home environment, through
the return journey and beyond as a continuous visitor journey. Whilst putting the customer ﬁrst may not be new to many 
industries, until now tourism has been slow to put the visitor centre stage.

The visitor journey methodology
In order to develop a powerful management tool, six key interrelated stages in the visitor journey have been identiﬁed 
and are shown on Slide 6. This enables the industry – at every level – to intervene by adding value to every experience 
encountered by the visitor. The model can be used in a variety of ways. The framework can be used by individual 
B&Bs, restaurants, hotel chains, large-scale attractions, transport providers, local authorities and regional development 
agencies alike. Individual organizations use the framework to identify their own unique contribution to the visitor 
journey and to identify action that they can take themselves to improve the experience. An individual business using 
the model looks at each stage in the journey to determine what they are currently offering, where the gaps are and 
what action they can take to enhance the experience for the visitor. It is through the quality experience of each phase 
of this seamless journey that individual businesses and destinations will be judged. If one element does not match or 
exceed expectations, then the overall visitor experience will be diminished. It can also be used by a destination where 
partners consider collectively how they can best meet the needs of visitors. A visitor is likely to encounter a whole 
range of service and facilities providers whilst on a trip.

Conclusions
The Visitor Journey model is a management tool with almost unlimited applications. Providing the needs of the visitor 
or customer are considered ﬁrst, it can be applied to virtually any project, speciﬁc market segments such as women, 
families, walkers, or those with disabilities. But like any strategy or framework it does not implement itself. An 
extensive action plan, identifying clear funding and delivery ownership is a key part of the process. In 2003 this 
approach was perceived to be very radical in the tourism industry. Since then, the understanding of how powerful it 
can be has gathered pace. VisitBritain’s 2006-2009 Marketing Strategy refers to the consumer journey in which they 
“look – book – travel – stay – tell – keep in touch” the six stages of the Visitor Journey demonstrating that the visitor is 
ﬁrmly at the heart of their business. As we have shown in this case study the real beneﬁts will come when the 
framework is cascaded through all levels of the industry and only then can we be truly competitive in the global 
tourism market place.
