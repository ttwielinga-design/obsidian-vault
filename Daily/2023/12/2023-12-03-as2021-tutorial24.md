# Pre-Masters Academic Skills Tutorial 2.4
## Structuring the Capstone Paper
Your task in writing the Capstone is to move beyond pure summary of your sources and critically analyze them.  Critical analysis means asking yourself whether you agree with a viewpoint and if so, why? What is it that makes you agree or disagree? You can ensure you are analyzing critically by testing out your own views against those you are reading about: What do you think about the topic? Then as you read each new study, does the evidence presented confirm your view, or does it provide a counter-argument that causes you to question your view? Also think about the methods used to gather the evidence – are they reliable or do they have gaps or weaknesses?
When writing up your literature review, under each of your headings or themes, compare and contrast the differing views put forward in the relevant studies and explain how they relate to your investigation.
Your literature review needs to tell an interesting “story” which leads up to how and why you are doing your investigation. If you are writing a story which reads like one thing after another, this is likely to be descriptive. Creating structure to support your thesis statement
The argument presented in your thesis will be supported throughout the paper and addressed again in the conclusion.  In order to write a successful argumentative essay, you should choose a debatable topic and develop a strong thesis statement with a clear point of view.  The main purpose of the argumentative essay is to persuade the reader to accept a position on an issue.  Arguments must therefore be presented with supporting evidence and facts from sources.  Often, counter arguments and opposing ideas are also addressed briefly in order to strengthen the argument. (Van Loon, Thuss, Schmidt & Haines, 2013, p.33)
Chapter transitions are an excellent way to build in the “story” of your paper, linking the chapters to the thesis statement directly and showing how this chapter addresses the answer to your original research question.  Think of each chapter heading as an important place to identify the big themes in your paper, and to introduce the new content or concepts that will be discussed in that chapter.  A good chapter transition will tell your reader how this new topic relates to the previous chapter, and how it relates to the thesis/paper as a whole.
Headings and Chapter Transitions
DO:
Include transitions after every new chapter heading – should include two-three introductory sentences about the new topic.
Provide a context or build a link to the previous chapter or your thesis statement, showing how topics are related
Show connections between sub-chapters and chapter topic!
Include a topic sentence after a new sub-chapter heading

DO NOT:
Introduce a new source as your chapter transition- the first sentence or two of a new chapter should be about a concept or idea in your own words, not reporting on your sources.
Example: In this chapter, Brown and Jones (2005) discuss...
Try to avoid meta-talk that reminds your reader of what they just read and what will follow:
In the last chapter, I wrote about X...Next, I describe Y...(this is a waste of words)
Avoid 2 headings after each other with no text in between (see example below)

Paragraph Structure
A good paragraph has the following criteria:
Unity – the paragraph deals with one topic, which is presented in the topic sentence.  The topic sentence states the main point you will make in this paragraph. All ideas in the paragraph should relate back to the topic sentence.
Well-developed - The paragraph contains enough supporting evidence to make the claim that was stated in the topic sentence (examples, facts/statistics from sources, etc.)
Coherence – There is a clear organization of ideas. Explanations within the paragraph follow a logical order.
Flow – The use of transition signals to connect ideas within sentences and between sentences.

Transitions
The main goal of academic writing is clear communication.  This can be achieved through the logical organization of ideas and precise word choice.  Transitions are important both to highlight the organization of the paper – for example at the beginning of a new heading or chapter – as well as within paragraphs to show the relationship between ideas.
Transitional sentences at the beginning of a new topic or paragraph improve the coherence of a paper.  Closing or concluding sentences are also important to provide a sense of closure before moving onto a new topic. Another way to achieve clarity in the presentation of ideas is the use of transitional words.
