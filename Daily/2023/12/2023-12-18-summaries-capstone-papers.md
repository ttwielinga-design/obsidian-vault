# Using Budgets for Performance Evaluation: Effects of Resource Allocation and Horizontal Information Asymmetry on Budget Proposals, Budget Slack, and Performance

Author(s): Joseph G. Fisher, Laureen A. Maines, Sean A. Peffer, Geoffrey B. Sprinkle
How traditional compensation plans based on budgets affect the actions of lower-level managers in companies.
These traditional plans usually encourage these managers to make their work seem less productive than it actually is and leave extra room in the budgets.
This behavior often leads to expensive mistakes in planning and ultimately decreases the profits of the company.
To counter this problem of budget slack, compensation systems were designed to encourage honesty in reporting private information, but these systems are hardly ever put into practice.
This study looks into whether two alternatives
using budgets to distribute resources
sharing information about colleagues
might help reduce budget slack AND improve performance when compared to these truth-based compensation schemes.

Hypotheses:
1. Resource Allocation Impact:
H1a: Subordinates propose higher budgets when superiors allocate resources.
H1a: Subordinates will submit higher initial budget proposals when the superior allocates productive resources to subordinates than when the superior does not allocate productive resources to subordinates.
Validation: Supported. The study's results indicate that when superiors used budgets to allocate scarce resources, subordinates indeed proposed higher initial budgets.
H1b: Lower slack exists when resources are allocated, positively impacting subordinate performance.
H1b: Slack in the final budget will be lower when the superior allocates productive resources to subordinates than when the superior does not allocate productive resources to subordinates.
Validation: Supported. The research findings demonstrate a reduction in final budget slack when superiors allocated productive resources among subordinates.
H1c: Subordinate performance increases when resources are allocated by superiors.
H1c: Subordinate performance will be higher when the superior allocates productive resources among subordinates than when the superior does not allocate productive resources among subordinates.
Validation: Supported. The study shows an increase in subordinate performance when superiors allocated resources among subordinates.


2. Effects of Information Asymmetry:
H2a: Low information asymmetry among subordinates increases initial budget proposals in fixed resource conditions.
H2a: Knowledge of other subordinates' budget proposals, counteroffers, budget levels, and performance will increase subordinates' initial budget proposals when resource allocations are fixed, but not when the superior allocates productive resources among subordinates.
Validation: Supported. When resource allocations were fixed, having information about peers' budget-related details led to higher initial budget proposals.
H2b: Lower slack exists in the final budget in fixed resource conditions with reduced information asymmetry.
H2b: Knowledge of other subordinates' budget proposals, counteroffers, budget levels, and performance will reduce the slack in the final budget when resource allocations are fixed, but not when the superior allocates productive resources among subordinates.
Validation: Supported. In situations where resource allocations were fixed, information about peers' budget-related details resulted in less final budget slack.
H2c: Subordinate performance increases due to reduced information asymmetry in fixed resource conditions.
H2c: Knowledge of other subordinates' budget proposals, counteroffers, budget levels, and performance will increase subordinates' own performance when resource allocations are fixed, but not when the superior allocates productive resources among subordinates.
Validation: Partially supported. The study indicated an effect on budget proposals and slack but didn’t show a significant impact on subordinate performance.

Explanation of Hypotheses:
Resource Allocation Impact (H1):
The hypothesis predicts that allocating resources by superiors positively impacts subordinates' behaviors.
When superiors allocate resources based on budget proposals, subordinates are motivated to propose higher budgets, leading to increased performance due to increased resources and motivation to exceed higher budget levels.

2. Effects of Information Asymmetry (H2):
These hypotheses explore the impact of information sharing among subordinates.
Lower asymmetry in information leads to increased competition among subordinates, encouraging them to propose more challenging budgets and perform better to avoid looking less capable than their peers.
Justification Hypotheses:
The hypotheses align with previous research that showed how traditional budget-based schemes incentivize slack creation and how truth-inducing schemes only partially mitigate it.
The paper introduces the concept of resource allocation and reduced information asymmetry as potential solutions to mitigate budget slack and improve performance in the traditional budgeting framework.

DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
When budgets were utilized to allocate scarce resources, subordinates exhibited no slack in their budgets and improved their task performance by 25%.
Influence of information asymmetry among subordinates on budget outcomes. In cases where budgets weren't used to allocate resources, subordinates having information about their peers' budget proposals and performance made higher initial budget proposals and had final budgets with less slack compared to those without this information.
Budgets were used for resource allocation, the degree of information asymmetry didn't significantly impact subordinates' proposals or final budget slack. Additionally, the level of information asymmetry didn't affect subordinates' performance.
Firms can mitigate issues arising from using budgets solely for performance evaluation or resource allocation by employing a single budget for both functions.
This finding explains why companies commonly use a single budget for planning and control and prefer traditional budget-based compensation schemes over truth-inducing ones.
how using budgets for both resource allocation and performance evaluation might eliminate inefficiencies like budget slack and capital rationing described in analytical research.
Benefits of studying the multifaceted roles of managerial accounting tools to assess their effectiveness.

Value Added by the Paper:
The paper contributes significantly to understanding the dynamics of budgetary behavior in organizations. It highlights the importance of using budgets for both resource allocation and performance evaluation, demonstrating their interdependent effects. Moreover, it underscores the role of information sharing among subordinates in mitigating budgetary slack, emphasizing its relevance in certain organizational settings.

Potential Further Research:
The study presents opportunities for future research in several areas:
Mechanisms Behind Information Asymmetry: Exploring in-depth the factors and motivations influenced by information asymmetry among subordinates, diving into economic, social, and psychological aspects.
Contextual Variables: Investigating how individual, task-specific, or environmental factors impact budget outcomes and behavioral responses.
Exploring Perceptions: Delving into subordinates' perceptions regarding fairness, equity, and strategic collaboration in response to different budgetary conditions.
Comparative Studies: Conducting comparative studies across different industries or organizational structures to generalize findings beyond the experimental setting.


# Vertical information sharing in the budgeting process
Authors: Robert J. Parker, Larissa Kyj

The study: how the budgeting process facilitates the exchange of information between superiors and subordinates and how this impacts job performance.

Summary
The study delves into the dynamics of information sharing during the budgeting process within organizations. It specifically explores two dimensions of information flow:
1. Upward Information Sharing: Communication of information from subordinates to superiors during budget discussions.
H1. Budget Participation and Upward Information Sharing (H1):
Higher participation in the budgeting process leads to increased communication of private information from subordinates to superiors.
That more involvement provides more opportunities for sharing insights.
H2. Organizational Commitment and Upward Information Sharing (H2):
Individuals highly committed to the organization are more inclined to reveal private information during the budgeting process.
Committed individuals prioritize organizational success over personal interests, thereby fostering information sharing.
H3. Upward Information Sharing and Job Performance (H3):
Sharing information during the budgeting process positively impacts job performance.
This is theorized as the revelation of private information leads to more accurate planning and support, ultimately enhancing individual performance.
H4. Budget Participation and Organizational Commitment (H4):
Greater involvement in budgeting activities is associated with increased commitment to the organization.
The study assumes that participation fosters a sense of identification with the organization, thereby increasing commitment.
H5. Organizational Commitment and Job Performance (H5):
Higher organizational commitment is expected to positively influence job performance.
Committed individuals are believed to exhibit higher motivation, which, in turn, enhances performance.

2. Downward Information Sharing: Communication of information from superiors to subordinates during the budgeting process.
H6. Budget Participation and Role Ambiguity (H6):
Active participation in budgeting is hypothesized to reduce role ambiguity, ensuring that employees have a clearer understanding of their duties and responsibilities.
H7. Role Ambiguity and Organizational Commitment (H7):
Role ambiguity is anticipated to decrease organizational commitment. If job expectations are unclear, employees may struggle to integrate into their roles and the organization, potentially lowering commitment levels.
H8. Role Ambiguity and Job Performance (H8):
Higher role ambiguity correlates with lower job performance.
Unclear job roles can lead to uncertainty and hesitation, negatively affecting performance.

HOW?
By conducting a questionnaire-based analysis among managers and supervisors in various manufacturing firms.
Discussion and Conclusion:
Highlighting the existing arguments in accounting research suggesting that subordinates possess crucial private information beneficial for both themselves and the organization.
This private information, if communicated upward to their superiors, is believed to be advantageous. VALUE ADDED:
However, while this relationship between budget participation and information sharing has been proposed by various researchers, it hasn't been directly tested until this study.

Reveals indirect relationships between organizational commitment, information sharing, and job performance.
While previous studies in various disciplines suggest a direct link between commitment and performance through increased work effort or motivation, this study demonstrates that information sharing fully mediates the relationship between commitment and performance.

Incorporates role ambiguity in the model of information sharing and confirms that budget participation inversely affects role ambiguity, which subsequently impacts performance.
Moreover, role ambiguity adversely affects organizational commitment, influencing information sharing.
These findings align with existing concepts suggesting that organizational members must clearly understand expectations to contribute effectively.

Further Research Opportunities:
Impact of Information Sharing on Organizational Performance: Investigate how information sharing during budgeting affects not only individual but also organizational performance.
Budget Participation, Organizational Justice, and Information Sharing: Explore how perceptions of organizational fairness influence information sharing tendencies during budgeting.
Strategic Uncertainties and Information Sharing: Analyze the impact of disclosing strategic uncertainties by subordinates on organizational strategy and success.
Role Ambiguity and Organizational Learning/Innovation: Explore how reducing role ambiguity fosters individual achievement and contributes to organizational learning and innovation.


# How Top-down and Bottom-up Budgeting Affect Budget Slack and Performance through Social and Economic Exchange

Authors: STEPHAN KRAMER AND FRANK HARTMANN
The budgetary control cycle and its impact on managerial behavior within organizations. Explore the impact of different approaches [Top-Down (TD) and Bottom-Up (BU)] in the budgetary target-setting process on managerial perceptions and behavior.
Outlined across four sequential stages:
issuance of guidelines
development of initial budget proposals
budget negotiation
approval

Key Concepts:
Budgetary Control Cycle: “A systematic process used by organizations to plan, execute, monitor, and control their budgets. It involves setting targets, allocating resources, comparing actual performance with planned performance, and taking corrective actions when needed.”
Top-Down (TD) and Bottom-Up (BU) Budgeting: “These terms represent different approaches to budgetary target-setting processes. TD involves higher management imposing targets and guidelines on lower levels, while BU allows more autonomy for lower levels to create proposals.”
Both are participatory budgeting processes: TD is only like a ‘lite’version
Managerial Perceptions of Exchange Relationships: “How managers perceive their interactions and relationships with the organization, specifically in terms of economic (transactional, task-focused) and social (relational, socio-emotional) exchanges.”
Social Exchange Theory (SET): A theoretical framework used to predict how individuals perceive their relationships within an organization, emphasizing the exchange of socio-emotional resources, like recognition and respect, or material/tangible resources, such as salary or resources for work.

Hypotheses Explanation:
Impact of TD/BU on Economic and Social Exchange (H1, H2, H3): The authors hypothesize that TD orientation enhances perceptions of economic exchange, while BU orientation increases perceptions of social exchange.
Managerial Performance (H4): Higher perceptions of social exchange are predicted to lead to increased managerial performance.
Budgetary Slack Creation (H5): Economic exchange relationships are expected to reduce the creation of budgetary slack.
This study contributes by focusing on the impact of budgetary process characteristics on managerial behavior beyond traditional supervisor–subordinate interactions.

H1: TD-stage 1a → Economic exchange with the company (+)
Validation: Supported. A dominance of TD budgeting characteristics in the issuance of guidelines enhances perceptions of an economic exchange relationship with the company, resulting in lower budgetary slack.
H2: TD-stage 2b → Social exchange with the company (−)
Validation: Supported. A dominance of TD characteristics in the development of the initial budget proposal leads to perceptions of a negative impact on social exchange with the company.
H3: TD-stage 3c → Social exchange with the company (−)
Validation: Partial support. This hypothesis did not achieve statistical significance.
H4: Social exchange with the company → Managerial performance (+)
Validation: Supported. Higher perceptions of a social exchange relationship with the company correlate positively with higher managerial performance.
H5: Economic exchange with the company → Budgetary slack creation (−)
Validation: Supported. Perceptions of an economic exchange relationship resulting from a TD dominance lead to lower budgetary slack.

Value Added by the Paper:
Development of TD and BU Typology: The paper contributes by developing and confirming a typology of TD and BU characteristics within the budgeting process.
Insights into Process Design: Emphasizes the significance of process design in budgetary procedures.
Further Research Opportunities:
Refinement of Typology: Future studies could refine and validate TD and BU orientations within the budgeting process.
Cross-Cultural Studies: Explore how management control system designs influence social and economic exchange perceptions differently across various cultural contexts.
Contextual Antecedents: Investigate organizational or contextual factors influencing TD/BU budgeting procedures.
Application of Social Exchange Theory: Extend the application of social exchange theory to other areas of management control.

# Flattening the organization: the effect of organizational reporting structure on budgeting effectiveness
AUTHORS: R. Lynn Hannan; Frederick W. Rankin; Kristy L. Towry

Introduction:
Budgeting within organizational planning has often centered on participatory budgeting within the superior-subordinate relationship. While prior studies explored participation effects, this research investigates the influence of organizational structure, particularly the span of control, on participatory budgeting effectiveness.
The hypothesis focuses on how expanding the span of control might reduce slack in subordinates' budgets due to the superior's inclination toward enforcing social norms.

Hypothesis 1: Superior's Decision-making under Expanded Span of Control (“Under an expanded span of control, the superior is more willing to reject projects.”):
An increased span of control might lead superiors to reject projects perceived with excessive slack. This stems from the tradeoff between wealth maximization and norm enforcement. As the number of subordinates increases, the marginal utility from wealth diminishes while norm enforcement remains constant, potentially tilting decision-making toward enforcing norms.

Hypothesis 2: Subordinate Behavior and Reporting (“Under an expanded span of control, subordinates report lower project costs”):
Subordinates, driven primarily by wealth maximization, strategically adjust reporting behavior under an expanded span of control. They anticipate increased project rejection and submit budgets with lower project costs to align with superiors' expectations.

Experimental Methodology:
Experiments simulate capital budgeting scenarios where span of control varies by the number of subordinates. Results support Hypothesis 1, indicating superiors tend to reject projects with excessive perceived slack, leading subordinates to incorporate less slack in their budgets.



Theoretical Foundation:
The study integrates behavioral economics, neuroscience, and organizational design theories to understand the superior's utility function. It emphasizes wealth maximization and norm enforcement in decision-making.


Discussion of Findings:
Effect of Span of Control: Increased span diminishes slack by emphasizing norm enforcement, leading to less slack in subordinates' budgets.
Financial Impact: Contrary to assumptions, widening span of control increases superiors' profit per subordinate.
Relevance to Organizational Flattening: Suggests positive impacts despite potential contextual variations and challenges.

Conclusion:
Behavioral mechanisms' importance in budgeting within organizational structures; The complexity of organizational design and control systems, calling for nuanced understanding.

Value Added by the Paper:
It enriches the field by highlighting behavioral aspects in budgetary decision-making, moving beyond contractual approaches. The study demonstrates how changes in span of control influence behaviors, altering the budgetary control environment.

Further Research Opportunities:
Explore contextual variations, non-monetary factors, complex organizational structures, long-term implications, and technology integration in budgetary practices.

Final Conclusion:
The research contributes significantly to understanding how organizational structure impacts participatory budgeting. It emphasizes behavioral mechanisms' role in shaping budgetary practices and calls for diverse contextual explorations within organizational design and budgeting processes.
