How do Corporate Social Responsibility and Innovation Co‑evolve 
with Organizational Forms? Evidence from a Transitional Economy
Helen Wei Hu1 · Jiamin Zhang2 
Received: 1 June 2020 / Accepted: 4 May 2023 / Published online: 16 May 2023 
© The Author(s), under exclusive licence to Springer Nature B.V. 2023
Abstract
How do corporate social responsibility (CSR) disclosure and innovation investment co-evolve with organizational forms to 
affect firm market value? To address this question, we draw on the co-evolutionary perspective and theorize that the contin-
gency effect of CSR reporting is more pronounced for firms with high uncertainty and low legitimacy by comparing start-
up firms vs. established firms and privately owned enterprises (POEs) versus state-owned enterprises (SOEs). Moreover, 
taking a dynamic approach, we propose that the effects of CSR and innovation investment on market value change when the 
organizational form transitions over time. Specifically, we argue that when start-up firms transitioned to established firms 
or when POEs were transformed from SOEs, the strengthening effects of CSR reporting become less pronounced. We test 
our theory using panel data on 2204 Chinese listed firms for the period of 2008–2017.
Keywords  CSR reporting · R&D investment · Co-evolutionary perspective · China
Introduction
Innovation investment, such as research and development 
(R&D), has long been recognized as a key determinant in 
advancing business growth and gaining competitive advan-
tages (Lengnick-Hall, 1992). However, the equity market 
does not always respond favorably to a firm’s R&D invest-
ment, and indeed, contradictory reactions on firm market 
value have been reported (Chan et al., 2001; Eberhart et al., 
2004; Stuart & Wang, 2016; Woolridge & Snow, 1990). 
On the one hand, high R&D intensity helps investors form 
appropriate expectations and allows a firm to leapfrog its 
competitors through early investments in technological 
know-how (Van de Vrande et al., 2009). On the other hand, 
significant R&D expenditures can depress current earnings 
and raise uncertainty about a firm’s future performance 
(Chan et al., 2001; Eberhart et al., 2004; Woolridge & 
Snow, 1990). To address such discrepancies in sharehold-
ers’ reactions and to more effectively realize the benefits of 
innovation, corporate social responsibility (CSR) becomes 
a crucial differentiator (Bocquet et al., 2017; Hull & Roth-
enburg, 2008).
As a form of information disclosure, CSR reporting sig-
nals appropriate and desired corporate activity (See, 2009), 
lowers the cost of capital (Verrecchia, 2001), and allevi-
ates concerns of potential misconduct (Lattemann et al., 
2009), among others. This effect is even more pronounced 
in transitional economies where market transparency is low, 
whereas costs of monitoring are high (Shleifer & Vishny, 
1997). In addition to lowering information asymmetry and 
assessment costs, CSR reporting helps generate moral capi-
tal that functions like an ‘insurance’ to reduce investors’ 
uncertainty about a firm (Godfrey et al., 2009), including the 
potential quality of its innovation investment. Furthermore, 
as an organizational response to institutional environments 
(Luo et al., 2017), the benefits of CSR can vary to different 
firms at different times (Muller & Kräussl, 2011). This is 
particularly relevant to transitional economies, where in con-
junction with fast economic and institutional development,

Therefore, by taking a dynamic approach, we ask: How do 
CSR reporting and innovation investment co-evolve with 
organizational transformations over time to affect firm

To address this question, we draw on the co-evolutionary 
perspective (Tan & Litschert, 1994; Tan & Tan, 2005; Tan 
& Wang, 2011), which provides a critical theoretical foun-
dation to capture the interplay between firm strategy, CSR 
practices, technology innovations, and changing institutions 
in transitional economies, such as that in China. The co-
evolutionary perspective suggests that various organizational 
forms coexist in China’s transitional economy, which leads 
to increased organizational diversity and transformations 
(Lewin et al., 1999; Lukas et al., 2001). As China shifts 
from a centrally planned economy to a free-market economy 
and more new entrants join the game, an increasing number 
of studies have focused on the performance implications of 
innovation and CSR strategy by considering different types 
of firms (e.g., foreign firms, state-owned enterprises (SOEs), 
start-up firms) (Choi et al., 2001; Tang & Tang, 2012; Zhou 
et al., 2017).
However, prior studies often compare different organi-
zational forms as if they were steady-state alternatives. The 
possibility of organizational form transformation within 
firms over time has been largely neglected (for rare excep-
tions, see Genin et al., 2020). We consider this neglect as an 
important limitation in understanding temporal co-evolution 
at the firm level (Tan & Tan, 2005), as transitional econo-
mies have undergone rapid institutional changes and dra-
matic revolutions, and there is significant heterogeneity in 
organizational forms not only across firms but also within 
firms over time. For example, the relaxation of controls by 
the Chinese government has facilitated the privatization of 
SOEs and has resulted in a more diversified business con-
text (World Bank, 2002). Moreover, newly founded firms 
may grow fast in transitional economies and transform to 
the mature state in a short period of time (Li et al., 2008).
To unravel the joint effects of CSR and innovation 
investment on market value in transitional economies, 
we specifically examine how CSR interacts with R&D 
investment to affect the market performance of (1) dif-
ferent types of firms and (2) within firms that have dif-
ferent organizational forms at different times. Based on 
2204 publicly listed firms in China from 2008 to 2017, we 
show that the market will positively respond to a higher 
R&D investment intensity when a firm provides a CSR 
disclosure because CSR reporting not only allows a firm to 
communicate its higher ethical standards to market actors 
but also alleviates the problem of information asymmetry 
and indicates the potential quality of a firm’s innovation 
activities. Furthermore, we find that the contingency effect 
of CSR is more pronounced for start-up firms and privately 
owned enterprises (POEs) because CSR reporting helps 
gain legitimacy and relieve market uncertainty for these 
traditionally disadvantageous firms in China (Luo et al., 
2017). Moreover, we take a dynamic approach to capture 
organizational transformations over time. The findings 
show that when start-up firms change to established firms, 
the strengthening effect of CSR reporting becomes less 
pronounced.
This paper makes several contributions to the innovation 
and CSR literature, as well as the co-evolutionary perspec-
tive in transitional economies. First, despite its importance 
in practice, the attention of CSR in general and integration 
into innovation in particular is lacking, especially in the 
context of transitional economies (Business Time, 2018). 
Furthermore, even though recent studies have highlighted 
the importance of the joint effect of CSR and innovation 
based on observations in developed economies, they largely 
focus on examining the impacts of CSR behavior on innova-
tion outputs, such as patent filings and product innovation 
(Bocquet et al., 2017; Mishra, 2017). Our study adds to the 
stream of studies by uncovering the role of CSR in innova-
tion input, i.e., R&D investment, which involves a higher 
level of uncertainty. This paper extends (but departs from) 
the prior research and sheds light on the ambiguous rela-
tionship between R&D investment and firm market value 
(Chan et al., 2001; Eberhart et al., 2004) by highlighting 
the role of CSR in addressing the uncertainty and unethical 
concerns surrounding innovation activities. While a prior 
study suggests that CSR has an indirect effect on financial 
performance by enhancing innovation efficiency via positive 
externalities (Lioui & Sharma, 2012), our study emphasizes 
the signaling and information disclosure mechanism driving 
the moderating role of CSR, which is crucial for firms in 
transitional economies that lack market transparency. More-
over, leveraging the unique empirical context, we relax the 
dominant assumption that organizational status is static and 
empirically examine the joint effects of innovation and CSR 
that co-evolve with heterogeneous organizational forms and 
changing organizational statuses in a transitional process. As 
an important departure, we suggest that the contingent effect 
of CSR alters when organizational forms transform over 
time, thereby adding a temporal dimension to traditional 
CSR research and enhancing our understanding of the co-
evolutionary aspect of innovation practice and CSR engage-
ment. Finally, using China as the research setting, this study 
provides an important contextual contribution asserting that 
CSR and innovation practices are embedded in firms’ trans-
formations in the institutional environment, and a combined 
strategy of CSR and innovation needs to be considered to 
aid in understanding the co-evolution of innovation, ethics, 
and strategic adaptations of firms in transitional economies 
(Ho et al., 2016; Hull & Rothenburg, 2008).

Theoretical Background and Research 
Context
Transitional economies are in a prolonged state of transfor-
mation that is characterized by a restructuring of administra-
tive frameworks, redistribution of property rights, and shift-
ing discretion over resource allocations (Lukas et al., 2001). 
Building on the rapid institutional shifts in transitional 
economies, the co-evolutionary perspective emphasizes the 
temporal dimension in firm strategic adaptations and pro-
vides an overarching theoretical framework incorporating 
the interplay between transitional institutions, technology 
innovations, business ethics, and performance implications 
(Tan, 2009; Tan & Litschert, 1994; Tan & Tan, 2005; Tan 
& Wang, 2011).
China provides an important setting to understand the 
interplay of innovation and CSR strategies because it has 
witnessed rapid advancements in technology innovation 
and CSR activities in a unique and changing institutional 
environment. Despite the country’s weak intellectual prop-
erty rights (IPR) protection (Peng et al., 2017), China has 
become the most prolific patent-filing country in the world.1 
Along with China’s stellar economic growth and innova-
tion advancement, however, it is facing widespread ethi-
cal, social, and environmental challenges (Su & Littlefield, 
2001). Therefore, a combined strategy of CSR and innova-
tion needs to be taken into account to understand the strate-
gic adaptations of Chinese enterprises (Bocquet et al., 2017; 
Ho et al., 2016). Moreover, the rapid change in its insti-
tutional environments leads to widespread organizational 
transformations at the firm level (Tan & Tan, 2005), thereby 
enabling the co-evolutionary perspective to provide deep 
insights into how CSR and innovation practices co-evolve 
with organizational transformations over time.
China has introduced multiple initiatives to transition 
the country from central planning to a market orientation. 
Rapid growth and prolonged transitions give rise to a diver-
sity of organizational forms in China (Nee, 1992). At the 
firm level, this process has resulted in ‘China’s relentless 
move from homogeneity to heterogeneity’ (Brown & Porter, 
1996, p. 8). As an increasing number of newly founded firms 
enter the Chinese market, start-ups and private firms have 
grown significantly (Tan, 1999; Tan et al., 2020; Zhang & 
Keh, 2010). Given the institutional transitions occurring in 
China, scholars have compared the performance implica-
tions based on typologies of different organizational forms, 
such as POEs versus SOEs and start-up versus established 
firms. However, the static typology approach ignores the 
temporal transformation of organizational forms. As sug-
gested by Van den Bosch et al. (1999) and Lewin and Vol-
berda (1999), a key element of studying the co-evolutionary 
process is to include a longitudinal design and compare the 
changes over time.
In contrast to previous studies that have overwhelm-
ingly taken a typology approach and treated organizational 
forms as static, we consider a dynamic aspect by examining 
how a combined strategy of CSR and innovation co-evolve 
with firm-level organizational transformations over time. 
For example, Chinese governments have taken initiatives 
to increase the efficiency of the state sector and promote 
market-based business via SOE privatization and restructur-
ing (Gan, 2009). However, it is unclear whether the advanta-
geous implications of innovation and CSR strategies remain 
resilient when an SOE is changed to a privately controlled 
firm. Moreover, the fast-growing economy has led to mas-
sive new entries, and these newly founded firms may rapidly 
expand and transform to the mature state. To understand the 
co-evolution in a transitional process, the effects of CSR and 
innovation input considering the organizational transforma-
tions driven by institutional development merit more atten-
tion. In the following sections, we draw on insights from the 
co-evolutionary perspective to examine how increased firm 
value can be an outcome of a combined strategy of CSR and 
innovation by considering firm-level organizational hetero-
geneity and transformations in China.
Combined Strategy of CSR and Innovation 
on Market Value
How do CSR and R&D investment jointly affect firm market 
value? The limited studies have either found a substitution 
or a complementary effect of CSR and innovation based 
on observations of US firms (e.g., Ho et al., 2016; Hull & 
Rothenburg, 2008; Mishra, 2017). However, research on a 
combined strategy of CSR and innovation in a transitional 
context is sparse. In this study, we argue that such a com-
bined strategy is crucial, which can be particularly benefi-
cial for firms in transitional economies, including helping 
to address information asymmetry issues, creating moral 
capital, lending credence to innovation efforts, and jointly 
improving firm performance. While firms sharing convic-
tions about the benefits of innovation maintain that invest-
ing in R&D indicates the strategic importance of innovation 
(Del Monte & Papagn, 2003), a high R&D intensity may 
also lead to concerns that firms are overemphasizing explo-
ration and hence risk spending scarce resources for very 
little return (Hoskisson & Hitt, 1988), partly because the life 
cycles of the resulting innovations may be short (Goldman, 
1982). This is especially the case in transitional economies 
because firms lack detailed information disclosure about

R&D expenditures and investors may ignore the potential 
future benefits of R&D investment due to low market trans-

2009). Moreover, the information asymmetry related to 
R&D investment may create space for corporate fraud and 
unethical intensions (Wang et al., 2021). Indeed, studies find 
that in transitional economies, agents may have incentives 
to misuse and take advantage of the uncertainties surround-
ing R&D results, thus aggravating the concerns about R&D 
expenditures (Cumming et al., 2016).
To address concerns about misconduct and issues of 
information asymmetry for firms with a high R&D inten-
sity, CSR reporting becomes critical. As CSR refers to cor-
porate activities that assume responsibility for the interests 
of society (Matten & Moon, 2008), its stand-alone report-
ing becomes a clear signal of a firm’s superior commitment 
to the greater good (Gelb & Strawser, 2001). By doing so, 
CSR can effectively alleviate concerns regarding R&D 
investment.
First, it has been widely documented that CSR leads to 
positive attributions by market actors (Godfrey et al., 2009). 
When a firm has a high R&D intensity, CSR reporting pro-
vides a form of ‘certification’ and improves the firm’s ethical 
image (Ho et al., 2016). CSR creates a reputation that allows 
market actors to believe the firm is doing good and doing 
well (McWilliams & Siegel, 2001). If a firm is considered 
to be one that values its reputation, the firm often desires to 
protect its reputation and avoid engaging in socially unac-
ceptable activities (Gardberg & Fombrun, 2006). Thus, 
external stakeholders trust that the firm would invest in 
R&D projects in the most strategically sensible way to avoid 
harming its reputation. As a signal of appropriate corporate 
activity, CSR helps alleviate concerns about misconduct and 
the misuse of R&D resources to satisfy the self-interests of 
agents.
Second, information is costly in terms of production, 
collection, analysis, and reporting. This is especially the 
case for R&D activities, which involve a large amount of 
tacit knowledge and know-how (Nonaka & Krogh, 2009). 
Although it is not directly relevant to innovation activities, 
CSR reporting is a form of information disclosure and can 
enhance firm transparency and reduce the information asym-
metry between a firm and its external stakeholders. With 
appropriate disclosure, CSR lowers the cost of evaluating 
intangible assets and facilitates a positive interpretation of 
R&D expenditures. In countries with high levels of infor-
mation asymmetry, such CSR practices become even more 
helpful in indicating the quality of a firm’s R&D investment.
Finally, CSR is perceived as a long-term value creation 
tool for firms and for social sustainability (Hull & Roth-
enburg, 2008; McKinsey Quarterly, 2009). While CSR can 
be resource-consuming and cause concerns regarding short-
term financial performance (Maon et al., 2019), CSR and 
R&D investment both target long-term developments. These 
two strategies can complement each other, send consistent 
and positive signals to the market, and jointly contribute to 
firm market value (Bocquet et al., 2017). Prior studies posit 
that CSR facilitates forward thinking (e.g., Orlitzky et al., 
2003; Porter & Kramer, 2006); thus, firms that fulfill their 
CSR obligations are less likely to behave myopically. For 
example, Kim et al. (2012) find that socially responsible 
firms are less likely to cut R&D investments and other cru-
cial expenses to boost short-term accounting earnings.
To summarize, we predict that a high R&D intensity is 
more likely to benefit firm performance when a firm has 
CSR reporting:
Hypothesis 1  CSR reporting strengthens the relationship 
between R&D investment and market value, such that when 
a firm reports CSR activities, R&D investment is positively 
related to market value.
Co‑evolution of CSR and Innovation 
Strategies with Organizational Forms
While CSR reporting strengthens a firm’s R&D investment 
and market value, the beneficial effect of CSR reporting can 
vary depending on the organizational form. Given the coex-
istence of different types of firms, transitional economies 
are characterized by the diversity of organizational forms 
and the complexity of organizational transformations (Tan 
& Tan, 2005). Two organizational forms that are particularly 
associated with the level of uncertainty and legitimacy of 
firms—start-up firms (versus established firms) and private 
firms (versus SOEs)—are important in this research context.
Start‑up Firms: The Liability of Newness
Compared with long-existing firms, start-up firms face lia-
bilities of newness, including the lack of demonstrable sig-
nals of quality and a long track record of performance (Singh 
et al., 1986). In transitional economies where information 
asymmetry is typically high, this issue becomes more severe 
for start-up firms (Zhou et al., 2017).
Without CSR reporting, market actors are even more 
skeptical toward the R&D investments of start-up firms 
given the higher level of uncertainty surrounding early-
stage firms. While start-up firms are generally considered 
to be more innovative, they are also highly risky since, by 
nature, innovation requires risk-taking and deviation from 
established practices (Morris & Jones, 1999). Thus, the high 
R&D intensity of start-up firms could send mixed signals 
to the market. On the one hand, market actors may appre-
ciate the substantial expenditures on the development of

CSR reporting can both exaggerate benefits and relieve 
concerns about the high R&D intensity of start-up firms

pretations in environments of high complexity (Luo et al., 
2017). CSR reporting discloses certain levels of internal 
information and relieves the issue of information asymme-
try for such firms. With CSR reporting, start-up firms can 
form their reputations and gain legitimacy (Lii & Lee, 2012). 
Appropriate CSR reporting is a reflection of the organiza-
tional track record of performance and indicates the quality 
of internal business activities, including the innovation-
related activities of start-up firms. Thus, CSR reporting 
facilitates positive interpretations of start-up firms, helps 
them overcome the liability of newness, and substantially 
enhances the market reaction to their R&D investments. 
Therefore, we hypothesize:
Hypothesis 2a  The strengthening effect of CSR reporting 
is more pronounced for start-up firms than for established 
firms.
Start‑up Firm Transition
Prior studies often compare start-up and established firms 
as if they were steady-state alternatives. However, start-up 
firms will expand and grow up to more mature states over 
time. This transition process is especially prevalent in tran-
sitional economies where firms grow rapidly, as evidenced 
by the large number of fast-growing companies in China.2 
However, what has been neglected in previous studies is the 
dynamic process of the start-up transition and how it co-
evolves with the impact of innovation and CSR engagement. 
We explore this co-evolutionary process by comparing the 
contingency effect of CSR on R&D investment before and 
after the transitions of start-up firms.
When a start-up firm transitions into an established 
firm, the information asymmetry problem is relieved and 
the involved uncertainty is reduced because growing firms 
have often already demonstrated performance records and 
generated profits. Combating the liability of newness, the 
firm will also gain legitimacy from its longtime survival 
and industry acceptance in the marketplace (Freeman et al., 
1983). All the risks afflicting the unproven nature of technol-
ogy and management are rife at the early stage of a firm, but 
these risks dissipate as the firm progresses in its life cycle, 
from founding, to early stage, to development, and to an 
established firm (Zimmerman & Zeitz, 2002).
As discussed above, CSR reporting plays a crucial role 
for early-stage firms because it helps them overcome the 
liability of newness by disclosing valuable information to 
market stakeholders and establishing an ethical reputation. 
However, information asymmetry and legitimacy issues are 
naturally relieved when start-up firms transition to estab-
lished firms (Zou et al., 2010). Consequently, one can expect 
that the impact of CSR reporting is weakened when start-up 
firms transform into established firms.
Hypothesis 2b  Among the start-up firms, the strengthening 
effect of CSR reporting is less pronounced for those firms 
once they become established firms than for those firms that 
remain as start-ups.
Private Firms: The Liability of Being Private
Given the weak protection for private property in transitional 
economies, POEs largely operate in an uncertain world. 
SOEs in China have access to high levels of government 
financial support and favorable policy procedures (Bruton 
et al., 2015). In contrast, the ability of POEs to conduct busi-
ness can be significantly hampered by governments, banks, 
and other established organizations (Goldeng et al., 2008). 
POEs also lack legitimacy because there is prevalent dis-
crimination against these firms in China (Cull & Xu, 2003), 
and they are disadvantaged due to their fewer political con-
nections and less support from the market (Li et al., 2015; 
Nee, 1992).
Innovation activities require a large amount of resources 
over a long time frame. Given the various challenges and 
uncertainty faced by POEs, market actors may be less 
assured with regard to their prospects and their ability to sus-
tainably invest in and develop innovation projects. Moreover, 
legal and financial institutions, especially IPR protections, 
are underdeveloped in transitional economies, such as that 
of China (Zhao, 2006). While SOEs have effective defenses 
against expropriation through favorable administrative meas-
ures by the government, POEs face a high risk of misappro-
priation of their innovation results (Fang et al., 2017). These 
factors make it especially challenging for private firms in 
China to sustainably conduct R&D activities and to appro-
priately capture profit from their innovation inputs (Guan 
et al., 2009).
One of the major functions of CSR is to facilitate risk 
management (Jo & Na, 2012). Prior research has suggested

likely to loom larger when POEs demonstrate good CSR

private firms only pursue financial returns and are indiffer-

their reputation by signaling goodwill and receiving public

Hypothesis 3a  The strengthening effect of CSR reporting is 
more pronounced for POEs than for SOEs.
Private Firm Transformation
Chinese firms are commonly categorized as either SOEs or 
POEs depending on whether they are owned or controlled 
by the government (Jia & Zhang, 2013; Tan & Tan, 2017). 
However, not all POEs are developed in the same way. Apart 
from those organically formed, i.e., founded and controlled 
by private owners, a large number of POEs were transformed 
from SOEs as part of the country’s SOE reforms under the 
policy of ‘seize the large and release the small’ (World 
Bank, 2002, p. 24). SOE privatizations and restructurings 
have been key policy initiatives in China, which led to a 
massive and sweeping privatization wave, which by some 
estimates ultimately privatized two-thirds of the state sector 
(Gan, 2009).
While the overarching goal of these privatizations is to 
increase firm efficiency and to transition the country from a 
central planning to a market orientation, these transformed 
POEs may possess different competitive advantages com-
pared to those organically formed POEs. Specifically, POEs 
that were transformed from SOEs have already built their 
legitimacy in the market and have established important gov-
ernment networks and support, including access to financial 
loans from state-owned banks (Andrews & Dowling, 1998). 
Such competitive advantages, by establishment, are not eas-
ily available to organically formed POEs, who have to build 
every part of their business from scratch (Peng et al., 2004). 
Indeed, the privatization event provides a natural cutoff in 
time, resulting in a sharp change in a firm’s ownership struc-
ture and state affiliation (Fang et al., 2017). Therefore, we 
adopt a longitudinal design that compares the contingency 
effect of CSR reporting before and after the change in sta-
tuses within POEs to better understand the transformational 
process at the firm level (Lewin & Volberda, 1999; Tan & 
Tan, 2005).
We argue that, unlike organically formed POEs, trans-
formed POEs face fewer legitimacy concerns and have more 
market recognition; hence, the benefits provided by CSR 
reporting become less crucial to these POEs. Furthermore, 
it is common that transformed POEs still maintain close con-
nections with their previous government owners and agen-
cies (Nee, 1992); thus, they have more advantages in terms 
of obtaining financial backing and resource support than 
their POE counterparts (Li et al., 2015). Following previ-
ous rationale, CSR reporting plays a critical role in helping 
POEs build legitimacy and an ethical image to send healthy 
signals to the market. However, the facilitating effect of CSR 
is weakened for POEs that were transformed from SOEs 
compared to those organically formed POEs. Therefore, we 
predict the following:
Hypothesis 3b  Among POEs, the strengthening effect of 
CSR reporting is less pronounced for those firms that were 
privatized from SOEs than for those firms that were founded 
by private owners.
Methods
Sample and Data
This study examines all firms publicly listed on the two stock 
exchanges—the Shanghai Stock Exchange and Shenzhen 
Stock Exchange—in China for the period of 2008–2017. We 
used 2008 as the starting point because ‘the year 2008 is 
widely regarded as the first year of CSR awareness in China’ 
(Zhang & Luo, 2013, p. 1743). In line with prior studies 
examining Chinese listed firms, we extracted information 
on CSR reporting and R&D expenditures, firm types, finan-
cial and other firm-level data from the China stock market 
and accounting research (CSMAR) database (Wang & Qian, 
2011). The CSMAR database system is regarded as the most 
widely used and comprehensive database for studying Chi-
nese listed firms (e.g., Sun et al., 2016; Zhang & Greve, 
2019). Furthermore, we collected regional economic data 
from the National Bureau of Statistics of China (Marquis 
& Qian, 2014) and gathered marketization data from the

unbalanced panel with a total of 2204 firms and 18,745 firm-
year observations.
Dependent Variables
Market Value
Following prior research, a firm’s market value (i.e., market 
capitalization) is measured as the total value of a firm’s stock 
price multiplied by the number of firm shares (Richard et al., 
2009). We used the natural logarithm of the firm’s market 
value since the variable is not normally distributed. Further-
more, we used Tobin’s q as an alternative dependent variable 
for measuring market value in the robustness checks. Tobin’s 
q is a commonly used market-based variable for measuring 
a firm’s market value, which is calculated as the ratio of the 
market value of a firm’s equity and debt to its total assets 
(Li & Zhang, 2010).
Independent and Moderating Variables
R&D
R&D activities have been widely examined in the research 
on firm innovation; thus, we followed the prior research by 
using the ratio of R&D expenditures to total sales to measure 
R&D investment (e.g., O’Brien & David, 2014).
CSR Reporting
As CSR reporting is voluntary in China, we used a binary 
variable to measure CSR reporting, following prior research 
(e.g., Gelb & Strawser, 2001; Marquis & Qian, 2014). After 
obtaining the CSR reporting data from the CSMAR-CSR 
database, we coded each firm with a binary variable equal 
to 1 if the firm released a CSR report in a given year, and 0 
otherwise.
Start‑up
In the transition from a start-up firm to an established firm, 
a 5-year cutoff point was used to measure a firm’s start-up 
status. The reason is that prior research has suggested that 
the average time needed for a firm to develop from founding 
to surviving and becoming established with a strong market 
position is approximately 5 years (Autio et al., 2000; Bantel, 
1998). Similarly, the research on a firm’s initial public offer-
ing has also used the 5-year cutoff as a ‘strategic threshold’ 
to measure the firm’s survival capability (Fisher & Pollock, 
2004). Furthermore, this five-year cutoff has been used in 
the research on start-up firms in China (e.g., Zhou et al., 
2017). Thus, following these studies, if the age of a firm 
was equal to or less than five years for the study period, it 
was considered a start-up firm and was coded as 1, and 0 
otherwise.
Start‑up to Established
A binary variable was used to measure the change of the 
firm from the status of a start-up to that of an established 
firm. That is, among the start-ups, we traced their develop-
ment for the entire study period. A firm was coded 1 when 
it transitioned from a start-up to an established firm (its age 
became greater than five years during the study period), and 
0 otherwise.
POE
POEs and SOEs have been frequently examined in the Chi-
nese context, and a binary variable has widely been used to 
measure the type of owner of the firm. In line with the lit-
erature, if a firm was held by a private owner(s) (i.e., was not 
state owned), it was coded as 1, and 0 otherwise (Marquis & 
Qian, 2014; Sun et al., 2016).
POE from SOE
A binary variable was used to measure the formation of a 
private firm. That is, among the POEs, a firm was coded 1 
if it transformed from a traditional SOE to a POE, and 0 
otherwise (i.e., POEs that were originally founded by private 
owners).
Control Variables
To rule out possible alternative explanations, we included 
several control variables at different levels. Firm size was 
measured by the natural logarithm of the firm’s total assets. 
Firm growth was measured by the percentage of the annual 
increase in total sales revenues of the firm. Profitability was 
measured by the operating profit over the total sales of the 
firm. Ownership concentration is common among firms in 
emerging economies; thus, ownership concentration was 
measured by the ownership percentage of the largest share-
holder of the firm. Patents was measured by the total number 
of patents that had been applied for by the focal firm in a 
given year to control for innovation capability (Jia et al., 
2019; Zhou et al., 2017). Moreover, we controlled for TMT- 
and board-level variables because they have been found to 
influence the strategic decisions of firms, including R&D 
investment and CSR reporting. TMT size was measured by 
the total number of members of the TMT of the firm. TMT 
education was measured by the average educational level of 
TMT members, varying from secondary school or below to

Analytical Method
Our sample consists of longitudinal data; thus, we employed 
fixed-effects regressions in all the model analyses. The rea-
son is that ordinary least squares (OLS) regression is not 
appropriate due to its associated correlated error terms 
and understated standard errors. After conducting the 
Breusch–Pagan Lagrange multiplier test, the panel-data 
model was found to be more appropriate than the OLS 
model. Next, we conducted the Hausman (1978) test to 
examine which panel model (random effects or fixed effects) 
was preferred, and the result of the model specification sug-
gested that the fixed-effects model is more appropriate for 
this study (p = 0.000). Furthermore, all our independent, 
moderating and control variables were lagged by one year 
to mitigate any potential problems of endogeneity in all 
models.

Discussion and Conclusion
In this study, we draw on the co-evolutionary perspective 
to highlight the benefits of a combined strategy of CSR 
and innovation on firm market value. We contribute to the 
literature on innovation management, business ethics, and 
the co-evolutionary perspective in transitional economies. 
First, extending recent studies (Bocquet et al., 2017; Ho 
et al., 2016; Hull & Rothenburg, 2008; Mishra, 2017), we 
argue and find a complementary effect of CSR and innova-
tion based on observations of firms in a context that has 
experienced significant economic transitions. This is par-
ticularly important to understand the signaling effect of CSR 
reporting on R&D investments in transitional economies. 
For example, Hull and Rothenburg (2008) suggest that CSR 
may provide less innovative companies with a significant 
edge to differentiate based on data from 69 US companies 
from 1998 to 2001. Extending this line of research, our study 
shows that a combined strategy of CSR and innovation can 
help firms create moral capital, thereby leading credence 
to their innovation efforts and subsequently improving firm 
performance. By showing the benefits of CSR reporting on

be relieved, especially from a dynamic perspective. Thus, 
it provides practical and policy implications for firms in 
transitional economies where the potential returns on R&D 
investment can be discounted because of the low level of

Moreover, we consider organizational diversity in transi-
tional economies and examine how the joint effects of CSR 
and R&D investment vary across different types of firms. 
Our findings indicate that CSR reporting is especially valu-
able for firms with high uncertainty and low legitimacy. 
Incorporating the liability of newness and the liability of 
being private adds nuance to the growing body of research 
on CSR in the context of transitional economies (e.g., Chen 
et al., 2016; Li & Zhang, 2010). Given that CSR engagement 
and reporting are still relatively new phenomena in China 
(Luo et al., 2017; Tan & Tang, 2016), our study broadens 
this line of research by highlighting the heterogeneity and 
complexity of the effects of CSR and innovation engagement 
in transitional economies. For instance, our research indi-
cates that start-ups and POEs have seen an increase in the 
importance of CSR reporting in their pursuit of innovation 
and market value. Awareness of these findings can be valu-
able to different types of organizations in making decisions 
regarding their CSR and innovation engagement.
To deepen our understanding of the co-evolution of CSR 
engagement and innovation inputs in a transitional process, 
we add a temporal dimension and theorize how the joint 
effect of CSR and R&D investment on financial perfor-
mance co-evolves with firm-level transformations over time. 
One interesting finding is that the facilitating role of CSR 
becomes less pronounced when start-up firms transition to 
established firms, but makes no difference whether POEs are 
transformed from SOEs or not. This indicates that among 
start-ups and POEs, firms’ current statuses matter more 
than their historical origins in determining CSR and R&D 
investment implications. Our findings also suggest that in 
transitional economies with rapid institutional changes and 
organizational transitions, it is essential to adopt a dynamic 
approach to account for the co-evolution of innovation prac-
tices and business ethics along with organizational form 
transformations over time.
As with all studies, our research findings need to be inter-
preted with caution; however, the limitations of this study 
can also provide opportunities for future research. First, 
while CSR reporting is voluntary in China and many other 
countries, prior research has found that some firms engage 
in CSR disclosure due to institutional and political pres-
sures, such as government funding and explicit or implicit 
disclosure requirements (Lounsbury, 2001; Luo et al., 2017). 
Such pressures constitute a new dimension when examining 
the effect of CSR reporting on the R&D investments and 
performance of firms. Therefore, we believe that differen-
tiating ‘nominal’ from ‘genuine’ voluntary CSR reporting 
practices can deepen our understanding of the effects of CSR 
reporting on R&D investment and firm performance. Sec-
ond, although this study focuses on two critical scenarios of 
organizational form evolution (i.e., start-up transition and 
POE transformation), other possible evolutions, such as for-
eign firm localization, exist. In this regard, we encourage 
future research to examine the interplay of CSR and innova-
tion strategies with other types of organizational evolution.
