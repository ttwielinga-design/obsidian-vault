Four AAAA’s for the effective leader  
Leadership is put to the test in interactions with people. What attitude is needed to be effective, 
especially in communicating with others? I offer you the four AAAAs as a mnemonic: Attention, 
Connection (~accept another person’s reality), Acceptance-without-judgment, and Authenticity. 
Being attentive, fully present 
One of the most important conditions for a successful conversation is having full attention, being 
there for the full 100%. Being present and paying attention means that you clear your head. It means 
that you neatly store all your own worries, tasks you still have to do, conversations you still have to 
have, and emotions you are feeling in the drawer of your brain and only have the drawer of this 
conversation open. This also means that you are fully present in the here-and-now. The other person 
then experiences that you show focused interest and take your time.  
If for some reason you cannot be fully present for the other person, it is better not to have a 
conversation at that time. The leader must ensure that he is fully present. 
Connecting with the other person's story (~accept another person’s reality) 
Complete attention and presence is necessary to be able to connect to the subjective world of 
experience and thinking of the other person.  Only then can you follow him therein. For example: 
• 
What exactly do you mean with "she should know better"? 
• 
You use the word 'bastard', what does that mean to you? 
Following the other person in their story gives them the space to tell their story in their own way and 
that gives you more information. Connecting and following also means effectively responding to 
what the other is presenting:  
• 
"She doesn't like me!"; How do you know that? What do you notice that indicates this? 
Note: Getting the other person to stop talking, when you no longer see the connection or feel that it 
is distracting details, is also a form of connecting. For example: 
• 
Wait a minute, now I can't follow you anymore, I thought we were talking about ... 
Accepting Without Judgment  
Being present for and connecting with the other person only works when we can accept them as 
they are, without judgment. This sounds very simple, but this is the hardest thing there is. Every 
human being who meets another is immediately inclined to a judgment and certainly if it fits an 
already existing prejudice: good, bad, nice, stupid, kind, unsympathetic, badly dressed, annoying, 
friendly, disgusting, unhealthy and so on. That's how people do, that's how people are. Even people 
of good will who have good intentions for each other are equipped with prejudices and "filters" that 
can impede connection and acceptance. Managers are people too. The stronger the filter, the 
greater the distortion. 
If you really want to perceive clearly and purely, you should not be preoccupied with all kinds of 
thoughts and feelings about the situation. Then you no longer hear what is really being said; your 
head is already filled with other things. You can be aware of your own reactions and (pre)judgments, 
but then park them for a moment and keep your head free and your heart open. Through an 
attentive, accepting attitude you say to the other: I really try to understand you. 

Accepting means in the first place: accepting yourself. You can only give something to another when 
you have it yourself. Only when you can let go of your own judgments (and also your own fear of 
failure) can you be there for the other.  
Accepting-without-judgment does not mean that you agree with everything the other person says, 
or that you have no judgment about what you hear. It doesn't mean that you don't have or aren't 
allowed to have your own opinion, or that you don't give it your own meaning. It does mean that 
you put aside what you think for a moment and are aware that it matters what you want to achieve 
in conversation with the other person.  
Authenticity, genuineness 
Both adults and children have an infallible sense of when someone is faking it. Those who act 
attentive fall through the cracks; those who are truly attentive are genuine. If you want to be liked, 
you should not act nice but be nice. 
Managers and coaches need to be aware in every fiber of their being: be who you are, be yourself 
(there are already enough others). Be aware of your own qualities and of your pitfalls and things you 
still want to learn.  
It means that you are able to experience yourself in the here-and-now and be yourself so that this 
has a positive effect on the working relationship. The reverse also appears to be true: when 
authenticity is absent, it stands in the way of an effective working relationship. Authenticity is being 
yourself completely, letting the other experience who and how you are, without going too far. It is 
about who and how you are, not about how you act. Don't play a role because you can't keep that 
up. Whoever can really connect attentively and acceptingly, can listen sharply and observe. If you 
are also authentic, it makes it easier for the other person to relate to you.
