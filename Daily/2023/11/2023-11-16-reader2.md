SIMPLE REGRESSION: THE MODEL
In the previous chapter we investigated the existence of a linear relationship between two
variables. Once we have established that
• such a linear relation exists, and
• the relationship is a causal one (that is: one of the variables depends on the other one),
we want to describe this relation by means of a linear equation. This equation can be used to
predict the value of the dependent variable (also called the outcome variable) based on the
value of the independent one (also called the predictor variable).
Simple linear regression is a statistical method that helps you to ﬁnd, for a given data set,
the ’best’ linear equation. The straight line corresponding with this equation is called the
regression line.
For the situation in Example1 of the foregoing chapter the living space of a property is the
predictor variable and the selling price is the outcome variable. We shortly repeat this situation
in the following example.

THE LINEAR MODEL
When we consider the scatter plot in Figure 1, then it is obvious that the data points ﬂuctuate
about the line. This makes it necessary to construct a probabilistic model that acknowledges
the random variation about a line.
The (linear) regression model we use to describe the relation between a predictor variable
X and a outcome variable Y will be
Y = (β0 + β1X) + ε.
Here
• β0 and β1 are numbers that are typical for the relevant population
• ε is a random variable with a normal N(0, σ2
ε) distribution.
The random variable ε, which is called the error term, summarizes all kind of factors that de-
termine, apart from X, the value of Y . In other words: the error term models the ﬂuctuations
about the regression line.

In this section we discuss an F-test that can be used to test the signiﬁcance of the regression
model. For simple regression, this test is another way to test the null hypothesis H0: β1 = 0
versus H1: β1 ̸= 0. The test statistic is the overall F statistic
F =
SSM
SSR/(n −2).

The null hypothesis is rejected if F is large, that is: when the explained variation is large
compared to the unexplained variation.

MULTICOLLINEARITY
In a model with more than one predictor variable there could exist a linear relationship between
two or more of the predictors. If such a relation exists we speak of multicollinearity.
Multicollinearity creates a problem in the multiple regression model because it can produce
estimates of the regression coeﬃcients that are not statistically signiﬁcant. In a sense, it can
be thought of as a kind of double-counting in the model.
114

It also causes a problem with the interpretation of a regression coeﬃcient.
A coeﬃcient
represents the change in the dependent variable for a 1 unit change in the relevant predictor
variable, when you hold all of the other independent variables constant. However,
when predictor variables are related, a change in one variable is associated with shifts in
another variable!

• by considering the correlation coeﬃcients, and
• by ﬁnding the V(ariance) I(inﬂation F(actors).

eﬃcients (for the variables in the model). A correlation (for two predictors variables) with

MODERATION
In Section 2 – see for instance Figure 1 – we showed that the relation between the size of
an apartment and its price depends on its location. More speciﬁcally, the variable ’location’
eﬀects the strength and direction of the relationship between ’price’ and ’size’. In this situation
’location’ is called a moderator variable.
A moderator variable is one that eﬀects the strength or direction of a relationship between
two others.
This can be visualized by the path diagram
size
price
location
The horizontal arrow in this diagram visualizes the (causal) relation between the predictor
variable ’size’ and the outcome variable ’price’. The vertical arrow reﬂects the situation that
the variable ’location’ aﬀects (or moderates) the direction or strength of the relation between
’size’ and ’price’.
Basically, one shows that a variable M is a moderator variable for the relationship between a
predictor variable X and an outcome variable Y , by
•
considering the model
Y = β0 + β1X + β2M + β3XM + ε
•
proving that the coeﬃcient β3 signiﬁcantly diﬀers from zero.
In summary: one shows that the variable M is a moderator by proving that the variables X
and M interact.
example
Suppose you want to investigate the relation between ’loss of weight’ and ’training duration’.
More speciﬁcally, you want to know how the length of someone’s training explains his or her
loss of weight. To answer this question we could introduce the regression model
Y = β0 + β1X + ε,
where Y represents loss of weight (in kg) and X is the duration of the training (in hours).
Probably this model is too simple: the relation under consideration could be diﬀerent for men
and women. In other words: someone’s gender inﬂuences the relation between ’weight of loss’
and ’training duration’. In this situation the variable ’gender’ is a moderator variable.

REPEATED MEASURES ANOVA
A repeated measures ANOVA is used in two situations:
(1)
the same group of participants has been ’measured’ over multiple time points to see if
there have been any changes, usually in response to an intervention; or
(2)
one group of participants has been subjected to more than one condition/trial and the
response to each of these conditions is to be compared.
example 1
Assume you might want to measure the resting heart rate of subjects one month before they
start a training program, during the middle of the training program, and one month after the
training program to see if there is a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in mean resting heart rate across
these three time points.
In this situation the same participants show up at each time point.
First we reconsider this problem for the situation with two groups.
1
COMPARING TWO GROUPS
In general, we consider pairs
(X1, Y1), . . . , (Xn, Yn)
of scores. Each pair of scores corresponds to one entity.
We focus on the diﬀerences
D1 = X1 −Y1, . . . , Dn = Xn −Yn.
One assumes that this group of diﬀerences is taken from a population with a N(µ, σ2) distri-
bution (with µ and σ2 unknown).
We want to test
H0: µ = 0
H1: µ ̸= 0.
