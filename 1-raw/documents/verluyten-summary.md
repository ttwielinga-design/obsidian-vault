**Verluyten CH.1**

Intercultural blindness is a major cause of failure of many business ventures in foreign countries. Although intercultural differences are not overlooked anymore, there is still resistance against intercultural training in businesses. This is especially the case in small and medium-sized companies.

A few reasons not to use intercultural training:

1.  Experience is better than theoretical knowledge.

    1.  *However, preparatory training is essential in providing people with the necessary tools to start, and thus avoid costly mistakes.*

2.  Fear for ‘opening Pandora’s box’. Meaning that things may come out which run against the prevailing ideology.

3.  Worries about the implications of an intercultural diagnosis to a problem.

International trade has been around since the beginning of time, however, now more than ever, economic success means doing business internationally. With doing business internationally, intercultural skills are required. For example, negotiations, international marketing and mergers. A major problem is the assumption that intercultural skills are easily acquired ‘on the spot’. The more contact you have with a cultural group, the better you can communicate with them. However, basic cultural training is required to improve communications with different cultural groups.

Intercultural skills are required when you are involved with the following:

1.  Having contacts with people from different cultures

2.  Buy and sell abroad

3.  Represent your country at international conferences and meetings

4.  Welcome foreign visitors to your country or company

5.  Market goods or services internationally and adapt them to foreign markets

6.  Work as an expatriate, including in diplomatic service or on UN missions

7.  Getting into a joint venture or a merger with a partner from another country

8.  Better integrate migrant workers and minorities into the work force.

- Failure rates for expatriates range from 10%-40%. An intercultural training program would probably cost less that the cost of returning home prematurely. Besides that, in almost 75% of mergers, no attention is paid to cultural aspects. There is probably a direct link between the lack of attention for cultural aspects, and the 50% failure rate in mergers.

Cultural groups are mostly divided by countries. Reasons for this division:

1.  A convenient number. There are around 200 countries. This is not too much or too less.

2.  Clear membership. There are clear borders and there is a clearly defined membership.

3.  A clearly defined societal framework.

4.  Empirical evidence.

- In short, national cultures have a clear, well-known concept and there is often an unambiguous definition of membership.

**Ecological Fallacy**: *Assumption that people will by necessity exhibit the characteristics that are typical for the place they inhabit or originate from*

Human beings must be defined in a multidimensional space. Examples of dimensions:

1.  Social class

2.  Gender

3.  Age

4.  Professional culture

5.  Corporate culture

6.  Situational factors

7.  Individual features

--\> The opposite of ecological fallacy is the complete denial of culturally determined characteristics.

For ICB culture means: ‘the values and practices that are acquired and shared by people in a group.’ Also, ‘the particular solutions which societies give to universal problems.’

**Onion Model:** The relationship between values and practices. The core of a culture is its values, which are expressed in practices.

Two warnings:

1.  Values may remain hidden and not manifest themselves in any clear way for a long time.

2.  A large number of practices cannot be traced back to underlying values.

**Verluyten CH 2.**

**Self-Reference Criterion (SRC):** You rely on your own set of values and experiences to make an assumption about things regarding other people.

**Attribution**: Ascribing an interpretation to what people around you say and do.

**Attribution mistake:** Ascribing to the other person’s words or deeds a meaning which does not correspond to the intended meaning.

Every attribution error is:

1.  Reversible Person A may get a mistaken impression of B, while B may get the reverse mistaken impression of A.

2.  Transitive What person A thinks about person B, person C thinks about person A.

**Verluyten CH. 8**

**Power Distance**: The certain amount of inequality in a society.

Characteristics:

- Managers will be more autocratic in high PD cultures and more participative in low PD cultures.

- In high PD cultures, powerholders must try to look as powerful as possible. In low PD cultures, powerholders try to play down the differences.

- In high PD cultures, powerholders tend to be more entitled to privileges.

- In high PD cultures, organizations tend to be more hierarchical. (Taller organizational pyramid, bigger difference between top positions and low positions).

- Organizations in different PD cultures should use different management styles.

- PDI differences between different cultures are in the same order of magnitude as differences between different social/professional classes.

5.  *Getting acquainted and being part of the in-group\*
    In group orientated cultures, you need to get acquainted with someone and get into his in-group, before you do business with him.

**Verluyten CH. 14**

**Acculturation**: The adaption process of migrants and sojourners who move to a new country and culture.

Typology of adaptation strategies

1.  *No communication*\
    Refusing the intercultural encounter. Seems inappropriate, but the intercultural communication process is not always possible or advisable. For example, sending a female to consult in a country where women have fewer rights.

2.  *Communication without adaption*

    1.  No need\
        Some intercultural encounters will go smooth without any adaption necessary. High degree of awareness is necessary however, to avoid attribution mistakes.

    2.  No willingness\
        In case of deep-seated cultural values. But also: refusing new foods.

    3.  Impossibility\
        Do not speak the language, cannot adapt to the used non-verbal communication. Does not need to lead to problems, as long as there is awareness.

3.  *Bilateral adaption(compromise)*\
    May be the most natural outcome of an intercultural encounter. All about making compromises. Again, awareness is necessary.

4.  *Unilateral adaption*\
    Seems the most common. Only one party adapts, most of the time the visiting party.

**Verluyten CH 7 - Etiquette**

Mastery of etiquette in a foreign country can ensure good contacts with people from other cultures. The rules of etiquette are not universal, but are often conscious and visible. The origin of the rules is often forgotten.

1.  **Greetings**

In every culture, people perform a greeting ritual when they meet someone for the first time. In the West, this is often shaking hands, but how this is done is culturally specific. In Arabic countries, men do not usually shake hands with women. In Japan, people bow to each other, how low has to do with social status.

2.  **Gift giving**

Exchanging gifts can create good relationships between people. In Western countries this is often less important and less attention and money is spent on business gifts compared to, for example, countries in Asia.

3.  **Dining, smoking and entertainment**

In many countries business lunches and dinners are important for a business relationship. In China, the way you eat is related to the way you do business. It also varies from country to country which foods are taboo. In many countries, especially in southern Europe, vegetarian dishes are not served. Table manners are also culture-specific, as is the serving of food. It also varies by culture who pays the bill. Outside the West, it is usually the person who invited the other person. Drinking habits vary by culture. In Central and Eastern Europe, drinking together (alcohol) is part of the socialization process. A final point is the difference in entertainment. In East and Southeast Asian countries, going to a karaoke bar is common, but in non-Muslim Asian countries, sexual entertainment is common. In general, you can politely decline, sometimes this is even valued more highly.

4.  **Hands, feet, the head and other taboos**

Etiquette consists of a set of customs that regulate behavior during social events in a society. Often, blunders by foreigners regarding etiquette are accepted, but some behaviors are best avoided. For example, in Arab countries it is taboo to use your left hand in interactions and to show the sole of your shoes. In many cultures there are taboos on the female body.

5.  **Belief systems and "superstition "**

In Arabic cultures it is believed that words can have an effect on events. Therefore, swearing and referring directly to bad events is not a good idea in these countries. In many cultures bad news has to be covered up or delivered very carefully. In many Southeast Asian cultures, words and numbers have a symbolic meaning that can have a great influence on reality. It is important how a word sounds and how it is written. Unfortunately, many of the world's (powerful) institutions and major corporations continue to make decisions based on their own Self Reference Criterion.

**Verluyten CH 11 – Uncertainty Avoidance: stress, anxiety and fear of the unknown**

Geert Hofstede’s dimension of uncertainty avoidance deals with the level of fear related to the future that is present in a culture. In countries with high uncertainty avoidance people are more stressed and will try to control the future, for example through rules, work stability and saving. In countries with high uncertainty avoidance, people prefer a stable job to a more challenging but temporary position.

1.  **Country scores on the Uncertainty Avoidance Index (UAI)**

Based on the scores of different countries on Uncertainty Avoidance Index (UAI), we again see different groups of countries emerging. Latin American and European Romantic countries (Portugal, France, Spain, Italy and Belgium) have high UAI scores, while Anglo-Saxon, Asian and Scandinavian countries have low scores. We see a strong relationship between uncertainty avoidance and age.

2.  **The UAI Scores in the workplace**

In organization certain rituals are used which have as main function the removal of uncertainty: -

- The appointment of experts

- Consultants from outside to reach consensus

- Memos and reports

- Sure, parts of the accounting system

- A fair part of the control system

- A fair part of the planning system

Many forms of registration can also be seen as uncertainty avoidance rituals (think of identity cards). Hofstede predicts that high uncertainty avoidance countries have higher rates of racism, intolerance, and political totalitarianism. Lower uncertainty avoidance is likely to lead to a preference for multiculturalism and more emigration. In countries with high uncertainty avoidance, workers like clear instructions and little authorization from managers. In countries with lower uncertainty aversion, managers focus on strategy and employees work out the details, allowing more authorization. Managers are more willing to take risks and people change jobs more easily. There is more innovation and creativity. People want fewer rules and are more likely to break them. Protest is more easily accepted in these countries.

3.  **Uncertainty Avoidance: discussion**

A number of characteristics mentioned by Hofstede relate to the fact that high uncertainty avoidance goes hand in hand with high anxiety and stress. In low UAI countries, people would be more restless and less inclined to work hard. However, when looking at uncertainty avoidance scores across multiple countries, there is no clear correlation with lower stress. Other features are that in high uncertainty avoidance cultures, or high anxiety cultures, people are more likely to express emotions. This does seem to be true, except for Japan. People from cultures with higher uncertainty avoidance may react aggressively to uncertainty.

**Chapter 13** Attitudinal issues and ethics

1.  **Ethnocentrism**

Ethnocentrism is the view that one's own culture is superior in comparison to other cultures. This was the prevailing thought in Western Europe in the 19th century and a cause of colonialism. The 'wild' indigenous people in Africa had to be 'civilized'. This ethnocentrism still exists, but not among economically successful countries. With economically poor countries, Westerners still have the idea that they should help them.

2.  **Universalism and cultural relativism**

Anthropologists have come to the conclusion that there are no substantial human values that are universal. An alternative is cultural relativism, in which each culture is judged by its own terms and thus no culture is better than another. The comment is that one culture can be criticized from another culture and it is not possible to have a dialogue between cultures. Evan off gives constructivism as an alternative. Authors still often use their own standards to judge other cultures. Verluyten concludes that different (cultural) values are unique and that cultural relativism is the only option. Many typical western norms are even on a global scale just an exception (e.g., gender) and the perception of objects is not always equal. Nevertheless, the author believes that there is a reasonably stable basis of human perception.

3.  **Minimizing and normalizing differences**

Many advocates of a multicultural society are often also advocates of human rights. This leads to problems because many cultures do not apply the same human rights. Often the differences are denied or minimized or it is argued that culturally determined characteristics have arisen due to influence from the West. The author believes that we can only achieve intercultural understanding if we admit the shocking differences.

4.  **Cultural bias**

    1.  *Cultural bias in publications*

Because of our cultural backgrounds it is difficult to avoid cultural bias, a milder form of ethnocentrism.

2.  *Hofstede's survey and the Chinese Values Survey (CVS)*

Hofstede also sees cultural bias in his research. Despite the attention to wording, the questions chosen were made from a Western point of view. Not all questions are relevant in all cultures and certain value systems cannot be discovered. Michael Bond, a Canadian researcher, had Chinese social scientists create a list of basic values relevant to Chinese people. From the questionnaire (Chinese Value Survey) a number of dimensions matched Hofstede’s dimensions, but the interpretation of the dimensions and the associated virtues do differ.
