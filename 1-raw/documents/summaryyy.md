**1.2 – Data collection**

**Quantitative data** refers to any information that can be quantified, counted or measured, and given a numerical value.

**Qualitative data** is descriptive in nature, expressed in terms of language rather than numerical values.

Based on the central question you can decide what question you will use. Then you will go on to make the concepts measurable. Finally, you go on to collect the data.

The main quantitative types of methodologies are:

1.  Surveys

2.  Experiments

3.  Secondary analysis

4.  Real-time data: data that is collected from mobile phones, GPS, and the internet

The main qualitive methodologies are:

1.  Interviews

2.  Observation

3.  Qualitative desk research

Quality of the research can be divided into two parts:

1.  Reliability: the extent to which your research contains random errors

2.  Validity: the extent to which your research contains systematic errors

    1.  Internal validity: the extent to which it is possible to draw the right conclusions

    2.  External validity: the extent to which your results are generalizable

    3.  Construct validity: the extent to which you are measuring what you want to measure

In order to draw a sample, you have 2 options:

1.  Probability or random

    1.  Simple random sampling 🡪 a simple sample of all units in the population

    2.  Stratified sampling 🡪 the population consists of existing subpopulations. A random sample is drawn from each stratum

    3.  Systematic with a random starting point 🡪 a starting point is selected randomly but the interval in which a person is chosen is set.

    4.  Cluster sampling 🡪 the population consists of cluster; a sample of clusters is drawn and these a=clusters are studied in their entirety (certain classes in a school)

    5.  Multi-staged sampling 🡪 this is a combination of several sampling methods, generally based on a clustered.

2.  Non-probability sample

    1.  Purposive sampling 🡪 you interview people based on their specific experience.

    2.  Snowball sampling 🡪 interview one person and through them get to another one until you have your interviews.

    3.  Quota sampling 🡪 set up what type of people you want to interview before hand

    4.  Self-selective sampling 🡪 flyering / advertisements

    5.  Convenience sampling 🡪 asking the 1<sup>st</sup> few people you see for an interview

Data collection for quantitative data:

- The sample must be sufficiently large to be reliable

- There must not be too many missing values, because then the data set is less representative

- For good construct validity and reliability, you need good questions and experimental instruments

For qualitative data

- Properly trained and prepared interviewers

- Large and random samples are not necessary, but you need enough information for theoretical generalizability’

- Good questions and the right observation categories enhance construct validity.

**2.4– Measures of central tendency**

The **median** indicates the exact middle of a (sorted) distribution, i.e., the middle observation. The median can be applied to variables from ordinal measurement level onwards.

The **mean** is calculated for variables from interval or ratio measurement levels. Is i.e., because means are only meaning full if you can use the values in calculations, if they are numeric and continuous.

**Weighted mean** is the same as a normal mean but you multiply the “grade” by the weight.

**2.3 – Measures of variability**

Simplest way to show the distribution of a variable is to show the range. This is the whole range of the observation, form min to max.

Interquartile range is a measure that you use to look at the middle 50% of your observations

- Evaluative

- Tracking trends

- Design

Well-defined objective:

- Tells you what kind of research it is: qualitative or quantitative

- Indicates the relevance for practice

- Mentions the client’s objectives and requirements

**Chapter 7**

**7.1 – Surveys**

Most commonly used type of quantitative data collection. The most important features are:

1.  They research a large group of people

2.  They are done at or on point in time

3.  They research knowledge, attitudes and perceptions (KAP)

4.  They ask descriptive and/or evaluative questions

5.  The questionnaires are structured or semi-structured

6.  They involve a large number of questions

7.  The samples are drawn randomly

8.  The data is analyzed quantitatively

**7.2 – secondary analysis**

Pro’s:

- Saves time

- Financial advantages

- Availability of the data

- Usability

Con’s

- Don’t have any control over the data

- Data set has already been created

- You have to find solutions for any errors you may find

- It may be that you have to adjust your questions to use some data sets

- You need to thoroughly process the data before use

**Meta-analysis**: Numerical comparison of large number of files on the same subject, with the results being re-analyzed

**Systematic review**: Research into original studies on the subject of your research

**7.3 – experimental research**

Collecting data from test subjects in a controlled situation.

**Quasi-experiment or field experiment**: Experiments that use “existing” situations, not a pure experiment i.e., not in a laboratory

**Double blind testing**: neither the subjects nor the doctor “treating” them knows which of the pills are real and which are placebos

**Chapter 9**

**9.1 – Reliability**

Checking reliability

1.  For quantitative research in particular

    1.  Sample size

    2.  Test-retest

2.  For quantitative and qualitative

    1.  Standardization (design standard questions) (using a system for analysis)

    2.  Pilot interview / project (first do test interviews for interviews and surveys)

    3.  Four-eye principle (peer feedback)

    4.  Reporting and justification (making notes during the project and of changes within it)

3.  For qualitative research

    1.  Inter-rater reliability / intersubjectivity (several researches observe the situation. The extend to which there is an agreement determines the intersubjectivity, which needs to be as high as possible.

    2.  Triangulation (The reliability is enhanced because you use various methods)

    3.  Iteration (observe analyses and interpret. Repeat until you can answer your central question)

**9.2 – validity**

**Validity** – the extent to which the research is free of systematic errors

**Systematic errors** – errors that have a systematic (not coincidental) cause that (consistently) lead to a result that is different to the expected value measurement

Types of Validity:

- **Face validity** 🡪 expert judgement

- **Content validity** 🡪 completeness: are you measuring all the specs?

- **Criterion validity** 🡪 is there a correlation between two measurements

- **Construct validity** 🡪 am I measuring what I want to measure?

- **Internal validity** 🡪 Extent to which accurate conclusions can be drawn from the results

- **External validity** 🡪 can you generalize your results from internal to external?

There are some cases where internal validity can be jeopardized:

1.  Selection

2.  Maturation (if the research goes on for long changes will automatically take place)

3.  External incidents / history

4.  Instrumentation (Not using the same questionnaire the entire interview)

Threats to validity:

- Participation error

- Participant bias

- Observer error 🡪 fatigue

- Observer bias 🡪 you are human and subjective

- Situational contaminants 🡪 instead or in real life on the phone

- Instrument clarity 🡪 participant’s background / culture

**Morality** – test subject drops out that leads to the systematic skewing of the results

**Usability**: the extend to which research has practical relevance

- **Conceptual usability**: The results are used to start a debate about a subject

- **Instrumental usability**: The results are used to draft policies
