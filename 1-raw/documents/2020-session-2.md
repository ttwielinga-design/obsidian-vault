steps to a killer presentation:

1.  Confident introduction

2.  Give your credentials – short but strong

3.  Deliver your hook

    1.  Ex Make the audience feel something

    2.  I’m going to show you…

        1.  You cant tell them what they are going to get until you tell them why they would want it.

4.  Introduce your agenda/topic

5.  Give a credible statement – short but strong

6.  What is the benefit for the audience?

Attention grabbers:

1.  Using interesting facts / statistics

2.  Using stories

3.  Using questions

4.  Using quotations

5.  Using silence

6.  Using humor

<p><em><strong>When I’m bored, I send a text to a random number, saying: “I hid the body, now what?” (Anonymous)</strong></em></p>
<p>We are pretty sure the quote got your attention, which is exactly what a good opening does – but, as you will have discovered by now, a r e a l l y good opening is so much more than that, because a r e a l l y good one involves the audience by showing it which needs it has that your presentation will meet. Which, by the by, is the notion that underlies the success of Apple (where Steve Jobs created products and then convinced the world it needed them), so there is a best practice you might want to follow.</p>
<p>In this session, you will be invited, in pairs, to deliver your killer opening of approximately one to two minutes. Note: you need not deliver the opening together, but you must have developed it together. Each opening is provided with feedback by the other students and is awarded a medal by each other pair plus your lecturer (40 minutes)</p>
<p>Next, each pair is set a TED talk to view in the course of the class. You need to analyse which presentation skills the speaker uses to make the presentation more attractive, list them in a Word-document and then forward the link plus the tips to the other pairs. At the end of the session, be prepared to explain what you feel is the single best tip ever to your classmates.</p></td>
