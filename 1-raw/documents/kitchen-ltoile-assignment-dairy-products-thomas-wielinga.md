**Assignment 1**

Before we can describe how cheese is made we need to talk about what ingredients go into cheese. The main ingredient of cheese is milk, this can be any kind of milk from the normal cow milk to goat, sheep water buffalo, or even a mix of these.

How you want your cheese to clutter together and how thick you want it to become all depends on the coagulant (=is a substance that causes particles in a liquid to curdle and clot together). 

Also sometimes other flavorings are added like herbs, wine, or peppers for example.

Now for the process. You can, dependent on your source, divide your process into 12 steps.

1.  The first step is to Standardize the milk. This is done so that you have consistent milk to fat ratio in your milk.

2.  After this, the milk gets pasteurized meaning that the milk will get heated. This is to reduce the bad bacteria in the milk. Some cheeses don’t get pasteurized, so they are made from raw milk, to make sure that it is still safe for you to eat they have to let the cheese mature for at least 60 days. 

3.  After pasteurization, the milk temperature will be reduced to 32°C to bring it to the temperature needed for the starter bacteria to grow. If raw milk is used the milk must be heated to 32°C.

4.  The starter cultures and any non-starter adjunct bacteria are added to the milk and held at 32°C for 30 minutes to ripen. The ripening step allows the bacteria to grow and begin fermentation, which lowers the pH and develops the flavor of the cheese.

5.  The rennet enzyme will react to the milk and turn the proteins into a more curdy texture. The rennet will sit in the milky substance for around 30 minutes

6.  The curd is allowed to ferment until it reaches pH 6.4. The curd is then cut with cheese knives into small pieces and heated to 38°C. The heating step helps to separate the whey (= The watery part of milk that remains after the formation of curds.) from the curd.

7.  After this, the whey is removed from the vat and the curd remains to form a mat.

8.  The curd mats are cut into sections and piled on top of each other and are flipped periodically. This step is called cheddaring. Cheddaring helps to make sure that more whey can get removed and allows the fermentation to continue until a pH of 5.1 to 5.5 is reached, and allows the mats to "knit"/connect and form a tighter matted structure. The curd mats are then cut into smaller pieces.

9.  The next step is to add salt to the curd or to put the curd into the brine (a saltwater solution)

10. After this, the salted curd pieces are placed in cheese hoops and pressed into blocks to form the cheese.

11. After this process, the cheeses get stored to age. The time varies between a couple of weeks to years depending on the cheese.

12. The last step is to package the cheese and/ or cut it into blocks so that it is ready to be eaten.

The differences between milk and cream are:

- Milk is thinner than cream

- Milk is whiter than cream

- Milk contains less fat

- The cream is a dairy product made from the butterfat in raw milk.

- Milks texture is more watery than cream 

- Milk has a less creamy taste

Unpasteurized cheese, like can be read above here in the ingredients part also known as raw-milk cheese, is made from milk that has not been pasteurized. Which entails that the milk has not been cooked.

The difference between young and mature (or old) cheese is age. Young cheese has riped for much less time than mature cheese.

**Assignment 5 – cheese board**

For the cheese board, I decided to go with two aged, two soft, two firm, and two blue kinds of cheese.

The cheeses that I chose are:

**Camembert - 4 weeks ripened (soft)**

- This young Camembert cheese is made with and has a milky, soft, and sweet flavor because it hasn’t matured for longer. 

**Capricorn British Goats Cheese – (soft)**

- Goat's milk cheese is produced in the lush valleys of Somerset. Soft and creamy, it has a mild, clean and nutty goat's cheese flavor. Each round has a soft rind and an oozing, gooey center, making it the perfect addition to any cheeseboard.

- Fun fact since 1983 Capricorn Somerset goat's cheese has won a staggering 83 awards and was recently voted “gold medal winner” at the 2016 International Cheese Awards in Nantwich.

As for the aged cheeses I have decided to go with the:

**Gruyère – 12 months (aged)**

- Gruyère is a hard yellow Swiss cheese that originated in the cantons of Fribourg, Vaud, Neuchâtel, Jura, and Berne in Switzerland. It is named after the town of Gruyères.

- The Gruyère cheese is a firm cheese with a pale yellow color and a rich, creamy, slightly nutty taste.

**Kernhem - 30 months (aged) - Semi soft cheese**

- Kernhem is made of Dutch cow milk and is only made in Ede, The Netherlands.

- Kernhem has a sticky orange rind that's been washed in brine, and underneath it, the texture is soft and gooey. The aromas are barnyard and the flavors are creamy and nutty, with an intense savory finish.

As for the Firm cheeses I decided to go with the:

**Manchego – 48 months (firm)**

- Manchego is a cheese made in the La Mancha region of Spain

- From the milk of sheep of the Manchega breed. 

- It is aged between 60 days and 2 years. As you can see here on the menu our Manchego has been aged for 48 months.

- Manchego has a firm and compact consistency and a buttery texture.

- It has an intense, zesty taste and a crumbly texture that's rich, full, and slightly salty at the finish.

**Parmigiano-Reggiano – 24 months (Firm)**

- Produced from cow's milk and aged at least 12 months. As you can see our Parmigiano has been aged for 24 months and can be called “Stravecchio” (=very old).

- It is made of unpasteurized milk.

- It has intense aromas and flavors of fruit, nuts, butter, and hay, and its high glutamate content produces a savory, flavor-enhancing quality.

- Parmigiano-Reggiano is a strictly controlled product and should not be mistaken for: “Parmesan” which is a non-European Union cheese.

- It can only be made in Emilia-Romagna, Lombardy

As for the blue cheeses I have decided to go for these cheeses:

**Gorgonzola – 4 months (blue)**

- Produced with cow milk and is only allowed to be produced in Gorgonzola, Italy

- Gorgonzola is a strict cow cheese 

- During the aging process, metal rods are quickly inserted and removed, creating air channels that allow the mold spores to grow into hyphae and cause the cheese's characteristic to a vein. Gorgonzola is typically aged for three to four months. Like you can see we chose a Gorgonzola that has aged for 4 months. The length of the aging process determines the consistency of the cheese, which gets firmer as it ripens.

- Gorgonzola has a sharp rich taste.

**Stilton cheese – 9 weeks (Blue)**

- Stilton is an English cheese

- It ripens for at least 9 weeks, as you can see we chose for the minimum to get the most creamy flavor possible since we think that this would be the best addition to our cheese platter. 

- It is made out of cow milk and originates from Derbyshire, Leicestershire, and Nottinghamshire, England

- Stilton has a creamy taste with a subtle, yeasty sweetness and a salty, nutty finish.
