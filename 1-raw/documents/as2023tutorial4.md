**Grammar: Active and Passive Construction**

In academic writing, we often use the passive voice when constructing sentences. It can also be useful paraphrasing practice to take an active sentence and turn it into a passive sentence (and vice versa). Although there has been a recent trend in academia towards more active sentence structure, there is great value in understanding the purpose of the passive voice, and then applying this understanding to your academic writing. (Foley & Hall, 2003, pp. 104-107)

Basic Patterns:

The **subject** of an active verb becomes <u>the agent</u> of a passive verb. We often do not mention the name of the agent in the passive form, but if we do, it is introduced with *by:*

Active form: The **mailman** *delivered* <u>the post</u>.

> **(subject)** (*verb)* (<u>noun clause)</u>

Passive form: **The post** *was delivered* (<u>by the mailman</u>).

We make the passive form of verbs in all tenses by using ***be** + **past participle*** of the main verb.

E.g., My bag ***was stolen***. (Active: A thief stole my bag)

E.g., The murder ***is being*** ***investigated*** (by the police). (Active: The police are investigating the murder)

**Uses of the Passive**

> 1\) If the agent is not known or not important (Unimportant agent)

- She ***was murdered***. (We don’t know who did it)

2)  To purposely avoid mentioning the agent to avoid blame or remain neutral (Not mentioning the agent)

- The washing up ***has not been done***.

3)  To focus on the idea instead of the actors: \[Commonly used in academic writing or formal English to describe rules, procedures and processes\].

    - The research ***was carried out*** over a period of six months.

    - Tribal lands ***were sold*** over a period of fifty years.

4)  To put new information at the end of a sentence (Information order)

- “Guernica” is a wonderful example of cubist art. It ***was painted*** by Picasso in 1937.

**Paragraph Structure**

Academic papers are divided into paragraphs. A paragraph is a group of related sentences that discuss one main idea. If you do not use paragraphs and your paper is just one long piece of writing, then it is difficult for any reader to follow your reasoning. The main idea of a paragraph is denoted by one sentence, usually at the beginning of the paragraph, called the **Topic Sentence**. This is followed by **supporting sentences** and a **concluding sentence**. [**Watch short video on paragraphing here**](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I0SMvLu2D1Y)

| **Topic Sentence – topic + controlling idea** | Research shows that e-tail customers are more demanding, less forgiving and more fickle. |
|----|----|
| **Supporting Sentences – develop the topic sentence** | The Web is fiercely competitive, and customers have access to unprecedented amounts of information. It takes more than a flashy website to convince them to part with their cash. An up-to-date and user-friendly website is taken as standard. |
| **Concluding Sentence – closing thought** (note, not every paragraph requires a concluding sentence; this is especially useful to end a chapter) | The issues that are decisive in whether a website is successful, and have competitive advantage, are still good old fashioned customer service and order fulfilment. |

**Topic Sentences**

The topic sentence states the main idea of the paragraph and also limits the topic to be discussed completely in the space of that paragraph. This focus is called the **controlling idea**. The topic sentence is the most important sentence in the paragraph to guide both the writer and the reader. It is also the most general statement in the paragraph, because the specific details will be discussed later.

**Coherence**

Another element of a good paragraph is **coherence** – meaning that the movement from one sentence to the next must be logical and smooth. There are four ways to achieve coherence:

1)  Repeat key nouns

2)  Use consistent pronouns (refers to a person)

3)  Use transition signals to link ideas

4)  Arrange your ideas in a logical order

**<u>How to refer to people in academic writing:</u>**

- Avoid you/we (this is vague/informal)

- Avoid exaggeration or overly general statements (people, everyone, etc.)

- Who are you talking about specifically – what group of people is your research based on?

- Refer to authors by their last names

- Avoid gender bias (he/she, they, one)

- To refer yourself as the author, use “I” first person voice

**Coherence using transition signals**

Transition signals are like traffic signs. They tell the reader when to go forward, look back, compare, to name just a few directions. They basically tell your reader when something is **similar** (*similarly, and, in addition, additionally*), **opposite** (*on the one hand/on the other hand, in contrast, but*), an **example** (*for example*), a **result** (*therefore, consequently*), or a **conclusion/summary** (*in conclusion, in sum, in essence*). Transition signals give a paragraph and paper coherence because they guide the reader from one idea to the next.

Transition signals are expressions such as *first, finally,* and *however*, or phrases such as *in conclusion, one the one hand/on the other hand* (must always be used together), and *as a result*. Other kinds of words like **subordinators** (*when, although*), **coordinators** (*and, but*), **adjectives** (*another, additional)*, and **prepositions** (*because of, in spite of*) can serve as transition signals.

Keep in mind that in academic writing, transitioning at the start of a sentence using a coordinator is not accepted. This means that you should not use ***and, but, besides* or *so*** to start a sentence. These words are of course acceptable when used in the middle of a sentence to connect ideas with a sentence.

**Concluding Sentences**

A concluding sentence serves two purposes. It signals the end of the paragraph and it leaves the reader with the most important ideas to remember. A closing sentence will either:

- Summarize the main points of the paragraph

- Repeat the topic sentence in different words

A concluding sentence is not always needed in a multi-paragraph essay. It is helpful to use a concluding sentence when you are done discussing one subject or section of a paper to show the end of this topic. Look at examples of closing sentences in tasks 3 and 4.

**The Writing Process: Organizing the body of essay**

Source: [Organization and Structure - Purdue OWL® - Purdue University](https://owl.purdue.edu/owl/graduate_writing/graduate_writing_topics/graduate_writing_organization_structure_new.html)

In order to clearly answer your research question, you need to consider how you can create a logical flow of ideas into chapters that will help your reader to understand the topic. Each chapter should help to develop your answer to the research question. What sub-questions need to be answered in order to address the main research question? In answering these questions, you are developing the argument of your paper.

To organise your paper around your **research question**, think about your answer, based on your research. This answer will eventually become the thesis statement or main claim that you are proving in the body of the paper. Unlike a **hypothesis**, which can be proven true or false, your **thesis statement** should always be proven true in the body of the paper. As you are writing the body of the paper, you may realise that you need more evidence to prove this claim, or that you actually cannot prove this claim. Your thesis statement can be revised as your continue writing and researching, but the body of the paper must prove the thesis statement is correct. We will spend more time working on the thesis statement in Week 5.

**Some tips for organizing your ideas into chapters:**

1)  Make decisions about which content from your sources to include based on your research question; **Combine and compare sources** when possible to create a clear argument

2)  **Each chapter should link to your research question** – think about which questions need to be answered in order to answer your main research question

3)  **Use keywords** in your chapter heading to identify the main theme of the chapter

4)  Begin with a **chapter introduction** of 2-3 sentences that explains how this chapter will address the research topic

5)  You may include **sub-chapter headings** to break down smaller themes within each chapter

**How does the structure of the paper answer the research question?**

- Funnel structure: more general information before more specific information

- Argumentative structure: your research leads to one main conclusion (proves the thesis statement)

- Analysis structure: each chapter is one variable/aspect of your research question, there is not one main conclusion; or you analyse both the pros/cons and don’t have one main conclusion

Sample Student Structure:

Research Question: How does CSR impact Firm Performance?

Thesis statement: CSR can have a positive impact on firm financial performance.

2.1 What is the purpose of CSR?

2.2 Stakeholder management & investing in CSR

2.3 Impact of CSR on firm financial performance

Look at the sample student structure above, which uses a **funnel structure** (more broad to more specific). Now think about your own chapter structure in your paper, did you use a funnel structure, or how did you decide to which chapter goes where?

This example uses an argumentative structure, the student will prove the thesis statement and has a clear conclusion that there is a positive impact from CSR on the financial performance of a firm. Does your paper use an **argumentative structure** (leads to one conclusion, usually proven in the final chapter of the body) or an **analysis structure** (looks at many variables, there is not one clear conclusion or relationship between the variables)?

**Argumentation**

Source: [Organizing Your Argument - Purdue OWL® - Purdue University](https://owl.purdue.edu/owl/general_writing/academic_writing/establishing_arguments/organizing_your_argument.html)

## How can I effectively present my argument?

In order for your argument to be persuasive, it must use an organizational structure that the audience perceives as both logical and easy to understand. One method of argumentation is the Toulmin Method (Stephen Toulmin, 1958). The Toulmin Method emphasizes building a thorough support structure for each of an argument's key claims.

The basic format for the **Toulmin Method **is as follows:

**Claim:** In this section, you explain your overall thesis on the subject. In other words, you make your main argument.

**Data (Grounds): **You should use evidence to support the claim. In other words, provide the reader with facts that prove your argument is strong.

**Warrant (Bridge):** In this section, you explain why or how your data supports the claim. As a result, the underlying assumption that you build your argument on is grounded in reason.

**Backing (Foundation):** Here, you provide any additional logic or reasoning that may be necessary to support the warrant.

**Counterclaim:** You should anticipate a counterclaim that negates the main points in your argument. Don't avoid arguments that oppose your own. Instead, become familiar with the opposing perspective.  If you respond to counterclaims, you appear unbiased (and, therefore, you earn the respect of your readers). You may even want to include several counterclaims to show that you have thoroughly researched the topic.

**Rebuttal:** In this section, you incorporate your own evidence that disagrees with the counterclaim. It is essential to include a thorough warrant or bridge to strengthen your essay’s argument. If you present data to your audience without explaining how it supports your thesis, your readers may not make a connection between the two, or they may draw different conclusions.

#### 

#### **EXAMPLE OF THE **TOULMIN METHOD:

**Claim: **Hybrid cars are an effective strategy to fight pollution.

**Data1:** Driving a private car is a typical citizen's most air-polluting activity.

**Warrant 1:** Due to the fact that cars are the largest source of private (as opposed to industrial) air pollution, switching to hybrid cars should have an impact on fighting pollution.

**Data 2:** Each vehicle produced is going to stay on the road for roughly 12 to 15 years.

**Warrant 2:** Cars generally have a long lifespan, meaning that the decision to switch to a hybrid car will make a long-term impact on pollution levels.

**Data 3:** Hybrid cars combine a gasoline engine with a battery-powered electric motor.

**Warrant 3:** The combination of these technologies produces less pollution.

**Counterclaim:** Instead of focusing on cars, which still encourages an inefficient culture of driving even as it cuts down on pollution, the nation should focus on building and encouraging the use of mass transit systems.

**Rebuttal: **While mass transit is an idea that should be encouraged, it is not feasible in many rural and suburban areas, or for people who must commute to work. Thus, hybrid cars are a better solution for much of the nation's population.

**Peer review and self-editing**

Peer editing is an interactive process of reading and commenting on a classmate’s writing. Giving and receiving feedback can significantly improve your writing. Feedback is best received when it meets the following criteria:

1)  The feedback given focuses on the content not the person

2)  **The feedback is specific.**

3)  The feedback is given in a way that allows the receiver to respond (e.g. Do you understand what I mean? Do you agree?)

4)  You formulate the feedback as an ‘I –message’ (e.g. “I think that…’) rather than a ‘you-message’ (e.g. “You do…”)

5)  Remember that your goal is to make suggestions to improve the writing, but the final decision is up to the writer to make any changes.

6)  Also, be sure to **start with positive feedback**, what does the writer do well?

7)  As a writer, it is up to you to decide if you agree with the feedback or not, and how to make improvements in your writing.
