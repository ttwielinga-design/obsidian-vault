**Question 5:** *Explain the ownership structure of your hotel and elaborate on the advantages and disadvantages for the owner and operator. * 

**Advantages for the Owner:**

1.  Income stream: Owning a hotel can provide a stable income stream, as hotels can generate revenue through room bookings, F&B sales, and other services.

2.  Long-term investment: Hotels can be a long-term investment, providing a steady income stream over several years, as they typically have a lifespan of several decades.

3.  Appreciation: If the hotel is located in a desirable location and experiences steady growth, its value can appreciate over time, resulting in potential capital gains for the owner.

**Disadvantages for the Owner:**

1.  High capital investment: Owning a hotel requires a high initial capital investment, making it difficult for many investors to enter the market.

2.  Operating costs: Running a hotel requires significant operating costs, including maintenance, utilities, staff salaries, and marketing expenses, which can impact the overall profitability of the hotel.

3.  Risk: The hotel industry is highly competitive and sensitive to market conditions, making it a high-risk investment for owners. External factors such as natural disasters, economic downturns, and changes in tourism trends can have a significant impact on the hotel's revenue and profitability.

**Advantages for the Operator:**

1.  Management fee: As an operator, the primary advantage is the management fee received for operating the hotel on behalf of the owner. This fee is typically a percentage of the hotel's revenue, and provides a stable income stream for the operator.

2.  Experience and expertise: Operators have the experience and expertise to run a hotel efficiently, including hiring staff, marketing the hotel, and managing operations.

3.  Brand recognition: If the operator is a well-known brand, this can help to increase the hotel's visibility and attract more guests.

**Disadvantages for the Operator:**

1.  Limited control: As an operator, the operator may have limited control over the hotel's operations and decision-making processes, as the ultimate authority rests with the owner.

2.  Risk: Operators also face the same risks as owners in terms of external factors affecting the hotel's revenue and profitability, as they are dependent on the success of the hotel.

3.  High competition: The hotel industry is highly competitive, and operators must constantly innovate and adapt to changing market conditions to remain competitive

**Question 7:** *Capital budgeting is used to plan capital investments. Elaborate on the difference between CAPEX and Repair & Maintenance. Which stakeholder(s) prefer(s) CAPEX over R&M? (Finance class 4) *

CAPEX refers to the funds used to acquire or upgrade long-term assets, such as new equipment, buildings, or technology. These expenditures are usually significant, require a large initial investment, and generate benefits over an extended period (min. 1 year). 

On the other hand, R&M expenses are the costs incurred to maintain and repair existing assets. These expenses are usually small, frequent, and ongoing, and they do not increase the asset's value or extend its useful life. 

Which stakeholders prefer what? 

- For operators they get incentives for revenues and GOP. Any CAPEX effecting these two will be welcomed. Since CAPEX does not affect the GOP. 

- For brands, the incentive is to increase revenues through franchise fees, and therefore, CAPEX may be required to attract and retain customers. However, brands are not responsible for paying the CAPEX bill. Instead, the owners bear the cost of the CAPEX investment, and the brands benefit from increased revenues. 

<!-- -->

- For owners, minimizing cash outflows and maximizing cash inflows are the primary concerns when considering CAPEX and R&M expenses. While CAPEX investments may be necessary to maintain or increase revenues and profitability, owners will likely try to minimize the costs of these investments. Similarly, R&M expenses that prevent major repairs and reduce downtime can help minimize costs and maximize cash inflows. 

  

**Question 8:** *Which methods can your case hotel use to make capital budgeting decisions? Elaborate on every method. *

There are 3 main methods (kijk naar finance class 5) 

- Discounted Cashflow (DCF) / Net Present Value (NPV): DCF is a method of calculating the present value of future cash flows of an investment after accounting for the time value of money, using a discount rate. The NPV is the difference between the present value of the cash inflows and outflows of an investment. A positive NPV indicates that the investment will generate a return greater than the required rate of return, and therefore, the investment is considered financially viable. This method takes into account the time value of money and is useful for evaluating long-term investments. 

  

- Payback period: The payback period is the length of time it takes for an investment to generate enough cash flows to recover the initial investment. This method is used to evaluate the time it takes to recover the investment and the risk involved in the investment. Projects with shorter payback periods are generally preferred because they recover the initial investment more quickly, providing the business with more financial flexibility. 

  

- Accounting Rate of Return: The accounting rate of return (ARR) is the ratio of the average annual profit generated by an investment to the initial investment cost. This method is used to evaluate the profitability of an investment over a specific period. The ARR is expressed as a percentage and provides an estimate of the average annual return on the investment. The ARR method does not account for the time value of money and is generally not considered as accurate as the NPV method. It is useful when evaluating the expected profitability of a project over a short time frame. 

  

  

**Question** **9:** *Explain the Internal Rate of Return and elaborate how the case hotel can use the IRR in capital budgeting decisions. Link the IRR to the WACC. *

<img src="media/image6.png" style="width:6.26806in;height:0.75625in" /> 

The Internal Rate of Return (IRR) is a financial metric that calculates the expected rate of return of an investment over its lifetime. It represents the discount rate at which the net present value (NPV) of the investment is equal to 0. In other words, it is the rate at which the cash inflows from the investment are equal to the cash outflows. 

Hotels can use the IRR in capital budgeting decisions by evaluating the profitability of potential investment opportunities. For example, a hotel may be considering a renovation or expansion project and needs to determine whether the project is financially viable. To use the IRR method, the hotel would first estimate the cash inflows and outflows associated with the project over its expected lifetime. 

Then, the hotel would calculate the IRR by finding the discount rate that makes the NPV of the investment equal to zero. If the IRR is greater than the required rate of return or the cost of capital, the investment is considered financially viable. The hotel can then compare the IRR of different investment opportunities to determine which project offers the highest potential return. 

<img src="media/image7.png" style="width:6.26667in;height:1.89167in" /> 

The Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) is the minimum rate of return that a hotel should expect from an investment to meet the expectations of its investors and maintain or increase the value of the company. The WACC is used as the discount rate for evaluating the present value of cash flows. 

In relation to the IRR, the hotel must compare the IRR to the WACC. If the IRR is greater than the WACC, the investment is considered financially viable because the hotel will earn more from the investment than the minimum required return. On the other hand, if the IRR is less than the WACC, the investment is not financially viable because the hotel will not earn enough from the investment to meet its minimum required return. 

Overall, the IRR is a valuable tool for hotels to evaluate the profitability of potential investment opportunities, and by comparing the IRR to the WACC, hotels can make informed capital budgeting decisions and minimize their financial risk.

**Core competence of an organization:**

A core competence of an organization refers to a unique strength or strategic advantage that sets the organization apart from its competitors. It is a combination of skills, knowledge, technology, and resources that allow the organization to provide value to its customers and achieve its strategic goals.

For example, Apple's core competence is its design and innovation capabilities. This core competence has allowed Apple to create products that are not only aesthetically pleasing but also highly functional and user-friendly, setting it apart from its competitors.

**Competence of an employee:**

A competence of an employee refers to an individual's skills, knowledge, and abilities that enable them to perform their job effectively. It is a combination of education, training, experience, and personal qualities that allow the employee to contribute to the success of the organization.

For example, a software developer's competence may include programming languages, problem-solving skills, project management, and teamwork. These competencies enable the developer to contribute to the development of software products, ensuring that they meet the company's quality standards and customer needs.

In summary, a core competence is a strategic advantage of the organization, while an employee's competence is their individual skills and abilities that enable them to perform their job effectively. Both are important for the success of the organization, as core competencies provide a competitive advantage, and employee competencies contribute to the organization's productivity and efficiency.

**Question 7:** *Explain why an external analysis is also important for HR strategy, how did you do this external analysis for HR and what were your conclusions?  *

- Understanding the external environment: An external analysis helps HR professionals to understand the external environment in which the organization operates. In summary, an external analysis is important for HR strategy because it helps HR professionals to understand the external environment, identify competition, predict workforce trends, and identify opportunities and threats. By taking these factors into consideration, HR professionals can develop effective HR strategies that support the organization's overall goals and objectives.

**Question 1:** *What are the 2 major schools of thoughts to define competitive advantage?*

A competitive advantage must be difficult, if not impossible, to duplicate…..

- Access to (natural) resources that are restricted from competitors

- Highly skilled labor

- Unique geographic location

- Access to new (proprietary) technology

- Ability to manufacture products at the lowest costs

- Brand image recognition

The two major schools of thought to define competitive advantage are:

1.  Competitive positioning: This school of thought emphasizes the external factors that contribute to a firm's competitive advantage. According to the IO school, a firm can gain a competitive advantage by developing a unique and valuable product or service that is difficult for competitors to imitate. This can be achieved through factors such as economies of scale, access to resources, and technological advancements.

    1.  Five forces

    2.  Generic competitive strategies

2.  The Resource-Based View (RBV) school: This school of thought emphasizes the internal factors that contribute to a firm's competitive advantage. According to the RBV school, a firm can gain a competitive advantage by possessing a unique and valuable set of resources and capabilities that are difficult for competitors to imitate. This can include factors such as human capital, brand reputation, and intellectual property.

While both schools of thought are important in understanding competitive advantage, they offer different perspectives on how firms can gain and sustain a competitive advantage in the market. The IO school focuses on external factors that are beyond the control of the firm, while the RBV school emphasizes the importance of internal resources and capabilities that can be developed and nurtured over time.

<img src="media/image10.png" style="width:6.30972in;height:3.54653in" />**Question 10:** *Porters Five Forces Framework model (lecture 1-week 4) describes the external environment and degree of competition. Define the five forces and apply a minimum of 2 of these forces to your case hotel.*

- **The threat of new entrants**: This refers to the degree of ease with which new competitors can enter an industry. If barriers to entry are low, new entrants can easily join the market, increasing competition and reducing profitability. Barriers to entry can include things like economies of scale, brand recognition, patents, and government regulations.

- **The bargaining power of suppliers**: This refers to the degree of power that suppliers have over the industry. If suppliers have a lot of power, they can raise prices or reduce the quality of their products, hurting the industry's profitability. The bargaining power of suppliers can be influenced by factors such as the number of suppliers, the availability of substitute products, and the importance of the supplier's product to the industry.

- **The bargaining power of buyers**: This refers to the degree of power that buyers have over the industry. If buyers have a lot of power, they can demand lower prices or better quality products, reducing the industry's profitability. The bargaining power of buyers can be influenced by factors such as the number of buyers, the importance of the industry's product to the buyer, and the cost of switching to alternative products.

- **The threat of substitute products:** This refers to the degree of threat posed by substitute products. If there are many substitute products available, the industry's profitability can be reduced as customers switch to cheaper or better alternatives. The threat of substitute products can be influenced by factors such as the availability of substitute products, their cost, and their quality.

- **The intensity of competitive rivalry**: This refers to the degree of competition within the industry. If competition is intense, profitability can be reduced as companies engage in price wars or invest heavily in marketing and advertising to gain market share. The intensity of competitive rivalry can be influenced by factors such as the number of competitors, their size and market share, and the level of differentiation between their products.

Porter (1985) developed a framework for analysing the nature and extent of competition within an industry. He argued that there are five competitive forces which determine the degree of competition within an industry. Understanding the nature and strength of each of the five forces within an industry assists managers in developing the competitive strategy of their organization. The five forces are:  

- the threat of new entrants to the industry;  

- the threat of substitute products;  

- the power of buyers or customers;  

- the power of suppliers (to businesses in the industry); and  

<!-- -->

- rivalry among businesses in the industry.  

By determining the relative ‘power’ of each of these forces, an organization can identify how to position itself to take advantage of opportunities and overcome or circumvent threats. The strategy of an organization may then be designed to exploit the competitive forces at work within an industry. 

<u>Threat of new entrants to the industry:</u> 

In the hotel industry the capital cost of entry is high, a building, furniture and equipment are needed, and these are expensive. Furthermore, in Germany there is an increasing brand loyalty. This means that unless the new hotel is from an existing brand, they have a lower competing power. So, all in all the barriers to entry are relatively high. Whenever they have entered however, they have an easy access to distribution channels and other hotels do not have a lot of power to resist new entries. Therefore, the potential of new entrants into the industry is medium. 

<u>Threat of substitute products:</u> 

For example, Airbnb, which have many places available in Wiesbaden in the form of rentable guesthouses or rooms. As a consequence of the covid pandemic, online meeting platforms have become a remarkable substitute for physical meetings. The availability of platforms like Teams or Zoom, that companies can make use of, threatens the marketability of conference spaces and possible overnight stays for the company's employees. 

Since there are little to no switching costs (especially as the online meetings require less costs) the barrier to switch into one of the substitute products is very low. However, there is a high level of perceived product differentiation, as an online meeting is very different then hosting a meeting in a meeting room in an hotel. As Airbnb does not offer conference spaces, the platform is less likely to be a threat for business guests. However, Airbnb does form a threat for leisure guests as they offer rentable houses and rooms for potentially less money.

**Question 2:** *What are the two common errors in producing a SWOT? Show how you avoided making these errors in the analysis of your case-hotel using an example*

There are two common errors that can occur when producing a SWOT analysis:

1.  **Confusing internal factors with external factors:** A SWOT analysis should include both internal factors (strengths and weaknesses) and external factors (opportunities and threats). However, one common error is to mistakenly include external factors in the internal analysis or vice versa. For example, listing a competitor's strengths as an internal factor would be incorrect. This error can lead to an inaccurate assessment of the company's position and can result in ineffective strategies.

2.  **Focusing too much on the present and neglecting the future:** Another common error in producing a SWOT analysis is to focus too heavily on the present and neglect the future. While it is important to analyze the current state of the company and its industry, it is equally important to consider future trends and potential changes in the market. Ignoring potential threats or opportunities that may arise in the future can lead to a lack of preparedness and missed opportunities. Therefore, it is important to take a forward-looking approach when conducting a SWOT analysis.

1.  **Corporate Level**: This is the highest level of strategic decision-making and involves decisions that affect the entire organization. At the corporate level, executives make decisions about the overall direction of the company, its mission and values, and the portfolio of businesses or products. They also determine the allocation of resources across the organization and evaluate potential mergers, acquisitions, or divestitures.

2.  **Business Level**: At the business level, strategic decisions are focused on individual business units or product lines. Business-level decisions may include developing competitive strategies, setting performance goals, and deciding on pricing and marketing strategies. Business-level strategies should be aligned with the overall corporate strategy.

3.  **Functional Level**: The functional level of strategic decision-making is the lowest level and is focused on specific functional areas, such as finance, marketing, or operations. At this level, decisions are made about how to implement the business-level strategies in the functional areas. For example, a marketing department may make decisions about how to promote a product to achieve the business-level goal of increasing market share.
