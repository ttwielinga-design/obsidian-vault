**Stereotypes:**

1.  Did you have any prejudgments when moving to the USA?

People at school really prepare you for that. They say that everyone in the USA is fake and everyone is telling you stuff and they don’t really mean it, so school was telling you stuff but on the other hand I was also like school is telling me this but it could be different, maybe they don’t know what they are talking about maybe they are just pretending to know. For me it was not really the case, however they were exaggerating everything, of course, they are Americans, they don’t mean it if they don’t like you. You meet people over there and they say they like me like they mean it. But them asking “how are you?” is a habit. They say like, in the morning, “hi how are you”, but in reality, they don’t actually want to know. So that was like a prejudgment.

**Hofstede’s dimensions:**

1.  What were the cultural differences you were already aware of when moving to Fort Lauderdale?

Everyone there is religious, they believe in God. I noticed that when I arrived there that people were really surprised that I didn’t believe in God. For them it was really hard to believe that you don’t believe in anything. I would say things like, “I believe in myself, this is not the way I was raised”. I would show people on google that like 50 or 60% of the Netherlands is not religious. This was something that I knew but I didn’t rely that it was so severe.

And then what I said about them being so extra, that is also something that I knew. And it is the case that over there the food and the drinks, they exaggerate everything. An example is a colleague of mine told me that she had the worst day ever and it turned out that there was not a sale at target that she expected would be there. I was like what when you say that does that not mean that someone like died.

2.  Were there many different cultures/ethnicities in Fort Lauderdale?

    1.  Which ones were the most prominent?

Yes, South-America has a large influence their, people from Peru and Cuba, they speak Spanish. In housekeeping we had mostly ladies with a black skin color. They were talking in French, there is a name for the dialect that they were speaking, but I don’t remember it.

3.  Do you think the hotel focused on creating a diverse work environment?

    1.  How did you experience this?

Yes, definitely, well there were a lot of interns there, from the Netherlands, Germany, Asia, South America. So, there were a lot of different people with a lot of different cultures and languages. We really used that, so if there was a guest at the front desk who only spoke Spanish, we would get like a bell boy to translate to make sure all the guests were understood properly.

2.  When looking at the main departments, could you sketch us a brief overview of how the teams looked regarding cultures/ethnicities?

Well at the front desk we had a lot of Americans, as well as Bram, and I. In housekeeping we had a lot of ladies with a dark skin-color. In reservations, only Americans. In F&B were a lot of interns from all over the place, that was for sure the most diverse.

Not that much, mostly men, in front office. And in housekeeping mostly women. Looking at managers it was really diverse. The front desk manager was a guy but the F&B manager was a woman, the reservation manager was also a woman.

5.  *What do you think about the distance of power within the Pelican Grand?*

    1.  *Were the managers easy to reach if you had a complaint or suggestion?*

The managers were very easy to reach, I mostly worked with managers, and my housekeeping manager was so nice to all the ladies, still everyone knew each other, they were friends. We all had lunch together. Everyone was like the same. Some people would feel better but it is just like their personality.

6.  *What did you think of the Long- versus Short-term orientation?*

I think we are more on the long-term here in the Netherlands, and there it was more on the short-term. For example, I was working in reservations and I got a call for a wedding that was a year and a half from the moment, and my manager was like no, no, not going to do that that is to far away. We maximum plan a wedding one year. I was like: “that is weird because it is a wedding”.

So, with traditions it is the other way around. They have a lot of holidays, like Halloween, and they do a lot of stuff for that. And here I think we have less traditions. We do but, they change, like Sinterklaas. In the USA I feel like they change that less.

6.  *Generally speaking, do you think the USA is individualistic than the Netherlands?*

    1.  *And why do you think that is?*

Yes, 100%, in a company they are more focused on themselves. Their benchmarks, income, status. If at the front desk we were able to upsell a room or walk-ins etc. we were able to keep 10% of that revenue. It was really a completion; people really were running towards the front desk when people were coming in. I however understand because it was big amounts in one month you can make 3000\$ extra. So, I get it but still.

**Low- versus High-context:**

1.  *Did you ever give feedback to your employees at your work or did you ever receive feedback?*

    1.  *What is the difference when giving/receiving feedback when comparing it to the Netherlands?*

I think that when we give feedback, we are very direct, we say what we want to say. And when they tell you something they are always giving you complements and being really nice but you are not sure if they mean it (sugar coating). They tell you something but they are also being very nice so what do you want from me. I did not get a lot of negative feedback because I was doing my job. Front desk was hard sometimes but they respect that I was trying my best and I was doing a good job. Positive feedback they give it a lot you don’t know if they mean it any more.

2.  *How did the management structure look like at the Pelican Grand hotel?*

Well, we had a GM but she was sick so we had a taskforce manager, so that was a bit vague, and every department had their own manager, a weekdays manager and a weekend manager. Then you had the staff who worked their full time and then you had the interns. For example, in F&B it was different because there was only one manager and pretty much everyone was an intern.

*Was it ever unclearing to you who you should listen to?*

Sometimes, especially with the taskforce manager because, like I said I was only there for 6 months and I saw 3 so I did not really know them. So, if I had a problem, I would always go to the front desk manager. Sometimes he was like we should ask the taskforce manager. But then I just didn’t really know what to do because I didn’t really know him. But then the front desk manager solved the problem.

3.  *When making decisions who made them and why?*

So, we were part of a chain, the Noble House, it was like 10 hotels. So, the person in charge there makes the desertions like the big ones. Then for the departments the department managers made the desertions. The task force manager was kind of in-between, because we had a general manager but she was sick so she was not really there. We as intern only had influence on small decisions, maybe employees do, but I was only there for 6 months so I didn’t understand the bigger picture.

4.  *How does having a disagreement with a coworker play out?*

    1.  *Is it confrontational? \[head on\]*

It is not like I didn’t have confrontations but I didn’t really have head on confrontations with employees. There was one person who I didn’t like I discussed that with my colleagues but not like directly with her. I thought she was really mean. She thought she had much more authority because she had been working there for a while but we both knew that we dint like each other but there was no confrontation or anything.

If it was in the Netherlands this could have been different. But me not knowing her well also played a factor in it.
