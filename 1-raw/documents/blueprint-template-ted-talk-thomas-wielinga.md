Introduction Ted Talk

Climate change is a global issue that is affecting communities, lives, businesses and economies all over the world. The World Economic Forum has now ranked climate action failure as the top global risk, demonstrating the urgent need for governments, businesses and people to act now.

Global emissions of carbon dioxide have increased by almost 50 per cent since 1990. This is having a notable impact on our climate. Every decade since the 1960s has been warmer than the one before, and we are seeing extreme weather events damaging lives and livelihoods around the world, particularly the poorest and most vulnerable people are being hurt by this.

Hi, I’m Thomas Wielinga, these days a proud student of the Hotel Management School Maastricht. And about 15 years ago I lived in California.

Here you see me at 9 years old making a pledge at the Grand Canyon to preserve and protect nature and wildlife. Today I still honor that pledge which is why I want to draw your attention to the sustainable development goals, climate action and how nations and governments need to do their part in fighting against the climate change

**So how are we going to integrate climate change measures into national policies, strategies, and planning?**

First of all we need to look at the facts. According to the United Nations in order to limit global warming of +1.5C global emissions must drop by 45% between 2010 and 2030, and reach net zero around 2050.

The Paris Agreement had 3 main goals:

1.  Limit the average global temperature to 2C

2.  Enhance resilience and adaptation to climate impacts certain to occur

3.  Align financial flows to these objectives

However in reality the Paris Agreement covers only 33% of the CO2 reductions necessary to keep the global warming to +2C. This means that in order for the +2C to be achieved we need to reduce the CO2 output globally by 3 times more than the Paris Agreement states.

At this moment in time only 85 counties have national strategies inline with the Sendai frame work. So what is the Sendai frame work? Maybe you have never heard of it or you have seen it come by on the news once or twice but no one ever explained it to you.

Well the Framework pretty much aims to achieve a substantial reduction of 4 things:

1.  Global disaster mortality

2.  Number of effected people

3.  Economic loss in relation to GDP

4.  Damage to critical infrastructure and service disruption

The framework also wants to substantially increase 3 things:

1.  Number of countries with national and local disaster risk reduction

2.  International cooperation to developing countries

3.  Availability and accessibility to early warning systems and Disaster risk reduction information

The framework aims to address this issue through setting goals just like the Paris Agreement set goals to lower the temperature and stop natural disasters from happening.

To put the mind at ease of all the people that say that the Paris Agreement and the Sandai framework will cost jobs, well no! It is estimated that this change to green energy will create around 18 million jobs by 2030.

So what can we do?

**Should we stay at home in order to reduce our emissions?**

It is not the answer. We’ve just witnessed a global experiment in staying at home.

Lockdowns around the world, coupled with economic downturns, meant that people reduced their carbon emissions. They took less transports, bought less products and didn’t use commercial spaces like office buildings. At the peak of lockdowns in April 2020, we were releasing 19% less CO2 than the previous year. However, the 2020 average reduction in CO2 emissions was only 7% less.

Asking average people to solve rapid climate change breaks down when we look at the scale of the problem. Personal contributions toward reducing greenhouse gas emissions are nice, but they are dwarfed by the systemic reality of global emissions. The concept of your personal carbon footprint was popularized by the oil producer BP in a 2005 ad campaign. Arguably one of the most effective and sinister pieces of propaganda that still seriously distracts all of us from the reality of the situation.

The reality being that we as individuals can not contribute all that much when it comes to reducing carbon emictions. If as I as an individual would not emit any carbon for the rest of my life I would only save around 1 second worth of emissions from the global energy sector.

So is it all doom and gloom?

I don’t think so. The world has produced more electricity from clean energy — solar, wind, hydro, and nuclear — than from coal in the past two years. So the energy revolution is taking hold. We see that climate tech investments are smashing all previous records. This means that people are taking notice and they tide is changing. I recently read an article in the “Future Crunch”, an online magazine for intelligent and optimistic thinking about the future. I paraphrase:

*There’s no way of judging whether we’re living through Collapse or Renewal. Future generations will decide that for us. The only thing that matters is the part we play as individuals and the leaders and the parties we vote for.*

**So** that is my message for you today. Look at your government, the policies and regulations they implement and question them if these are the right ones. Do your own research and find a party that does lay a priority on reducing carbon dioxide. If you don’t agree with the policies don’t vote for them. Look at your employer and question what they can do different. Come with ideas. Because we live in a world where our vote and voice CAN change our world for the present and the future.

I’m Thomas Wielinga and I again make a pledge that that I will vote for greener parties and that I will ask more for greener options.
