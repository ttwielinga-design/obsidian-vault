**Summary: Understanding the Dynamics of Intrinsic and Extrinsic Motivation in Work Performance**

Overview:

The chapter explores the intricate relationship between intrinsic and extrinsic motivations and their impact on work performance. It delves into the theoretical frameworks, empirical evidence, advantages, disadvantages, and the art of balancing these motivations in different work scenarios.

Extrinsic Motivation's Initial Perceived Effectiveness:

Historically, it was widely believed that extrinsic motivators, such as monetary rewards, positively influenced performance. Psychological and managerial literature supported the idea that rewarding a particular action increased the likelihood of its future occurrence, considering instrumental and classical conditioning mechanisms.

The Crowding Out Effect:

Despite the initial belief in the effectiveness of extrinsic rewards, a phenomenon called the "crowding out effect" emerged. This effect indicates that under certain conditions, external rewards can diminish intrinsic motivation, especially when tasks are considered interesting and rewards are perceived as controlling. This undermines the initial enthusiasm or intrinsic motivation for a task.

Theoretical Background:

The crowding out effect is rooted in cognitive evaluation theory (Cognitive evaluation theory is a theory in psychology that is designed to explain the effects of external consequences on internal motivation.) and psychological contract theory (unwritten set of expections and obligation between empolyees). It involves a shift in the locus of control (Internal Locus of Control: This refers to an individual's belief that they have control over their actions and outcomes. AND External Locus of Control: This occurs when individuals believe that external factors, such as rewards, commands, or other people, control their actions or outcomes) from internal to external factors when external interventions are perceived as restrictive. Depending on how rewards are perceived (controlling vs. informative), intrinsic motivation can either be undermined (crowded out) or reinforced (crowded in).

*Empirical Evidence:*

*Numerous laboratory and field studies support the existence of the crowding out effect. Meta-analyses and real-life cases like blood donations, nuclear waste acceptance surveys, and studies on managerial control and family businesses provide evidence of how extrinsic rewards can negatively impact intrinsic motivation and performance.*

Importance of Intrinsic Motivation:

Intrinsic motivation is crucial, especially in tasks requiring creativity, handling multiple ambiguous goals, and transferring tacit knowledge. It aids in innovation, prevents task myopia, and encourages knowledge sharing.

Challenges and Considerations:

However, intrinsic motivation is not without its challenges. It can be difficult to change, might lead to undesirable behaviors, and might not always align with organizational goals.

Achieving the Right Balance:

A balance between intrinsic and extrinsic motivations is necessary. Factors like personal relationships, participation in goal-setting, and interest in tasks boost intrinsic motivation, while factors like reward contingency, commands, and perceived justice violations can undermine it.

Conclusion:

The chapter concludes that while monetary rewards are important, they should be integrated with intrinsic motivators and feedback discussions. Relying solely on monetary incentives oversimplifies the complexity of human motivation in organizations, especially in knowledge-based roles, and may lead to decreased performance.
