**W2. Video Lecture 2 MAC**

**Retained earnings**: reflect earnings generated but not distributed as dividends. Changes in this account during the year are commonly shown on a statement of owners’ equity. (Retained earnings link the P&L to the balance sheet.)

- **Horizontal analysis**: looks at the performance of a hotel across more than one year for each category

- **Vertical analysis**: looks at the performance of a hotel in all categories as a percentage of total revenues, for example, for a period of one year

- **Ratio analysis**: used when comparing figures to each other and across financial statements.

Different types of ratios

- **Liquidity** shows the ability to meet short-term obligations

- **Solvency** measures the extent to which the firm has been financed by debt and is able to meet all financial obligations

- **Activity** ratios reflect management’s ability to use the property’s assets efficiently

- **Profitability** shows management’s overall effectiveness as measured by returns

- **Operating** ratios assist in the assessment of operations

Ratios provide several users with important information regarding the performance of the hotel and its ratios

- Creditors use **liquidity** and **solvency** ratios to assess the riskiness of future transactions and loans

- Managers use **activity** and **operating** ratios to evaluate the results of their operations and their ability to achieve goals

- Investors highlight **profitability** ratios to evaluate the performance of a hospitality operation

(Schmidgall, Chapter 3 and 5)

**Income** flows result from operations generating **revenues** and collecting **expenses.** (Income statement)

**Cashflows** result from the **receipt** and the **payment** of **cash.** (Cashflow statement)

**Cash management**: the management of a hospitality operation’s cash balances, cash flow, and short-term investments in securities.

<img src="media/image1.png" style="width:5.27526in;height:3.225in" alt="Cash converstion cycle " />The **cash conversion cycle** (**CCC**) is a metric that expresses the length of time (in days) that it takes for a company to convert its investments in inventory and other resources into cash flows from sales.

The inventory holding period is the time difference between the moment of purchasing inventory and selling it to the guest

The holding period is calculated as follows:

- **Inventory** **turnover** = Cost of food sold / **average** **inventory**

- **Inventory** **holding** = 365 / **inventory** **turnover**

The accounts collection period is the time difference between the moment of selling the goods to the guest and the receipt of the accompanying cash

The collection period is calculated as follows:

- **Accounts receivable turnover** = **Revenue** /**average accounts receivable**

- **Collection period** = 365 / **accounts receivable turnover**

The outcome shows how many days you will have to wait for your cash receipts

The payables deferral period is the time difference between the moment of purchasing inventory and paying for that inventory in cash

The deferral period is calculated as follows:

- **Accounts** **payable** **turnover** = Cost of food sold / **average** **accounts** **payable**

- **Deferral** **period** = 365 / **accounts** **payable** **turnover**

The outcome shows how many days you wait until you pay your bills

**Operating cycle = Inventory holding + Collection period**

**CCC = Operating cycle – Deferral period**

(Schmidgall, Chapter 4 and 11)

**W7. Video Lecture 7 MAC**

The **USALI** (Uniform System of Accounts for the lodging industry) is used as a standard for the Profit & Loss-account within the hotel industry. **USALI** makes it easier to identify which costs are relatively high or low compared to the competition and what the effect of these costs is on profit. The **USALI** combined with **HOSTA** **(Hotel Statistics)** makes it possible to perform a benchmark quickly and easily. This document explains how a hotel’s financial performance can be compared to the industry averages.

It is important to note that comparison with **HOSTA** is only possible for:

1.  the departmental incomes/profit (department managers are held responsible for this);

2.  the GOI (Gross operating Income);

3.  the GOP (Gross Operating profit).

The GM is responsible for the GOP, because the fixed charges can’t be influenced by the GM.

**no conclusions** can be drawn about the **cost of sales**, **payroll** & **related expenses** and other **costs of F&B**, expressed as a percentage of the total revenues, **because** these costs must be expressed as a **percentage** of the **revenues** of **F&B**. It goes without saying that the same applies to the rooms department and the other profit centers.

**The sales mix** show how revenues of the profit centers are related to total revenues.

**W8. Video Lecture 8 MAC**

**STR** (Smith Travel Research) creates different reports:

- STAR: ADR, Occupancy, REVPAR

- **HOST**: P&L benchmark

- Pipeline: future developments

- And many more

The **HOST** report provides averages for the full P&L and is tailor made to an individual hotel’s needs (based on data availability). 3 types of benchmarks:

1.  Ratio to sales (comparable to HOSTA)

2.  Amount per available room (yearly)

3.  Amount per occupied room night (daily)

The differences between the **HOSTA** and **STR** are:

- **Scale**: **HOSTA** is aimed at the BENELUX market, **STR** operates globally

- **Composition**: **HOSTA** is based on a sample of 400 hotels, composition changes every year. The **STR** database contains 1000’s of hotels, composition is stable

- **Benchmark**: **HOSTA** provides a general overview (based on either size, location, stars, price), STR offers a tailor-made benchmark

The **Ratio to Sales** benchmark enables you to compare your own expense percentages to the averages from the industry (STR)

The other **two benchmarks** (mount Per Available Room & Amount Per Occupied Room Night) provide you with more information about the amount in euros per available room/occupied room nights

All three benchmarks offer you a perspective to your own performance
