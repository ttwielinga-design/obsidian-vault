**Tutorial 1.2: Using Outside Sources**

Academic writing requires students to support ideas and claims with reliable facts and details from published sources. There are three ways you can use outside information in your own writing:

1.  You can summarize it – make it shorter – include only the main ideas

2.  You can paraphrase it – put in your own words

3.  You can quote it – use “quotation marks” and repeat words from the text

**Paraphrasing vs. Summarizing**

When you paraphrase, you rewrite information from an outside source in your own words without changing the meaning. When you paraphrase, nearly all the information is included from the original source, whereas a summary is much shorter than the original text and does not include all the same information. **Even after you have put an idea into your own words, you must always cite the original source of these ideas to avoid plagiarism.**

**Paraphrasing Techniques**

**To paraphrase a text, a student is required not only to substitute important words in the sentence, but also to change the order of ideas or sentence structure.** Because this is difficult, many students are tempted to simply quote the author. Although a short quotations using very specific vocabulary is acceptable, students should aim to minimize their use of quotations and paraphrase/summarize texts as often as possible. Make sure you have not changed the meaning of the original text or given any wrong information. **Instead of seeking alternative vocabulary, you should look for an alternative syntax (word choice/phrasing) and sequence (word order).** You should consider changing the original verbs into nouns or adjectives, the original nouns into verbs and so on. You can also try starting with the middle or end of the original idea and revising the order of ideas.

**Summarizing**

You will often need to write a summary for an academic paper. The following steps should be considered when writing a summary.

1)  <span class="mark">Read through the whole passage to get an overall view</span>.

2)  Read it through again, <span class="mark">underlining each important point</span>. These will all need to be included in the summary.

3)  Create a mind-map or <span class="mark">outline of the each important point</span>.

4)  <span class="mark">Write your summar</span>y of the text <span class="mark">using your notes</span> from the mind-map/outline, omitting all unnecessary matter.

5)  <span class="mark">Compare</span> the summary with the original, adding anything of importance that had been left out. (Note: the summary should be much shorter in length than the original text)

6)  <span class="mark">Re-read carefully</span> to see if the summary flows smoothly, logically, and is grammatically correct.

**Additional tips for writing a summary:**

1)  Put ideas in your own words and your own sentence structure, or clearly show when using a “direct quotation” (include Author, Year and page number of source).

2)  A summary is much shorter than a paraphrase. Include only the most important points and main ideas, leaving out most of the details.

3)  Do not change the meaning of the original text

4)  Give credit to the source of your information (Author, year) or any sources they used (X, Year, as cited in Author, Year).

**Plagiarism**

Academic writing involves researching and reporting on information written by established experts in a field. You must support your ideas and opinions with facts, statistics and quotations from reliable sources. In order to give credit to where these facts and supporting ideas came from, we must always cite the sources of our information.

It is important to learn how to use information from outside sources without committing plagiarism. Plagiarism is using someone else’s words or ideas as if they were your own. Plagiarism can occur intentionally or unintentionally (as a result of poor note-taking). In either case, it is a serious offense. Students who plagiarize generally fail a class and may be expelled from school. When you use information from an outside source without properly acknowledging that source, you are guilty of plagiarism (Oshima & Hogue, 2006).

**Examples of plagiarism**

These examples are taken from: Clines, R. H. & Cobb, E. R. (2012). *Research Writing Simplified*. USA: Pearson.

Original: “World problems such as poverty, pollution, war, and hunger are inherent in the current system of world order based on nation-states and economic competition. They can be solved if people know and understand one another on a global, grass-roots basis. By developing people-to-people linkages irrespective of national borders, we can start to ameliorate global tensions and inequities.” (Ellis, 2011, p.23)

**Type 1: Word-for-word plagiarism**

Word-for-word plagiarism is the most explicit form of plagiarism. Text is copied verbatim. Even if the source is acknowledged, quotation marks must be used around passages copied word for word. In this form of plagiarism, the writer gives the reader the impression that this text is paraphrased when it is actually a direct quote. All text copied directly from another source must be put in quotation marks and given a reference.

*Example: Ellis (2011) asserts that world problems such as poverty, pollution, war, and hunger are inherent in the current system of world order based on nation-states and economic competition (p. 23).*

**Type 2: Patchwork plagiarism**

Patchwork plagiarism occurs when the writer reorders the information but continues to use much of the exact wording. Again, this implies a paraphrase when it is not.

*Example: Global tensions and inequalities can be solved if people begin to help one another on a grass-roots basis, moving beyond the current world order of economic competition (Ellis, 2011).*

**Type 3: Paraphrase without documentation**

Paraphrases without documentation are also a form of plagiarism. When a writer paraphrases but does not include a reference (in-text or in parentheses), the reader believes that these ideas are original

*Example: Economic competition is at the basis of many of the world’s problems. Only by seeing ourselves as a single human family without the separation of national boundaries can world tensions begin to be eased.*

**Type 4: Paraphrase with incomplete documentation**

Another similar form of plagiarism is a paraphrase with incomplete documentation (references). The example below is identical to the one above except for the fact that a parenthetical reference has been added after the first sentence. Unfortunately, this is still plagiarism as the second sentence which is paraphrased from the original does not have a reference. <u>Paraphrased text that is more than one sentence must be framed by a reference that clearly marks the beginning and end of borrowed material</u>.

*Example: Economic competition is at the basis of many of the world’s problems (Ellis, 2011). Only by seeing ourselves as a single human family without the separation of national boundaries can world tensions begin to be eased.*

**Type 5: Misrepresentation of original source**

Plagiarism due to misrepresentation occurs when the content of text presented with a reference is not in any way similar to the content of the original text. This results from careless note-taking or from an attempt to make claims seem credible.

*Example: Ellis (2011) argues that world problems are caused by overpopulation and that the only possible solution is an enforced tax on families who have more than one child.*
