7.  Capital budgeting is used to plan capital investments. Elaborate on the difference between CAPEX and Repair & Maintenance. Which stakeholder(s) prefer(s) CAPEX over R&M? (Finance class 4)

CAPEX refers to the funds used to acquire or upgrade long-term assets, such as new equipment, buildings, or technology. These expenditures are usually significant, require a large initial investment, and generate benefits over an extended period (min. 1 year).

On the other hand, R&M expenses are the costs incurred to maintain and repair existing assets. These expenses are usually small, frequent, and ongoing, and they do not increase the asset's value or extend its useful life.

Which stakeholders prefer what?

- For operators they get incentives for revenues and GOP. Any CAPEX effecting these two will be welcomed. Since CAPEX does not affect the GOP.

- For brands, the incentive is to increase revenues through franchise fees, and therefore, CAPEX may be required to attract and retain customers. However, brands are not responsible for paying the CAPEX bill. Instead, the owners bear the cost of the CAPEX investment, and the brands benefit from increased revenues.

- For owners, minimizing cash outflows and maximizing cash inflows are the primary concerns when considering CAPEX and R&M expenses. While CAPEX investments may be necessary to maintain or increase revenues and profitability, owners will likely try to minimize the costs of these investments. Similarly, R&M expenses that prevent major repairs and reduce downtime can help minimize costs and maximize cash inflows.

8.  Which methods can your case hotel use to make capital budgeting decisions? Elaborate on every method.

There are 3 main methods (kijk naar finance class 5)

- Discounted Cashflow (DCF) / Net Present Value (NPV): DCF is a method of calculating the present value of future cash flows of an investment after accounting for the time value of money, using a discount rate. The NPV is the difference between the present value of the cash inflows and outflows of an investment. A positive NPV indicates that the investment will generate a return greater than the required rate of return, and therefore, the investment is considered financially viable. This method takes into account the time value of money and is useful for evaluating long-term investments.

- Payback period: The payback period is the length of time it takes for an investment to generate enough cash flows to recover the initial investment. This method is used to evaluate the time it takes to recover the investment and the risk involved in the investment. Projects with shorter payback periods are generally preferred because they recover the initial investment more quickly, providing the business with more financial flexibility.

- Accounting Rate of Return: The accounting rate of return (ARR) is the ratio of the average annual profit generated by an investment to the initial investment cost. This method is used to evaluate the profitability of an investment over a specific period. The ARR is expressed as a percentage and provides an estimate of the average annual return on the investment. The ARR method does not account for the time value of money and is generally not considered as accurate as the NPV method. It is useful when evaluating the expected profitability of a project over a short time frame.

- The Internal Rate of Return (IRR) is a capital budgeting method that calculates the discount rate that makes the net present value (NPV) of an investment equal to zero. In other words, it is the interest rate at which the present value of future cash inflows from an investment equals the initial cash outlay.

9.  Explain the Internal Rate of Return and elaborate how the case hotel can use the IRR in capital budgeting decisions. Link the IRR to the WACC.

The Internal Rate of Return (IRR) is a financial metric that calculates the expected rate of return of an investment over its lifetime. It represents the discount rate at which the net present value (NPV) of the investment is equal to 0. In other words, it is the rate at which the cash inflows from the investment are equal to the cash outflows.

Hotels can use the IRR in capital budgeting decisions by evaluating the profitability of potential investment opportunities. For example, a hotel may be considering a renovation or expansion project, and needs to determine whether the project is financially viable. To use the IRR method, the hotel would first estimate the cash inflows and outflows associated with the project over its expected lifetime.

Then, the hotel would calculate the IRR by finding the discount rate that makes the NPV of the investment equal to zero. If the IRR is greater than the required rate of return or the cost of capital, the investment is considered financially viable. The hotel can then compare the IRR of different investment opportunities to determine which project offers the highest potential return.

The Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) is the minimum rate of return that a hotel should expect from an investment to meet the expectations of its investors and maintain or increase the value of the company. The WACC is used as the discount rate for evaluating the present value of cash flows.

In relation to the IRR, the hotel must compare the IRR to the WACC. If the IRR is greater than the WACC, the investment is considered financially viable because the hotel will earn more from the investment than the minimum required return. On the other hand, if the IRR is less than the WACC, the investment is not financially viable because the hotel will not earn enough from the investment to meet its minimum required return.

Overall, the IRR is a valuable tool for hotels to evaluate the profitability of potential investment opportunities, and by comparing the IRR to the WACC, hotels can make informed capital budgeting decisions and minimize their financial risk.
