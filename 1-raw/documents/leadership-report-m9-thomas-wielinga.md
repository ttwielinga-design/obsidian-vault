**Competing value framework**

When looking at my MSAI grid you can see that I have already mastered managing competitiveness, and energizing employees. Furthermore, you can see that I still need to develop my coordination and managing the control systems. From the literature that we have read I know that this means that I am more of a traditional leader. “Emphasizing the importance of having a strategy that creates competitive advantage and aligns activities in support of the organizations’ vision” (Quinn et al., 2015).

One of the paradoxes that was mentioned in the book was: “that people want a leader to provide a clear vision for the organization, people are unwilling to embrace a vision unless they see the vision as their own” (Quinn et al., 2015). I noticed this to be true when I was working as a bar manager during my internship. My colleagues found it extremely difficult to see the vision that I had for the bar. Only when they became convinced of the idea that a new bar menu was indeed the way to go, even though it had not been updated in more than five years, they wanted to help me.

Now regarding my less developed managerial behavior, the part in the Master Manager book about documentation really spoke to me. I realized that during my internship and in general whenever I am working or studying, I do not like to document things or write them down. SOPs are also something that I struggled to create and adhere to. I think the reason for this is because I believe that everyone should be able to give their own little spin on each and every activity. I do agree with one of the paradoxes stated in the Master Manager book though. I saw first hand how that if to much emphasis is put on only getting the SOP’s right all the broader things that have to be done will not be done correctly. Or like they state in the book: “An overemphasis on control also can result in getting the details right but failing to accomplish broader objectives” (Quinn et al., 2015).

When looking at what I learned about myself during the MFD it is interesting to see that the qualities that I had in my Ikigai about what I am good at are very similar to the two management skills that I excel in the most, energizing employees, and managing competitiveness. In my Ikigai I said that I am good at raising morale and analysis.

The fact that I am a more traditional leader also came back during the talent part of MFD. A compliment that I got from one of my teammates was that I am good at setting goals and motivating others. This is again in direct correlation with two of the five competencies of the Compete quadrant.

I tend to remain loyal to a person or organization even when it goes against my values or principles, which can be harmful to me and those around me. I noticed this especially during my last job that I had. I was too loyal to the bar that I worked at and just shut a blind eye to all the things that were going wrong.

Similarly, my ambition can be a pitfall when it turns into impatience. I can become too eager to achieve my goals and may overlook important details or relationships in the process. My self-discipline can also become a pitfall when it turns into excessive rigidity. I tend to become to set in my ways and resist change or new ideas. A TSI test that I took in my first year at the hotel school stated that these two pitfalls can come forward during times of stress. (TTI, 2020)

Furthermore, I have noticed that I have an allergy to disloyalty, passivity, and laissez-faire behavior in others. This means that I may have a negative response to people who lack loyalty or motivation. I need to recognize this allergy and work on not letting it hinder my ability to work effectively with others who have different strengths and weaknesses. From the activity that we did during the expert workshop about event management that Ruud Bogaert thought I learned that especially in must preform situations I tend to get this reaction the most.

The reason I got this reaction during this workshop was because we had to come up with a totally new event concept for the MECC here in Maastricht. I had someone in my group that I felt did not want to participate. He had quite a passive stance on the whole project. Looking back this is a perfect example of how my own ambition and self-discipline butted heads with the more passive and laissez-faire approach. Because of my approach I overlooked some of the details during this project and this person who was more laid back actually caught my mistakes which taught me that I need to sometimes take a step back and assess the situation again.

This can further be explained through the negative zones of Quinn. During this master class I was really focused on the result which drove my managerial role from productivity to perpetual exertion . Because of the stress that we were under I went from being productive to going into a state of ‘never ending mental effort’. Me being in this state meant that it was inevitable that I would clash with someone who was in the complete opposite state of mind and that did not feel the urgency of this project.

**Results and reflection:**

In module 9, I set three SMART goals to improve my skills and mindset, which have helped me to make significant progress. Firstly, practicing mindfulness and meditation using the Headspace app daily has reduced my stress levels. During a stressful work situation, I used the breathing exercises from the mindfulness sessions to calm myself down and finish my work in a healthy mindset.

Secondly, I committed to seeking feedback from my peers regularly to remain open to new ideas. Asking for feedback from Daphne and Sebas helped me restructure my week and focus on relevant tasks, resulting in a more relaxed and organized approach.

Lastly, reading through my assignments with a fresh set of eyes has prevented me from overlooking any details. After procrastinating and missing a small detail in a financial analysis assignment in week two, I learned to avoid procrastination and view my work with a fresh perspective.

To further develop my skills, I could focus on stress management techniques, such as mindfulness and self-awareness. Improving my organizational skills to avoid procrastination and seeking feedback from colleagues would also benefit my personal and professional growth.

SMART goals team explained

Goal 1:

There was one session that I could unfortunately not attend. This was because I had a personal appointment. My team had to do the presentation without me because of this. So, the task in front of our team was to prepare the presentation with out me. The actions that we took as a team to make sure that the presentation would still go smoothly was already in the planning. One of my own SMART goals made sure that I had already finished all my work one day before the presentation so this meant that Daphne and Sebas were able to present my part with out any trouble. Reflecting on this I think that because of the way that we make sure all members of our group have the proper knowledge to present without the other members of the group there allowed us to, even though I was not there, have a successful presentation.

 

Goal 2:

During this process we have had meetings pretty much every single week. One meeting in particular stood out to me. This was the meeting in week five where we discussed who was going to do what for the final paper. The task during this meeting was just to divide the tasks but what I found was very interesting was that very organically and with the best interest of the process in mind everyone was willing to take on whatever part that was necessary. Resulting in a proper distribution of tasks and not one being overlooked. No struggle at all. Reflecting back, I see that because we all know each other so well now it allows us to decide with ease who does what. Making sure that all bases are covered.  

 

Goal 3: 

In the beginning we were in a group with four people. The task that we had laid out was to adhere to the CoC from school and our own as well as the module book to make sure that we would all our work as proper as possible. Unfortunately, in the first two weeks one of our team members didn’t show up so we had to take action. The action we took was to sit with him in week three and ask him what he wanted to do for our group to get back into the project. Unfortunately, he did not take any initiative and failed to deliver results. This led to us putting him out of the group at the beginning of week five. The result this had on the group, how sad it may seem, was a positive one since the group morale was being brought down by this individual. Reflecting on this situation I have empathy for what this person had to go through however I feel that the lack substance of what was produced by him let us to the only proper conclusion to let him go out of the group.
