**What relevant information regarding culture, sustainability, and market environment should a hotel management school student consider when applying for an internship, in Florida?**

Choosing an internship location can be an overwhelming task, however, it is crucial to acquaint yourself with different aspects of your future destination. In this report, we will discuss the most important aspects to consider when choosing the Pelican Grand hotel in Fort Lauderdale to be your internship destination. We will discuss aspects such as elements of national culture, corporate culture, sustainability, human rights, and market environment. The most important aspect of the aforementioned topics will be discussed in detail while some aspects which are not relevant to this specific location will not be talked about. In specific we will be making a comparison between these aspects concerning the US and the Netherlands. When talking about the US some people might not recognize the massive differences that occur between states and they might believe that Florida will be much like other states they might have visited beforehand. In this report, we will discuss not just the Hotel itself but Miami-Dade County and Florida as a whole.

Regarding cultural elements such as the concept of time and verbal communication, some differences are important to mention. In regard to verbal communication people in the Netherlands are prone to be very direct and straight to the point. Whilst in the US it is customary to start conversing with the others before making requests and getting to the point. (Meyers, 2019) This is important to keep in mind for students as such a direct style in the US may be perceived as rude. Looking at time in the US vs the Netherlands we found them to be similar. In personal life in the US “It depends how, how well do you know that friend? If it is someone who you know more, than more flexibility” (Q.G.- New York). From personal experience, we can say it is similar in the Netherlands. However, when it comes to business, time tends to be more important. “My calendar is busy from 8 am to 5 pm for meetings… So it's very structured.” (Q.G.- New York). The business life of our interviewee is busy hence he deems it disrespectful if someone is late. We found the same in the Netherlands as implied by one of our interviewees: “In the Netherlands, business, I would still see it as a sign of respect for the other party.” (K.B. - New York). 

According to Hofstede's 6 cultural dimensions, the main differences in culture regarding the US and the Netherlands that one may observe are within the dimensions of masculinity and long-term orientation.

Regarding masculinity, the US scores 62 and the Netherlands 14 (Hofstede Insights 2021). The Netherlands has a highly feminine culture while the US is more masculine. Some aspects to keep in mind from this information would be that the US has a stronger separation of gender roles, and they may value work above other aspects of life. For a student of the HMSM, this could be a new situation as most Dutch families do not usually have such strong gender separation and each parent is likely as much a part of their young lives like the other in most cases. 

The second most important cultural difference is the aspect of long-term orientation. While the Netherlands is relatively long-term oriented with a score of 67, the US is rather short-term oriented with a score of 26 (Hofstede Insights 2021). From this we may conclude that in the US people may be more prone to keep traditions intact, they often assume that people stay the same and cannot change, and in business, they find the bottom line important. Meanwhile in the Netherlands people may be more flexible with traditions and may think of good and evil as relative rather than absolute. These differences in thinking might affect a future student's internship experience. When your self-reflective criterion is based on the Dutch way of longer-term thinking this might translate to some misunderstandings in the more short term oriented US. 

The remaining 4 cultural dimensions that Hofstede researched are close enough in value that we cannot draw clear conclusions of their differences. Therefore we will not discuss them in this report. 

According to the aforementioned information, the most important cultural dimensions for a student to keep in mind when applying to an internship in Florida would be the differences in masculinity and long-term orientation as being unaware of such differences may lead one to uncomfortable encounters. Taking into consideration these differences is crucial for understanding the differences in culture one may see when moving to this destination.

According to Meyer’s book: “The Culture Map” the eight key concepts that will improve your ability to navigate international business and increase success are communication, feedback, persuasion, leadership & hierarchy, decision making, trust, disagreements, and scheduling. These concepts reflect a picture of a country’s context levels. When comparing the US versus the Netherlands we found that there are four areas where they differ significantly. 

The first area is feedback, when giving negative feedback the Dutch are mostly direct, meaning that criticism may be perceived as blunt and honest by cultures who do not score lower in this category. On the contrary, when giving negative feedback an American will probably hide the negative feedback in a positive tone. Another key difference is that in the Netherlands people will often give feedback in front of peers, whereas in the US this is often done in private.

The second aspect is leadership and hierarchy, the US is split between egalitarian and hierarchical. This entails some aspects like power distance. Although in Hofstede’s research of power distance interestingly there was only a difference of 2 between the US and the Netherlands. Hall’s in-depth research of context, however, reveals that in some aspects of leadership and hierarchy they do differ. One of these aspects is needing approval to execute. In the US you usually need the permission of a superior before executing. In the Netherlands however, this is not always the case. The same goes for when you are addressing the group. In the US it is common to have a clear seating arrangement and speaking order, whereas in the Netherlands we found that this is not needed and the person with the most pressing matter will often speak first.

The third aspect is decision making which is either top-down or consensual. The Netherlands scored consensual which may mean the following: emphasis in discussion before the decision to act, everyone's opinion is considered and group consensus is needed. This could lead to slow decision times. However, once the decision is made there is no more deliberation, and the process moves quickly. In this category, the US scored middle which implies elements of top-down decision making might be incorporated. The main difference is in top-down there is a discussion before a decision, more discussion occurs after the decision is made. The decisions are made fast and usually done so by the boss. However, the process is also more flexible. In a top-down decision-making culture, the decision might be made without hearing from all parties. Therefore in the US deals are made fast and the personal relationship does not matter. This is supported by our interview where J.G. said “relationships are very important (...) yes, but in the end, I need to also make ends meet.”

The fourth and final field is disagreements. In this category, the Netherlands scored confrontational meaning disagreements are considered positive for the organization. People engage openly as it will not affect the relationship negatively in business or personal life. The US however scored in the middle meaning elements of cultures that avoid confrontation can be found. Non-confrontational cultures tend to think of disagreements as negative or likely to disrupt harmony. They do not engage openly as it affects the relationship negatively both in business and personal life. Word choices can often be played down.

Looking at the context of a country is essential in understanding why locals talk and act the way they do. When looking at the US versus the Netherlands key differences are feedback, leadership and hierarchy, decision making, and disagreements. These elements together with the rest that were not mentioned work together to create the contextual identity of a local in the US.

If we look at the Pelican Grand and the Noble House through Schein's onion model we will find valuable information for a future intern. Through our interview with a past intern, we found the basic underlying assumptions of the hotel. When we look at the artifacts of the hotel a clear one is the pelican which appears in their logo. In the espoused values today on the Noble House website we see they value nature and refer to it as one of their shareholders. Through these two layers of the onion model, one would conclude that the hotel emphasizes the environment and nature. However, through our interview, we found that in 2019 the basic underlying assumptions differed. Iris mentioned that the company talked about caring for nature on the outside but internally had no rules in place for sustainability. This aspect of their basic underlying assumption will be further discussed in the sustainability section of this report.

We found information about the team diversity and the workforce in the Pelican Grand through our interview with a former intern. On an operational level, she said “Well at the front desk we had a lot of Americans … In housekeeping… a lot of ladies with dark skin color. In reservations, only Americans. In F&B were a lot of interns from all over the place, that was for sure the most diverse” (Iris Ooms, 2021). From this, we deducted that the workplace on an ethnic level in the operational sector was on the surface level fairly diverse. If we look at the management level in regards to the gender divide Iris also mentioned that “The front desk manager was a guy but the F&B manager was a woman, the reservation manager was also a woman” hence proving that on a managerial level gender biases were not present. 

Sustainability is a big topic amongst hotels these days and customers are more concerned about how their leisure activities are impacting our planet. Therefore, hotels are doing more to decrease the negative effects they have on the environment. They do this to follow trends or adapt to the needs of their customer segment. Pelican Grand is part of Noble House, ultimately, they are the ones making decisions in these matters. According to their website Noble House does concern itself with sustainability issues, they donate to organizations regularly. Most of these organizations are related to SDG 14, life below water. As they are the only beachfront hotel in Fort Lauderdale, they value the cleanliness of the beaches and waters not just around them but across the globe. Some other organizations they donate to include animal rehabilitation centers thus including SDG 15, life on land. Another organization they donate to is the Big table which supports hospitality sector workers who make below poverty level wages therefore to a certain extent supporting SDG 1, no poverty. The Noble House group also has a reward program in all their hotels in which you receive dining credits or stash reward points if you opt not to have your room serviced daily. Even though it may seem small, not having your room serviced daily minimizes the use of chemicals, decreases water usage, and lessens energy usage. From our research of sustainable development of this hotel, we believe they actively work towards social and environmental sustainability. However, for the triple bottom line, it would be important to work towards economic sustainability as well. While the Noble House group is working towards creating a sustainable company, they still have ways to go. Most of their products are imported which is detrimental to the environment. Although they have made improvements in the past couple of years, our interviewee who was an intern at the Pelican Grand in 2019 said “For sustainability, they were telling me that they were doing a lot to lower their impact. But in reality, they were not. An example of this is that there was a water tap in the lobby with a sign next to it that it was forbidden to bring your cup. They had plastic cups next to them. And it was for promotion.” (Ooms, 2021). Since then, the Noble House group has implemented the aforementioned reward program as well as the donations to various organizations. Through the improvement they have made between the years of 2019 and 2021 we concluded a positive transition in the Noble House and Pelican Grands attitude towards sustainability.

Human rights play a big role in sustainability as without them social sustainability is unachievable. Human rights reflect in multiple SDGs. For this aspect, we will focus on Florida rather than the hotel’s area. In Florida, there is a real issue with violence against transgender individuals with elevated levels of violence against people of color. Between 2016 and 2021 “88 percent of the transgender people killed in Florida were people of color” (Ryan Thoreson, 2021). As reported by Human Rights Watch in 2021 a record number of legislations were filed regarding anti-trans policies, some of which have passed into law. 

When referencing the macro-environment the hotel itself has little control over it. However, it is important to look at it for a multitude of reasons. It shows aspects of your business that must adapt or change. When looking at Pelican Grand we realize they have a competitive advantage in Fort Lauderdale as the only hotel with a private beach. Due to this the economic outlook of the hotel is good as no other hotels have this in the area. However, due to COVID, we cannot predict if after the pandemic the hotel will grow as they are reliant on the economy and policies of the States. When examining purchasing power, if the economy stabilizes, more people will visit this hotel. Exchange rates do not affect it as most of its guests are from the US hence these don't matter in this case. The competitive environment is very important for our hotel because it is hard for competitors to enter the market and near impossible to create another beachside hotel.

Angeline Green – 20/12/2021

1\. Did you have any stereotypes or prejudgments before moving to the US?

I guess I had some ideas based on what I saw on media, a lot of cars, a lot of fast food, I thought there would be a lot of advertisements and that the portion sizes would be really big.

2\. Were they confirmed or are they generalized?

To an extent definitely, the portion sizes are huge compared to what I’m used to and there are a lot of commercials on tv! The people are pretty nice like I thought too, but something I was wrong about was I thought people would be a lot more vocal, people tend to stay in their own lane.

3\. Do you think your place of work focused on creating a diverse environment?

I think they certainly tried for diversity in their own way, but I found that the approach was mainly numbers-focused. I think there should be more of a discussion about making sure everyone feels included, and that this is a better way of management than top-down policy

4\. How did you experience gender divide at your place of work or in your personal life?

I think a lot of the jobs are very gendered to begin with, for example housekeeping for women and chef for men, and I think these roles were perpetuated in the hiring of staff. That being said, when it came to handing out tasks there was very little gendered assigning, and when there was it was more on the level of the individual. Some I noticed outside the job was the power imbalance between married couples. In the Netherlands marriage is seen as a team, whereas in the US I often found wives walking on eggshells trying not to spoil their husband’s mood, or prioritizing his needs over their own consistently.

5.What do you think of the distance of power within a company that you worked at?

I think the power structure was pretty engrained in the company, while the managers were easy to reach, after that the higher in the ranks the less accessible they were. Luckily I never really needed to contact many superiors, but I always had the feeling that listening to workers was just seen as a burden or part of the job and the complaints weren’t taken to heart.

6\. Did you ever receive or give feedback to employees or employers? What is the difference in the US vs the Netherlands?

I have given feedback to my managers following comments from guests, I think the process is quite similar in the Netherlands but something I felt was quite different was that my superiors in the Netherlands really made an effort to seem like workers just like the rest of us. From day one they made it clear they’d take feedback to heart and that they had aspirations, hopes, concerned, and interests just like anyone else.

7.Generally speaking do you think the US is more individualistic than the Netherlands?

I think it is a bit more individualistic mostly as a result of neoliberalism. Of course, neoliberalism is present in the Netherlands and is more a product of the capitalist world economy than any one country, but as the poster child of capitalism I found I thought a lot of responsibility was placed on the individual and as a result the safety nets and support systems were harder to access.

8\. When someone invites you over for dinner in the US when do you arrive?

I like to arrive 5 minutes late- on time if I don’t know the person very well, I think its a bit more respectful to be a few minutes late than early just in case they’re still wrapping up the preparations. If I do know the person well I’ll push it to 10 minutes late but ideally 5 minutes max.

9\. Does this differ in case of a business meeting?

I like to arrive between 10 and 5 minutes early, leaves time to get situated and say some formalities before the meeting starts.

10\. When waiting in line how far do you stand from the other person?

Maybe a half meter?

11\. When communicating with other people do they use more/less/equal body language than the Netherlands?

I find myself using more body language in the US to punctuate and illustrate my stories, as I think more people there do this than in the Netherlands. I also think this is just because Americans tend to be louder than the Dutch so I feel the need to do this to keep their attention. I don’t think the Dutch are as emotive with their body language when they speak, and more punctuate with the tone/ intonation of their voice.
